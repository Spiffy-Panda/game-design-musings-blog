The Ledger of Correspondences

How the old and the to-be line up. The left column is history; the right column is the setting; the strip beneath each pair is the mechanic that carries it into play. The brief argues — this page keeps the receipts.

The Old — Latin Christendom, roughly 800–1300 (one flagged anachronism) The To-Be — the Reach, the settled Weave

Handles SF.n are stable and append-only — new rows take new numbers; nothing renumbers. Chips name the pillars from the brief's §1 (Lag · Specie · Tempo · Freight · Trust · Mortal). Constants (the 90-day fade, the 60-day frontier haul) live in the brief's §3 — cite them, don't fork them.

Crown & Distance — authority at range

SF.1

The auditors who arrived stale

Lag
The old

Charlemagne's missi dominici rode out in pairs to audit the counts. The counts fed them, flattered them, and outwaited them — the crown's questions and the province's answers both moved at horse speed.

The to-be

Company inspectors jump the circuit with quarterly books already six months stale. The resident governor smiles, hosts, and signs; by the time contradiction reaches the core, the fiscal year — and the evidence — has turned over twice.

In playNPC lords are principals, not puppets: they optimize locally against your last order, never your intent. Loyalty is a simulated quantity with a courier delay on every observation of it.

SF.2

News at hull speed

Lag
The old

No signal outran a rider. Kingdoms learned of invasions, deaths, and councils weeks late, from whoever happened to arrive first — and acted on rumor because rumor was the freshest thing available.

The to-be

The Weave carries hulls, not signals: no ansible, no beacon chain. Every fact crosses space as cargo in somebody's hold, aging en route, arriving with the carrier's interests attached.

In playFog of authority: every datum on every screen — prices, fleet positions, your own holdings — is stamped with its courier age. Nothing in the game is live except the system you are standing in.

SF.3

The court that travels

LagSpecie
The old

Kings ruled from the saddle: the court toured the royal estates eating its food renders in place (feorm), and justice rode circuit with the eyre. Moving the court was cheaper than moving the grain.

The to-be

A tithe hauled to the core loses a third to the fade before it docks. So the liege's Progress tours the fiefs instead, consuming tithe-chrism on site — and the visit is simultaneously audit, assize, and muster review.

In playThe Progress is an event chain: escort contracts, hosting costs, petitions heard at your own dock. Being skipped on the itinerary is a public signal everyone reprices.

Coin & Grant — money, and what replaces it

SF.4

Paid in rights

SpecieTrust
The old

Coin scarce, mints local, salaries impossible — so service was bought with tenure. The revocable benefice hardened into the heritable fief (Quierzy, 877, is the milestone historians point at), because land collects its own revenue and needs no paymaster.

The to-be

Core scrip fades in the bunker and redeems nowhere near the frontier. The only pay that survives the distance is a grant that collects itself: font shares, lane tolls, local justice — held heritably, owed back in tithes and a season's service.

In playThe contract engine prices wage against grant as a function of courier distance. Past a computable radius, grants win strictly — feudalism emerges as the optimizer's output, not a scripted era.

SF.5

The melting treasury

Specie
The old

Wealth that lasted was land, plate, and sworn men. There was no deep credit to park a fortune in — a lord's balance sheet was acreage and retainers, and war chests were literal chests, quickly empty.

The to-be

Chrism is income, never wealth: a reserve of size S decays unless fed by harvest flow to match. Scrip inherits the fade, so money itself carries demurrage — saving means keeps, improvements, and retinues, never a vault.

In playNo treasury-hoard victory exists. Power equals current harvest flow, so wars target flow — fonts, tolls, charters — and a rich lord who loses his font is poor in ninety days, on schedule.

SF.6

The manifest gamble

LagTrust
The old

Renders in kind, notched tally sticks, accounts audited whenever the lord's men finally came around. Between visits, the reeve's arithmetic was the only arithmetic.

The to-be

Tithe ledgers are sealed manifests, audited only when the Progress or its traveling exchequer actually docks. Between visits, the castellan's count is the count.

In playSkimming is a wager on the itinerary — steady small theft against the odds of an audit year. NPC officers run the same wager, which is why your own tithe reports are gently, chronically wrong.

Keep & Tempo — war at the speed of relief

SF.7

Stone answers speed

Tempo
The old

Longships beached, burned, and left before any royal host could form. The working answer was local and vertical: the castle and the mounted retainer — garrisoned response-time, paid for out of the ground it protected.

The to-be

Reaver tides hit font stations in days; naval relief needs weeks. The working answer is the keep on the lane mouth — emplaced guns, fresh local chrism, a garrison that was always going to be there.

In playRaid alarms resolve with local assets only; central aid arrives after the fact by design, always. The protection a lord sells is therefore real — which is where legitimacy comes from.

SF.8

Siege by half-life

TempoSpecie
The old

Few fortresses were stormed, and fewer starved than the ballads claim: most sieges broke on the attacker's clock — the forty days, the harvest, the pay chest, winter — or ended at a table, with terms priced by how long the besieger had been kept waiting.

The to-be

Interdicting the font kills a keep's reach, not its people: three half-lives and the garrison can't jump, but the loops inside run for a generation. It's the besieging leaguer that melts — its reserve re-bought every season from a font it doesn't hold. Two clocks, and both admirals can read both.

In playSiege gameplay is blockade gameplay: the keep's industrial decay (years) races the fleet's melting logistics (seasons), and most sieges end in terms or a lifted leaguer. The full doctrine — and the system map it stands on — is the siege page (SIEGE.1–6).

SF.9

The forty days

TempoTrust
The old

The knight's fee owed the host a customary forty days a year, self-equipped. Campaigns were sized to the obligation — and to the harvest that both armies had to be home for.

The to-be

A fee-holder owes the liege one calm season's squadron service a year, fueled from the vassal's own font. Wars are sized to the muster, and the storm calendar sends everyone home.

In playThe muster meter is public: arriving short, late, or stingy is visible to every house and priced into your credit, your marriages, and your next charter.

SF.10

The bought peace

TempoSpecie
The old

Danegeld: when the raiders price in faster than your musters, you pay them to leave. It worked, repeatedly — and taught the raiders exactly what returning was worth.

The to-be

Tribute schedules to reaver clans, negotiated like any other charter — cheaper than a garrison this year, and a standing invitation compounding against every year after.

In playProtection contracts with a ratchet: each payment re-prices the next demand. The break-even against fortifying is always computable and always getting worse — the player gets to decide which year the ledger flips.

SF.11

The march bargain

TempoMortal
The old

Marcher lords and palatine counties: frontier fiefs granted extra sovereignty — private war, own courts, sometimes own coin — as the price of absorbing the border violence the crown couldn't reach.

The to-be

March charters on the storm frontier carry rights no interior lord holds — private war, an own mint, first claim on salvage — because the tide breaks there first, and the metropole knows what it's buying.

In playThe march is the player's fastest on-ramp to lordship: the richest charter terms in the game, priced in exposure. Interior politics is slower, safer, and pettier — choose your ladder.

SF.26

The license to swarm

TempoTrust
The old

Licenses to crenellate: the crown claimed the right to say who might fortify, and unlicensed — adulterine — castles were razed in their hundreds when a strong king finally got the chance, because every private wall was borrowed sovereignty.

The to-be

Shell charters: the suzerain caps a vassal's home-swarm tonnage the way his forebears licensed battlements. An unchartered — adulterine — drone shell is borrowed sovereignty in autonomous form, and razing it is the first item on every strong liege's agenda.

In playShell tonnage is a chartered stat with a hard cap and a standing temptation: overbuild in secret and your deterrence improves while your legal position rots. The thread behind it: LOOM.1, the fleet that never jumps.

SF.27

The tournament of ghosts

TempoTrust
The old

The tournament: martial display as social technology — proving ground, marriage market, and a public audit of who actually keeps knights that can fight, wrapped in pageantry and ritualized violence.

The to-be

The muster of ghosts: seasonal live-fire reviews where a lord's drone shell performs before invited witnesses — part festival, part deterrence, and the only audit an autonomous arsenal ever gets. Rumor does the rest of the year's work.

In playReviews are public events with real stakes: impress, and your house's credit rises at every fair by courier; fumble, and your neighbors' calculus updates the same way. Skipping one reads as weakness or as something to hide — both invite visitors. The thread behind it: LOOM.2.

Cart & Manifest — trade under the freight wall

SF.12

The ox eats its cargo

Freight
The old

Overland, the team ate the load: grain rarely paid its way past a few dozen miles, so staples stayed home while silk, spice, plate, and relics crossed continents. Trade was thin, sharp, and luxury-shaped.

The to-be

Freight is priced in fading chrism per ton-jump — a melting currency taxing every mile. Ore and grain never jump; data, specialists, medicines, and treasure do. Each system feeds itself: the manor, rediscovered.

In playThe genre's bulk-hauling instinct meets a wall, and the wall explains itself: the tooltip on a money-losing ore run shows the fade arithmetic. Interstellar trade plays as a luxury game with luxury margins.

SF.13

Fairs and letters

FreightTrust
The old

Trade pooled at calendar points — the Champagne fairs — and ran on paper between them: bills of exchange, merchant houses, coalitions like the Hanse enforcing contracts by boycott where no court could.

The to-be

Each calm season, fairs convene at the nexus fonts; letters of credit ride with bonded couriers between them. Merchant houses enforce by blacklist — a name struck at one fair is struck at all of them, eventually.

In playFairs are periodic price-setting events; reputation is literal collateral. The arbitrage game is flying facts between fairs faster than consensus — the courier fortune, honestly earned or otherwise.

SF.14

The lord's mill

SpecieFreight
The old

The banalités: the lord's mill, oven, and press, which tenants were obliged to use, at the lord's fee. Infrastructure monopoly as quiet, reliable taxation.

The to-be

The lord's refinery, drydock, jump-beacon, and chart-cache — every service a transiting hull can't skip, each at a posted fee. The keep taxes by being the only plumbing in the system.

In playOne lever, three roles: as tenant you pay the fees, as lord you set them, as smuggler you evade them — and the evasion routes are the same lanes the reavers like.

Oath & Blood — trust, bonded in persons

SF.15

The key ceremony

TrustLag
The old

Homage was sworn skin to skin — hands between hands, then investiture. The bond was personal because only persons could be held to it; there was no institution underneath to sue.

The to-be

Investiture is a key ceremony: signing keys exchanged in person, because no channel exists to revoke at range. A traitor's codes stay valid until a hull physically carries the revocation — treason has a courier-days-long grace period.

In playKey custody is gameplay: who holds your codes, how fast you could revoke them, and what a defector can lawfully order in the window. The window is measured on the same map as everything else.

SF.16

Heirs in custody

TrustMortal
The old

Fosterage and hostageship, often the same arrangement: the vassal's heir raised at the liege's court — educated, connected, and collateral, all in one child.

The to-be

Heirs foster at the liege's station, and the succession keys foster with them. Custody of the child is custody of the house's continuity — schooling, surety, and escrow in a single berth.

In playFosterage slots trade legitimacy for leverage: your heir comes home educated and allied — or doesn't come home, which is the point. Refusing the invitation is itself a declaration.

SF.17

The wedded treaty

TrustMortal
The old

Marriage was the durable treaty technology: dowered, witnessed, productive of heirs whose very existence rewired the map's claims a generation out.

The to-be

Unchanged — the Weave has no better instrument. Dowries run in font shares; claims propagate down the marriage web on the same courier lag as everything else.

In playThe alliance web is the dynasty layer's circuit board. Claims travel it, slowly; a wedding at the frontier reprices politics in the core four months later, when the news docks.

SF.18

The chart-keepers

TrustLag
The old

The Church was the estate that crossed every border: the literate class, the calendar, the archives, sanctuary — and excommunication, the sanction that needed no army.

The to-be

The Ephemeris keeps the storm-charts every transit needs, carries the courier rite, holds neutral ground, and brokers the Truce of the Calm while fair convoys ride. Its sanction is interdict: your charts go stale, and the Weave closes without a shot.

In playStanding with the order gates navigation quality — forecast freshness, lane tolerance, storm insurance. Interdict is the late-game political weapon that fires no shot and wins anyway.

SF.19

The bound gangs

TrustFreight
The old

Serfdom followed a grim algebra (Domar's): land abundant, labor scarce, coercion available — so elites bound the scarce factor to the soil rather than bid for it.

The to-be

Bloom-harvest is crewed, brutal, and far from anywhere; passage home is priced in chrism the gangs never own. Company scrip and passage-debt bind labor to the font as surely as any hide of land.

In playThe bound-labor slider is the setting's darkness knob — company town at one end, outright bondage at the other — and it should be a visible, honest choice. Abolition exists as late-game politics, not a checkbox.

Crown & Clock — offices, deaths, successions

SF.20

The mortal office

MortalLag
The old

Offices were persons; when the person died, the office convulsed. Inheritance was the only continuity technology, and succession disputes were how the system relieved pressure — violently, as a rule.

The to-be

No bureaucracy survives its holder at four months' round trip. Rights attach to bloodlines because blood is the one record that certifies itself on site, with no courier required.

In playCharacters age and die mid-campaign. Every death within reach is a political event with a courier delay attached — the succession is often decided before half the claimants have heard.

SF.21

The company becomes the crown

LagSpecie
The old

(The flagged anachronism.) The East India Company: a trading charter that, left unaudited at distance long enough, acquired armies, courts, and revenue — and had to be nationalized to be stopped. The rhyme is too exact to leave out.

The to-be

Settlement opens corporate: chartered companies front the fuel rush. When the audit fails and the pay crisis bites, the company man on the spot becomes the lord — same office, older name.

In playThe default campaign opening: you fly for the Company, and the mid-game is watching it feudalize around you — org chart to family tree in three generations of play.

The Dissolution — four gunpowder moments, pick one

SF.22

Shield-money

Specie
The old

Scutage: as coin returned, knights paid the crown instead of serving, and the crown hired professionals who didn't go home at forty days. The wage quietly dissolved the fee.

The to-be

Stabilized chrism ends the fade. Navies stand year-round, service is bought out in convertible fuel, and the muster season becomes a tax season within a generation.

In playDissolution tech I. The moment reserves can be banked, power decouples from the fonts — and every fief's founding logic goes quietly obsolete.

SF.23

The wire returns

Lag
The old

Roads rebuilt, posts regularized, print spreading: the crown could finally see its provinces faster than they could dissemble. The auditor state returned, and with it the salaried official.

The to-be

Lane-lasers thread signal down the Weave at last. The ansible ends the Lag; the core reads frontier books in hours, and the plenary governor shrinks back into an employee.

In playDissolution tech II. Fog of authority lifts region by region as relays light — and every institution built on the fog gets renegotiated, rarely politely.

SF.24

The drone artillery

Tempo
The old

Artillery cracked the castle: the fixed defense that had anchored local autonomy for centuries became rubble on a schedule, and with it went the castellan's bargaining position.

The to-be

Decoherence-hardened cores survive transit. Drone fleets jump, arrive without crews to feed or fuel-fear to leash them, and the keep's tempo advantage dies like the curtain wall did.

In playDissolution tech III. Fortress economics invert campaign-wide; every lane-mouth keep becomes a target list, and the marches feel it first.

SF.25

The common coin

SpecieTrust
The old

Royal money, royal courts, royal debt: once one coin cleared everywhere and one court enforced strangers' contracts, the personal bond stopped being load-bearing — and became ceremony.

The to-be

Imperial clearing rails make marks convertible across the Weave. One money, one market, one crown — and homage decays into heraldry within living memory.

In playDissolution tech IV. Mature exactly one of SF.22–25 per campaign: each picks a different early-modern door for the setting to exit through, and the endgame is the fight over which.
