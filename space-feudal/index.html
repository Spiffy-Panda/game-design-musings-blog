A game-design musing · the cockpit is rented · the economy is the game

Space Feudal

The flight model is the genre's — X-series stations, Elite docking, No Man's Sky wander. The design work is the layer above it: rebuild the economic problems feudalism actually solved, stipulate nothing else, and let the lords condense back out of the simulation. Dynasty weather above; a cockpit economy below.

§0 · The wager

Feudalism is an equilibrium, not a coat of paint

Feudalism in games is usually upholstery — titles, banners, a vassal screen bolted onto an economy that would run identically without it. The wager of this musing is that feudalism is better understood as an equilibrium: the cheapest set of contracts a civilization can still run when a specific bundle of things is broken — when information crawls, money fails at distance, defense must be local, bulk goods can't travel, strangers can't be sued, and every office dies with its holder.

So the design is: break exactly those things, on purpose, with invented physics — then hand every NPC house a contract engine and self-interest, and let the pyramid re-condense on its own. Hereditary lords, homage, castles at the crossings, serfs at the harvest, an itinerant king. If it doesn't condense, the simulation is wrong in an interesting way. If it does, the player lives inside the reasons for feudalism instead of its wallpaper.

Don't paint fealty on. Make fealty the cheapest contract that survives distance.

A caveat owed to the historians: "feudalism" is a bucket term, and medievalists from Elizabeth A. R. Brown to Susan Reynolds have made a sport of kicking the bucket. Fair. We're designers — we get to use the bucket, because we're reproducing the constraint bundle, not defending a textbook diagram.

§1 · The old problems

Six things were broken. Each writes one line of the spec

For each pillar: what happened, the economics underneath, and the requirement it hands the design. The six requirements are the spec — everything invented in §2 exists to make them come true, and nothing invented is allowed to repair one.

Pillar I · The Lag

Authority decayed with distance

Charlemagne sent paired inspectors — the missi dominici — to audit his counts. The counts outwaited them, because every order and every report moved at horse speed; a round trip of supervision took months, and the counts lived there.

The principal–agent problem, with monitoring costs no institution could pay down.

SpecDistance must tax information more than mass.

Pillar II · The Specie Famine

Money failed at distance

The coin economy contracted; kings could not pay salaries or standing armies in money that held value far from the mint. What a king could grant that stayed valuable was tenure — land and rights over it. The benefice hardened into the hereditary fief (the milestone historians point at is Quierzy, 877).

Fiscal capacity failure: wages replaced by self-collecting property rights.

SpecThe only payment that survives distance is a right, not a remittance.

Pillar III · The Tempo Gap

Raids were faster than armies

Viking, Magyar, and Saracen raids resolved in days; royal hosts mustered in months. Security had to be produced locally — the castle and the mounted retainer were a response-time technology (the "castellan revolution" around 1000, if you buy Duby; the debate doesn't move the design point).

Public goods fail when response latency exceeds threat tempo.

SpecRaids must resolve faster than any central navy can answer.

Pillar IV · The Freight Wall

Bulk could not travel

An ox team eats its own cargo: grain couldn't profitably move more than a few dozen miles overland, so staples never traveled — only silk, spice, plate, and relics did. Every manor fed itself, and long-distance trade stayed thin, sharp, and luxury-shaped.

Transport cost sets a value-density floor on trade; below it, autarky.

SpecBulk stays local. Only treasure crosses.

Pillar V · The Trust Horizon

No court bound strangers

Nobody could enforce a contract two duchies away. So politics and commerce ran on personal bonds — homage sworn skin to skin, heirs fostered as hostages, marriages as treaties, merchant coalitions enforcing by collective boycott (the Champagne fairs, the Hanse, Greif's Maghribi traders).

Third-party enforcement replaced by repeated-game reputation and collateralized kinship.

SpecContracts must be self-enforcing, or bonded in persons.

Pillar VI · The Mortal Office

Every office died with its holder

With no bureaucracy to outlive a man, every office was a person, and continuity had exactly one technology: inheritance. Succession crises were the system's earthquakes, and everyone planned around them.

Institutional persistence, solved by dynasty.

SpecRights attach to bloodlines. Death is a game event.

§2 · The kit

Five inventions, each earning its keep

FTL exists — that's the brief's one stipulated constraint. Everything else is chosen. The rule of the kit: every invention must earn at least one pillar, and none may repair one.

The Weave — lanes, not an ocean

FTL runs only along fixed lanes between neighboring stars: the map is a graph, and every border is a short list of doors. Lanes carry hulls, not signals — nothing massless propagates down one, so there is no ansible, no beacon chain, no interstellar radio. News travels as cargo.

Buys:LagTempo — chokepoints

Chrism — the jump medium, and it fades

Jumps burn chrism, an unguent skimmed at font stars — rare, scattered, mediocre real estate otherwise. It cannot be synthesized, and it fades: half-life around ninety days, and every stabilization attempt curdles it. Chrism is income, never wealth — holding a war reserve of size S demands harvest flow proportional to S, so nobody banks a navy. And the fonts are why people spread: the core's appetite for chrism is bottomless, the fonts sit in marginal systems, and the fuel rush is this age's gold rush — charters do the rest.

Buys:SpecieFreightTempo — range leashthe dispersal itself

The Bloom — the harvest needs hands

Font harvest zones sit inside the bloom, an electromagnetic hell that wrecks autonomous systems in hours. Harvesting chrism is crewed work — gangs, tenders, dredge-rigs — labor-hungry, dangerous, and far from home.

Buys:dispersal with teeththe manor's labor politics

Transit decoherence — the one licensed miracle

A lane transit scrambles running computation above roughly an insect's worth. Recorded data survives cold; minds survive warm — why is the setting's one unapologetically magical clause. So no drone fleets between stars, no AI couriers, no remote audit; every jump needs a living lodesman at the board, and information means people. In-system, automate whatever you like — the cockpit sim underneath keeps its toys.

Buys:LagTrust — presence is verification

Weather — the Weave has seasons

Storm tides close lanes for weeks on a loose annual rhythm, forecastable only with fresh charts. War and trade get a calendar; the chart-keepers get an estate (the ledger calls them the Ephemeris).

Buys:campaign seasonsfairsa clerisy

Marks — derived, not invented: local money

With no signal faster than a hull there is no interstellar clearing, so every currency is a warehouse receipt against a particular lord's chrism and stores. Scrip inherits the fade — money itself carries demurrage, hoarding cash is punished, and "savings" means acreage, keeps, and retainers. A frontier mark is wallpaper in the core, because redemption is local. Long trade runs on barter, bullion-goods, and letters of credit carried by bonded couriers. This is Pillar II, rebuilt from parts.

§3 · The worked example

The tyranny of the fade

One decay constant does a remarkable amount of medieval work. Fix the numbers — these are the setting's constants; future pages cite them rather than fork them:

chrism half-life t½ = 90 days  ·  lane transit = 3–6 days  ·  core ↔ frontier ≈ 10 lanes ≈ 60 days with layovers

tithe hauled 60 d → 2^(−60/90) ≈ 63% arrives. A 37% haircut, before politics. So the court goes to the tithe and eats it in place — the Progress: itinerant kingship, re-derived.
order + report loop ≈ 120 d. Every decision stands on facts four months old. So the man on the spot must be a principal, not a puppet — plenary governors, re-derived.
a punitive fleet arrives at ~63% bunkers, no harvest rights; the rebel keep sits on its font at 100%. So defense dominates at the lane mouth — the castle, re-derived.

One knob, three institutions. When a single invention keeps re-deriving history unprompted, it's load-bearing — keep it, and tune everything else around it.

§4 · The ladder

How it condenses — company to crown to fief

No step below is scripted. Each is the contract engine picking the cheapest instrument that still works one ring further out.

  1. Charter companies

    driver: capital

    The metropole grants font monopolies to companies — somebody has to front the fuel rush. The age opens corporate, not feudal.

  2. The audit fails

    driver: the Lag

    Quarterly books arrive six months stale and self-dealt; inspectors get the missi treatment. The metropole replaces boards with resident governors-plenipotentiary — a person, on site, holding everything.

  3. The reavers come

    driver: the Tempo Gap

    Raids on the fonts resolve in days; naval relief takes weeks. Governors raise local squadrons and must fund them locally — tolls, harvest shares, tax farming. The governor is now, functionally, the state.

  4. The pay crisis

    driver: Specie + Trust

    Squadron loyalty can't be bought with core scrip that fades in the bunker and redeems nowhere nearby. The metropole concedes the only coin that works at range: hereditary grant — font shares, lane tolls, local justice — against tithes-in-place and a season's squadron service every calm. The forty days reborn. The fief.

  5. It fractalizes

    driver: all of them, recursively

    A margrave holding six systems faces the same lag and tempo inside the march, and re-grants moons and keeps on the same terms. Subinfeudation; the pyramid.

  6. It locks

    driver: Trust + the Mortal Office

    Chart-tithes to the Ephemeris, fosterage, marriage webs, the Progress eating its way around the realm — the arrangement becomes self-stabilizing, and the metropole wakes up a suzerain rather than a sovereign.

§5 · At the stick

What the pilot feels

The genre loop survives intact — fly, dock, haul, fight, build. The layer above leans on it in a few specific places:

  • Your range is a leash. The chrism in your bunkers is melting on a schedule; the frontier is measured in half-lives, not light-years.
  • Every price has an age. Market screens timestamp every fact by courier age — and a lord's own empire screen is just as stale, system by system. Fog of authority as UI. Flying fresh facts between fairs is a legitimate fortune (ask the Fuggers).
  • Muster season. Hold a knight's fee and one calm season a year your squadron belongs to the liege, fueled at your expense. Arriving short or late is public, and priced into your house's credit.
  • Sieges are blockades — of functions, not larders. Cutting the font kills a keep's reach on the fade curve; the keep itself, loops closed, can live a generation. A siege is two clocks — the garrison's slowly decaying industry against the besieging fleet's melting bunkers — and most end at a table. Running the blockade with fresh chrism and fab-grade spares is still the relief mission. The long version, with the system map: the siege page.
  • The Progress docks at your station. The court consumes your tithe in place — inspection, assize, and muster in one visit. Hosting is expensive. Being skipped is worse.
  • The ladder is climbable. Freelancer → bonded courier → knight's fee → castellan → charter lord → margrave. Every rung is a contract, and the same engine that runs NPC houses runs yours.

§6 · Knobs & the dissolution

Tuning the dark age — and ending it on purpose

KnobSet too lowSet too high
Fade half-life No interstellar anything — city-state ponds that never touch. The core stockpiles a standing navy; empire wins, no lords condense.
Lane density Isolated clusters; politics never percolates. No chokepoints — keeps are pointless, raiders roam free, mass beats position.
Bloom severity Automated harvests; no manor, no labor politics, thin settlement. Harvest costs exceed yield; the fonts never draw a population out.
Storm season length War never pauses; no campaign rhythm, no fairs, exhaustion politics. The realm freezes solid most of the year; nothing can be projected at all.

Slowboats exist, for honesty's sake — sub-light colony arks that matter over centuries, not campaigns. They're where the setting's strangest cultures come from, and no fleet ever waited on one.

And the age should be able to end. Feudalism dissolved when its constraints reversed — money returned, roads and posts and print restored the audit, artillery cracked the castle. Each pillar gets a gunpowder moment; each is a late-game technology or policy, and the endgame politics is the fight over them:

  • Stabilized chrism ends the fade — navies stand, service is bought out. Scutage: the wage returns.
  • Lane-lasers thread signal down the Weave — the ansible ends the Lag, and the auditors come back.
  • Hardened cores survive transit — drone fleets jump, and the keep's tempo advantage dies like the curtain wall did.
  • Imperial clearing makes marks convertible — one money, one market, one crown.

Mature one per campaign. All four at once skips the story; one at a time picks which early-modern door the setting exits through.
