The Long Patience

Why a keep that can feed itself for a generation still opens its gates; what a blockade actually buys; and the map the whole argument stands on. This page supersedes the one-line siege in the brief — it was compressed to the point of being wrong, and the objection that caught it is where we start.

§1 · The objection, sustained

Jump fuel is not life support

The objection Chrism is a jump medium. Interdict a keep's font and it can't leave the system — but a station with near-future closed loops grows its own food, recycles its own water and air, and with a large enough population lives comfortably for generations. So why would it ever fall?

Sustained, in full. A well-found keep under blockade does not starve — not in ninety days, not in ninety months. The fade curve kills something real, but it isn't the people: cutting the font kills the keep's reach. Bunkers at 2−t/90: two half-lives and the garrison's squadron has a quarter of its legs; three, and the keep cannot even flee. It is still armed, fed, powered, and populous — it has simply stopped existing interstellar-ly. Everything else this page does follows from taking that seriously.

And here is the pleasant surprise: taking it seriously makes the analogy better, because the medieval record says the same thing. Storming was rare and ruinous. Starving out a provisioned castle usually took longer than the besieger had — the feudal host owed its forty days, the harvest called, winter came, the pay chest emptied. Most sieges broke on the attacker's clock or ended at a table; the years-long strangulations were the exceptions, and they were royal, professional, and ruinously expensive. The castle's larder was long; the army's patience was short. So it is here.

A siege is not a race to the keep's last meal. It is two clocks — the garrison's slowly decaying industry against the leaguer's rapidly melting logistics — and everyone can read both.

§2 · The ground

Harrow — a worked font system

Siegecraft is geometry before it is anything else, so here is the geometry. One deliberate lie up front: the map is not to scale — to scale, nothing is readable. The distances and transit times printed on it are the true numbers; the picture is arranged for the eye. The map lies about space so it can tell the truth about time.

What the map teaches

  • In-system war is chess by mail. Reaction drives announce themselves: every burn is visible system-wide, so a fleet's arrival is known weeks before it happens. Surprise exists in exactly one place — emergence at a mouth. Ambush lives in the Shallows; everything else is a slow, mutually-legible board.
  • Nobody can cover both gates. Hearthkeep to Kingsgate is twenty-five days hard burn; gate to gate is forty. The lord fortifies the coreward mouth, buys watch services on the far one, and grants the Gatehouse to a castellan — the geometry forces subinfeudation, and every seam it creates is something a siege can pry.
  • The leaguer has a natural campsite. Millstone: volatiles, deuterium, and reaction mass three weeks from the keep but only three from the picket line. Every serious siege of Harrow is really a contest for Millstone — the counter-castle, re-derived.

§3 · The keep

Three loops, one leak

What is a keep made of, honestly, at near-future tech? Three loops — two of which close, and one of which cannot. The one that leaks is the siege.

Loop I · Life — closed

Air, water, food

Hydroponics, recycling, aquaculture decks: energy in, calories out, indefinitely. The objection is right and the doctrine concedes it in full — a blockaded keep does not starve. (Its outposts do: a 2,000-soul station has stores, not loops. That is why the small stations yield first.)

Loop II · Power — local

The star, the ice

A deep keep drinks its star: at 0.25 AU, collector acreage is power. Fusion feedstock comes off ice runs a blockade can harass but rarely sever. Power is not the lever either — a besieger who counts on darkening the keep is planning someone else's siege.

Loop III · Industry — open

The pyramid leak

Thirty thousand people cannot host the whole industrial pyramid. The keep prints parts, machines simple stock, patches and cannibalizes — but fab-grade chips, precision optics, catalysts, medical isotopes, biologics all arrive by convoy. Under blockade the top of the pyramid erodes. Slowly. In years. That is the real clock, and it is long.

Mouths are munitions. The counterintuitive corollary: population is fortification. Every additional trade aboard covers another branch of the maintenance tree, so the bigger the keep, the longer its industrial clock — a burg of two hundred thousand decays slower than a keep of thirty, and an outpost of two thousand barely decays at all, it just runs out. Lords pack their stations for the same reason their forebears walled their towns.

§4 · The leaguer

The fleet is the thing that melts

Now the other clock, the fast one. A blockading force — the leaguer — must hold, at all times, enough fresh chrism to jump home and enough to fight whatever emerges at the mouth. Call that its reserve. The reserve fades at the same 2−t/90 as everything else, and unlike the keep, the leaguer is sitting on no font: every gram of decay is re-bought from home and convoyed in through the very mouths it is guarding. One 120-day season on station re-buys roughly sixty percent of the reserve. That is the meter running.

Stack the rest: crews on feudal muster owe one calm season — every season past the first is paid tonnage, which means scutage pressure on the besieger's own vassals. Warship loops are tighter than station loops, so consumables ride the same convoys. The storm season closes the lanes on schedule, and the leaguer must either winter over — cut off, at full burn, praying the charts were fresh — or lift and re-establish next calm, handing the keep a resupply window every year. And the natural campsite, Millstone, must be taken and held first, because a leaguer without local volatiles is a leaguer on a countdown.

The keep's counterplay writes itself off the map: sortie against the skimming rigs (announced for a month by physics, so it is really a feint game), cut out pickets during storm onset when the mouth wanders, and above all make the convoy war expensive — every escort the besieger adds is reserve it must also feed.

§5 · The two clocks

The leaguer of Harrow — a worked siege

reserve R = 1,000 t fresh  ·  season = 120 d  ·  re-buy = R×(1−2^(−120/90)) ≈ 0.60·R  ·  muster covers season one only

leaguer: 600 t re-bought per season + convoy escorts + a wintered-over picket ≈ 1,400 t-equivalent, year one — rising, paid, political. The besieger's curve is steep and denominated in his own vassals' patience.
Hearthkeep: reach dead by day 270 (three half-lives); pharmacy floor at ~month 30; hull-risk years away. The defender's curve is shallow and denominated in morale.
window: besieger's projected burn to the keep's next milestone exceeds the value of the keep's functions ≈ month 7–9. So the table opens. Composition: five years' tithe redirected, gate tolls shared, the second son fostered at Wardenhall, the garrison keeps its colors. Nobody ever fired on the keep itself.

Two features of that ledger deserve names. First, everyone can compute it — both curves are public arithmetic, which is why sieges here, like their medieval ancestors, are mostly negotiations conducted in logistics. Second, terms decay: the customary law of the siege prices surrender by the clock — yield at the summons and keep your tolls; yield at the pharmacy floor and keep your colors; be stormed, and custom stops protecting you. Every season of defiance is a bet that relief comes before the terms rot.

§6 · The endings

Six ways a siege resolves

Handles SIEGE.n are stable; cite them. Rough base rates attached, because the distribution is the doctrine.

SIEGE.1

Composition — terms at the table

the plurality

The modal fall. Tithes redirected, tolls shared, hostages fostered, fealty re-sworn — the keep survives intact because the keep is the prize; rubble pays no tithe. Terms are a decaying function of resistance time under the customary law of the siege, which is what makes the decision to hold out a real wager rather than a reflex.

SIEGE.2

The masking — defang and move on

the professional's default

Interdict the font for three half-lives and the keep's squadron is legless: it can hold its rock forever and threaten nothing. Leave a watch squadron at the mouth, write the keep out of the campaign, and spend the fleet somewhere it buys more. The cheap win — most "sieges" in a war of movement are this, and the keep's chronicles will still call it a siege withstood.

SIEGE.3

The long rot — strangulation in years

kingly, rare

Hold the cordon through storm seasons and pay the melt, for years, until the pharmacy floor or the maintenance wall moves the keep's council. Only worth it for a gate that commands an irreplaceable mouth, and only possible for a purse that can pay professionals long past any muster — which is to say: kings, and the rare margrave who is one in all but name. The medieval analogues were the wonders of their age precisely because almost nobody could afford them.

SIEGE.4

The storm — assault at a breach window

rare, ruinous, remembered

Possible only when something has already failed — defenses degraded by the rot, treachery at a lock, or tonnage so overwhelming the exchange is survivable. You inherit wreckage, a decimated assault echelon, and a population that hates you, minus the customary protections it forfeited by making you storm it. Doctrine prices it honestly and then recommends against; epics get written anyway.

SIEGE.5

The turning — politics does the work

commoner than pride admits

The fostered heir signs, a faction opens a dock, the Ephemeris lays interdict and the keep's remaining charts go stale, the old lord dies mid-siege and the succession needs the besieger's blessing more than it needs the walls. Every seam the geometry forced — the castellan at the far gate, the bought watch, the passage-debt gangs — is a place the siege can be won without a shot. The counterintelligence war is siege warfare.

SIEGE.6

The lifting — the defender wins

the modal outcome, where the web holds

The liege's relief muster emerges at the mouth, or simply doesn't need to: the storm season breaks the leaguer's budget, the convoy war bleeds it, the besieger's vassals go home. Most sieges of a solvent lord's keep end this way — as most always did — and the whole feudal web exists to make sure the relief clock beats the terms clock. Fealty, at bottom, is siege insurance.

§7 · At the stick, under siege

The siege as a campaign of missions

For the pilot, a siege is not a cutscene — it is the densest mission economy in the game, on either side of the cordon:

  • Picket watch. Weeks of boredom at the mouth's drift ring, then ninety seconds of emergence contact that decide whether the convoy war is won this season.
  • The last convoy out. The cordon is forming, the tithe and the heir both need to be elsewhere, and every hour of loading is a hull on the scope getting closer.
  • Blockade running. Fresh chrism and fab-grade crates in, timed to storm onset when the mouth wanders off its charts and the pickets hold station blind. The relief mission from the brief, now with its full price tag visible.
  • The rig raid. Twenty-eight days of announced approach to Millstone means the raid is really a feint game — three burns telegraphed, one meant.
  • Parley escort. Ferrying the terms shuttle under truce marks while both fleets watch, and deciding whether what you overheard in the hold is worth more than your bond.
  • Leaguer life. On the other side of the cordon: skim duty at Millstone, convoy escort, counting your own bunkers fade — and learning exactly why your liege wants this over before the second calm.
