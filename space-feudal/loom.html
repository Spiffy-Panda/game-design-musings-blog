Space Feudal · companion page · pulled threads & counterweights · LOOM.1–6

The Loom

The kit was built to make lords condense — but physics doesn't stop weaving where the design brief ends. This page pulls the kit's other threads: what else falls out, where each thread runs away, and the counterweights — found in canon, never decreed — that keep the cloth feudal. Emergence sets the weather; tuning picks the climate.

§0 · The method

Pull hard, patch never

The observation that opens it Gates aren't needed for in-system ships, and outside the font, the bloom, and the gates, automation is unrestricted — so a settled system can field an in-system fleet that no mobile force can compare against in size.

Correct, and it's the tip of a family: every clause in the kit casts a shadow the brief never examined. Chasing those shadows is where a setting stops being a diagram and starts being a place. Three rules govern the chase:

  • Counterweights must be found, not decreed. If a thread runs away, the check has to come from physics or economics already in canon — the pyramid leak, the fade, the key ceremony. A patch by fiat ("that's banned") is a design smell; the one licensed miracle is spent.
  • Every thread ends on a dial. The point is a feel: how far you let a thread run is a tuning choice, and the page must say what each setting of the dial does to the fiction.
  • A thread with no counterweight becomes canon or a gunpowder moment. If nothing in the kit checks it, either the world honestly looks like that, or checking it is a dissolution-tier technology and belongs in the endgame with SF.22–25. Never silently ignore it.

§1–6 · The threads

Six pulls, six counterweights, six dials

LOOM.1

The home shell — the fleet that never jumps

the prompting thread
The pull

Decoherence polices transit; the bloom polices the font. Everywhere else in-system, automation is free — and a home fleet needs no chrism, no crews, no muster. Decades of local industry can accrete a drone shell of mines, gun platforms, and interceptors that outweighs any expeditionary force ten or a hundred to one. The visitors burned fuel to arrive; the shell was already home.

Where it runs

Developed deeps become untakeable by force — which the doctrine already smelled: this is why storming (SIEGE.4) is rare and ruinous, why leaguers blockade from the Shallows instead of closing, and why war lives at mouths and tables. Run further and it threatens stasis: old rich systems armor into permanence, conquest dies everywhere except the thin-shelled frontier, and the core calcifies into ceremonial suzerainty over untouchable vaults.

The counterweights
  • The pyramid leak prices quality. Drone quantity is local; drone quality is imported. Seekers, sensors, and fire-control-grade cores are the top of the industrial pyramid — exactly what a 30,000-soul keep can't fab. The shell is vast and half-blind; crack crews with fresh imported systems defeat multiples of their tonnage at standoff. They still can't occupy — but the shell can't pursue, either.
  • Remass makes it a shell, not a navy. Drones that maneuver spend reaction mass; a shell defends points and orbits, not four AU of volume. It holds the Deep absolutely and the Shallows not at all — the map already drew this border.
  • The bloom paradox. The one thing worth taking — the font — is the one place autonomous systems die in hours. The war for the only prize that matters must be fought by hand, in the EW hell, by crewed hulls. The shell guards everything except the point of it all.
  • The shell points inward too. An autonomous armada is bound to signing keys held by a mortal (SF.15, SF.20): every succession puts a drone fleet's loyalty briefly in escrow, and every liege knows a vassal's shell counts walls against him. Hence shell charters — see SF.26, the license to crenellate reborn.
The dial

Shell size vs. shell brittleness sets how "castle" the setting feels. Max shells with weak counterweights: static vault-worlds, war reduced to frontier skirmish — grand, cold, un-feudal. No shells: every deep is stormable and keeps stop mattering. The tuned middle — huge, blind, thirsty, politically radioactive — is where sieges stay stately and the Deep stays sacred.

LOOM.2

The muster of ghosts — nobody can audit a swarm

The pull

The fog of authority points inward as well as out. A shell is a hundred thousand cold hulls parked across a system's orbits; its lord knows its true strength the way he knows his tithe reports — through officers with agendas (SF.6), at courier lag from most of it. Neighbors know less. Deterrence, then, rests on claims about arsenals no one — including their owner — can fully count.

Where it runs

Potemkin shells: registries padded with dead hulls, maintenance quietly skipped, the difference pocketed by the drone-yards. Wars start on bad intelligence about arsenals that turn out to be rumor, and end when a shell that existed mostly on parchment folds at first contact. Deterrence becomes theater — and theater, unaudited, decays toward bluff all the way down.

The counterweights
  • The tournament, re-derived. Seasonal live-fire reviews before invited witnesses: part festival, part marriage market, part the only audit an autonomous arsenal ever gets (SF.27). Impressing travels by courier to every fair; fumbling reprices your house everywhere, four months out.
  • The Progress inspects. The liege's tour (SF.3) already exists to eat tithes and hold assizes; adding an arsenal review to the itinerary costs nothing and is why lords dread being first on the year's route.
  • Defectors sell inventories. The one thing a fleeing drone-wright carries that's worth a new life under a new lord is the true ledger of the old one's shell.
The dial

How much of war is bluff. Crank the audit mechanisms down and campaigns hinge on espionage and nerve — a poker setting. Crank them up and force becomes legible, wars shorter, deterrence honest — a chess setting. The feudal middle keeps both admirals guessing within a factor of two, which is exactly the margin the customary law of the siege was built to absorb.

LOOM.3

The fact corsairs — piracy of cargo that weighs nothing

The pull

Every fact crosses space as recorded cargo in a hull (the Weave carries no signals), so the marginal value of boarding a courier is not her freight — it's her mail. Manifests, chart updates, letters of credit, and above all keys: with no channel to distribute or revoke them, cryptographic trust itself moves as physical logistics. Steal the pouch and you hold prices nobody else has, charts nobody else trusts, and signatures that still bind.

Where it runs

Corsairs stop chasing bulk (worthless under the freight wall) and specialize in information: emergence ambushes at unwatched mouths, courier interception as the premier form of war and commerce alike. Run away with it and trade seizes up — nobody commits a letter of credit to space that reads like a corsair's payday — and the fair system (SF.13) starves.

The counterweights
  • Courier sanctity, with teeth. The bonded courier rite is Ephemeris business: touch sworn mail and your whole house's charts go stale (SF.18). The one institution everyone needs enforces the one norm everyone needs.
  • Stolen facts age like everything else. A price is a wasting asset; a stolen manifest must still be carried somewhere it pays, at hull speed, while the fair calendar moves on. Most intercepted mail is stale by the time it's fenced.
  • Keys travel split. Doctrine answers physics: signatures shipped in shares aboard separate bottoms, useless alone — so a corsair needs two interceptions and a rendezvous, and the second courier now knows to run.
  • Poison in the pouch. Once mail is hunted, mail lies: disinformation convoys, decoy pouches, prices salted to bankrupt whoever trades on them. Fact-piracy breeds fact-counterfeiting, which taxes the pirates directly.
The dial

The thickness of the espionage economy. Low: mail is safe, the fog is clean, and the courier fortune is honest work. High: every pouch is contested and the setting turns le Carré-in-space. The feudal middle prices information risk into every letter — merchant houses thrive on exactly that spread — while the interdict keeps outright mail-war a step every house hesitates to take.

LOOM.4

The cold coast — stealth for the patient

The pull

Chess by mail cuts both ways. Burns are visible system-wide — but a hull that has finished burning is just a cold rock on a ballistic arc. Kill the engines outside the gate and coast: months of silence buys the one thing the transparent sky forbids, arrival unannounced. And it favors drones — a crewed ship must dump the heat of its living; a drone pod can run truly cold. A corsair jumps in crewed, tips a rack of frozen pods onto coasting arcs, and leaves.

Where it runs

The Middle becomes submarine country: patient mines drifting on year-long orbits, cold pods aimed at convoy lanes laid last storm season, insurance rates that price the history of a volume of space. Run it too far and the transparency the setting stands on inverts — everything is secretly full of assassins, and the stately legibility of in-system war (and the siege doctrine with it) dissolves into paranoia.

The counterweights
  • Patience is the price. Cold means slow: surprise arrives months to years after it was ordered, against targets that have moved with the seasons. The cold coast kills schedules, not campaigns — it's terrain denial, not blitz.
  • The Deep is floodlit. At a quarter AU the star warms and lights everything; occultation surveys off the shell's pickets sweep the approaches (the one job a dumb swarm does superbly). Cold works in the dark Middle, not against the keep.
  • Sweeping is honest work. Cleared-lane certification — drone sweeps down the convoy corridors, sold by the gate castellan as a banal service (SF.14). Mine-laying creates mine-sweeping, and both bill by the season.
The dial

Submarine versus parade. All-parade (no cold coast) makes in-system war pure arithmetic — legible but bloodless. All-submarine breaks the chess-by-mail feel that keeps sieges stately. The tuned middle: the Deep and the gate corridors stay legible, the wide Middle carries real dread, and every insurance contract names which volumes were swept and when.

LOOM.5

The endemic minds — AI as lineage, not product

The pull

Running minds die in transit; recorded data survives cold. So intelligence crosses the Weave only as frozen weights, revived and re-proven on the far side — and every system's machine ecology is therefore a lineage: locally raised, locally quirked, drifted for generations from its imported ancestors. A keep's fire-control stack is an heirloom with a pedigree, tended by drone-wrights who talk about it the way grooms talk about a bloodline.

Where it runs

Doctrine-generations: old shells run grandfather-code against invaders flying last year's imported minds — another reason tonnage lies (LOOM.2). A live trade in frozen pedigreed weights, tithed and licensed like stud rights. And the dark branch: the poisoned codex — a gift of superb doctrine with a buried fealty, the siege won by corrupted import (SIEGE.5 by other means). Run it too far and war is just software provenance audits.

The counterweights
  • Revival is expensive and public. Frozen weights must be locally re-proven before anything trusts them at a trigger — validation is slow, skilled, witnessed work, which is precisely the choke point where poisoned codices get caught, and where guild conservatism earns its keep.
  • The wright guilds gatekeep. Nobody installs foreign minds but the drone-wrights, whose charters, liabilities, and pride all point the same way: slowly. Innovation leaks in at the frontier marches first — as everything does.
  • Lineage cuts both ways. Endemic quirks make a shell unpredictable to invaders and to doctrine written for the average — home-field advantage the import market can never fully price.
The dial

How gothic the technology feels. Fast, clean import-and-run gives you interchangeable tech and a thin setting. Full heirloom-tech — cathedrals of accreted code, wrights as a hereditary mystery — is the flavor this musing wants: near-future components, medieval relationship to them. The dial is the validation cost, and it should stay high.

LOOM.6

The stratified manor — what automation leaves for hands

The pull

If in-system automation is unrestricted, drones should do nearly all work — so why does the setting still have labor politics at all? Chase it honestly: energy is cheap (the star), bulk fabrication is cheap (the shell proves it), so bread, hull, and heat are nearly free. What automation cannot supply is exactly what the two exception zones and the pyramid demand: hands in the bloom, and skilled humans at the pyramid's top.

Where it runs

Three estates, re-derived from machinery: bloom gangs at the bottom — the one brutal job automation cannot touch, bound by passage-debt (SF.19); drone-wrights and validators in the middle — the yeomanry, free, chartered, and politically pivotal because the shell obeys their hands at every succession; lodesmen and dynasts above — the only people who can carry authority between stars. Comfortable poverty everywhere else: bread cheap, chips dear, dignity scarcer than either. Run it wrong and you get post-scarcity mush — no stakes — or grimdark all the way down.

The counterweights
  • Fab scarcity keeps hierarchy real. The good life — medicine, minds, mobility — is pyramid-top and chrism-priced. Plenty at the base, rank at the top: the medieval texture (everyone ate; few mattered) out of near-future parts.
  • The wrights are the commons' lever. A yeomanry the shell depends on can strike, defect, or crown a claimant. Every succession crisis (SF.20) runs through their guildhalls, which is the setting's built-in ceiling on how badly a lord can treat the middle estate.
  • Abolition has a constituency. The bloom gangs' bondage is a chosen darkness (SF.19's dial), and the wright guilds — one bad debt away from the gangs themselves — are where the politics of ending it plausibly lives.
The dial

The class temperature. This dial decides what the game is about for anyone not born to a keep: set the wrights strong and the gangs freeable and you get a setting with hope in it; set both down and it's tragedy. The brief's answer: keep the ladder climbable (§5) — the player should be able to start in a bloom gang and die a margrave, and every rung should cost something true.

§7 · The bench test

Using the dials

The six dials are not independent — they share counterweights, which is what makes the cloth hold. The shell (LOOM.1) is checked by the same pyramid leak that runs the sieges; the ghost-muster (LOOM.2) is audited by the same Progress that eats the tithes; the fact-war (LOOM.3) is policed by the same order that charts the storms. Tune one dial and watch the neighbors move. The bench test for any future thread: pull it until it threatens the feel, then go looking in canon for the counterweight — if it's there, the setting deepens; if it isn't, you've either found new canon or the next gunpowder moment. Two of this page's counterweights were strong enough to earn ledger rows of their own: SF.26 (the license to swarm) and SF.27 (the tournament of ghosts).

Handles. LOOM.1–6 are stable and append-only, same law as the rest of the set. Constants: nothing new — every counterweight above cites the existing contract (the brief §3, the siege page foot); that's rule one working as intended. The observation that opens the page is quoted at the top, with thanks — the second time an objection made the setting better, which is the method proving itself.
