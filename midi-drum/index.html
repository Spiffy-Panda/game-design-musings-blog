A musing + a playable coach · self-contained, no build

[M]IDI [D]rum [C]oach

Plug an electronic kit into the browser and get told what to play next. The interesting design object isn't the notation or the sounds — it's the suggestion engine, treated as a game director that shows its reasoning.

The bet

Rhythm games spend most of their budget faking an instrument — plastic guitars, quantized lanes, charts that must be authored song by song — and the player's ceiling is the chart's ceiling. An electronic drum kit inverts both halves for free. The instrument is real, and it already speaks in clean events: a note number per pad, a velocity, a millisecond timestamp. Web MIDI hands that stream to a webpage. Nobody plugging in wants a chart; they want to get better. What's left to design is exactly the part games are good at: the loop that decides what you should try next.

That decider is a game director — dynamic difficulty adjustment wearing a practical hat. Directors earn trust by being legible: the coach's director is five rules you can read on the page, and every suggestion arrives with the rule that fired, spoken plainly ("92% twice in a row at 96 BPM — same family, one level up"). And it is overridable: the full pattern library sits right under the suggestions, filters and all. A director you can audit and ignore is advice; one you can't is a difficulty slider with delusions.

Everything else is presentation, borrowed from machines drummers already read. Grooves render as step grids, not staves. Hits are judged in fixed millisecond windows, and the score that matters is not the percentage but the signed bias — whether you rush or drag — because that's the one number a human can act on next loop.

Five claims

  1. MDC.1The kit is already the controller.

    E-drums emit note-mapped, velocity-tagged, timestamped events — the hard input problem rhythm games spend peripherals on is solved before the page loads. Design effort belongs downstream of the instrument, not in imitating one.

  2. MDC.2Suggestion is a director problem — and directors should be legible.

    Picking the next pattern from observed play is dynamic difficulty adjustment. The coach's director is five visible rules, each suggestion cites the rule that fired, and the library remains one click away. Auditable, overridable.

  3. MDC.3Notation is interface.

    The 16-step machine grid says "play this" at a glance where a stave says "decode this first." Meter lives in the layout: a shuffle isn't a new lesson, it's a 12-cell grid — subdivision taught by the shape of the page.

  4. MDC.4Judge the gap, not the hit.

    Windows in milliseconds (±30 perfect, ±70 good, ±120 off), misses and strays tallied — but the coachable number is the signed mean: rushing or dragging, and by how much. Accuracy scores the past; bias instructs the next bar.

  5. MDC.5Practice is already a core loop.

    Count-in → loop → judge → suggest is a game loop with the fantasy of getting better for real. Progression is pattern families — new textures, not just faster tempos — so "level up" means the ghosted 'e', the open-hat off-beat, the leaving of the hats for the toms.

The pages

I · The tool

The Coach — plug in and play →

Connect a kit over Web MIDI (or use the keyboard and on-screen pads — no hardware required), monitor your hits, audition thirteen grooves as step grids with a synthesized kit, then practice: count-in, millisecond judging, rush/drag bias, and a director that suggests the next groove and says why.
II · candidate

The Fill Director

Suggest one-bar fills as transitions between grooves and judge the re-entry downbeat — the moment fills exist to protect. The director learns which exits you can land.

The coach runs entirely in the page — hits are processed locally and never leave the tab. Web MIDI works in Chromium browsers and Firefox; Safari hasn't shipped it, so there the keyboard/click pads carry the demo (the grids and sounds work everywhere).
