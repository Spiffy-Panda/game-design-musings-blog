LoMa · a magic-system pitch · one page, self-contained

[Lo]gical [Ma]gic

The world is a model. A spell is a sentence the world does not yet satisfy. Casting is proving you may say it — and paying to make it so.

The wager of the system: every classic magical effect — fire, wishes, wards, scrying, true names — falls out of taking mathematical logic literally, priced in two honest integers. No field equations. The physics of LoMa is metamathematics.

§ 1 · The Premise

Casting is proving

Take the logician's picture of reality at face value. The world is a first-order structure W: a domain of things, and an interpretation that says, of every atomic claim — Aflame(wick), Mine(coin), Crosses(taxman) — whether it holds. The totality of these atomic facts is called the Record. Underneath the Record sits a short, protected list of axioms nothing may contradict — the Bedrock (things persist unless acted on; no thing is its own part; the dead stay dead; a handful more). The Bedrock is why magic has no perpetual engines and no paradox bombs.

A spell — the academies say plea — is a sentence φ in a formal language, the Tongue, that the world does not currently satisfy. Casting it is a four-step transaction:

Strokes — the caster's ledger

One stroke = one inference-rule application you must actually perform, spoken or inscribed. A drilled war-caster sustains ≈4 strokes/second aloud. Strokes measure how hard the spell is to say lawfully — so the whole skill game is proof brevity: lemmas, notation, preparation. A grimoire is a lemma cache. A scroll is a warrant you paid for last night.

Flips & grace — the world's ledger

One flip = one atomic fact of the Record changed at settlement. Flips draw on grace, a slow local budget (a quiet vale accrues ~10,000 flips of grace a day). Spend a region dry and it goes stiff — lawful pleas simply fail to settle until the tide refills. Grace is why magic has logistics, sieges, and sacred ground.

The Miser's Law

Among all ways of making your sentence true, the world settles on one that flips the fewest atoms — and among those, it owes you no kindness in the choice. Every monkey's-paw story is this one law. Underspecify and the cheapest model is rarely the one you meant; the fix is not piety but more clauses, each with a stroke price. §3, Plea 02 does the arithmetic.

Notice what is not here: joules. LoMa's sibling system, Thaumodynamics (MDYN), buys its groundedness with field equations — flux, couplings, conservation. LoMa refuses the physics on purpose. The effects arrive by fiat, as magic should; what is conserved, counted, and audited is the logic: the length of your proof and the size of your miracle. You do not pay for how much you burn. You pay for how hard it is to lawfully say “all of it.”

The novice asks the world for fire and is refused. The magistrate asks the world to agree that, all things weighed, the barn is already burning — and is granted costs. — Corvane, Reader in Warrants, Vell College

§ 2 · The Ladder

Power is quantifier depth

A plea's reach is read straight off its logical form. Naming one thing is a cantrip. ∃ hands the world a blank to fill; ∀ makes a promise over everything in scope. Stack them in alternation and each layer multiplies what the plea can bind — and what it costs to warrant. The academies number their circles by exactly this: circle n = n quantifier alternations. It is not a metaphor. It is the arithmetical hierarchy, worn as a curriculum.

0quantifier-free

Atomic flips on things you can already name. Light the candle, latch the door, warm this cup. One stroke, one flip, no surprises — the domestic tier. “The wick, alight.” — 1 stroke, 1 flip

1Σ₁ · ∃

Conjury and wishes: there is an x such that… Cheap to warrant — and the world chooses the witness, under the Miser's Law, with no kindness owed. Every clause you add to pin the witness costs strokes. Safety is purchasable; it is never free. “Let there be a light before me.” — and it is the foe's lantern

1Π₁ · ∀

Wards, blessings, edicts: for all x in scope… The warrant must cover the whole domain — by brute instantiation (pay per thing) or by induction, if the domain has a constructor you can cite. The entire economics of large magic lives in that “if.” “No thing crosses this line.” — priced per thing, or per proof

2Σ₂ · ∃∀

Summoning with a job description: there is a guardian that answers every blow. The inner ∀ makes the outer witness hard to fake — and hard to warrant. This is the first circle where pleas can supervise other pleas. “Let there stand one whom nothing passes.”

3+Σ₃, Π₃, …

Treaty-grade magic: for every curse there is a counter no curse defeats. Casts measured in careers; warrants the length of books. Above every finite circle, the diagonal — and the door to the second order (§5). the work of colleges, not casters

Game semantics — why duels are fair

Evaluating a sentence is a two-seat game: ∃-moves are the caster's to play, ∀-moves belong to the falsifier. Unopposed, the world plays falsifier lazily. But another mage may buy the seat and play your ∀'s viciously: you plead “every blade turns aside” and the duelist, as falsifier, names the one blade your step-proof forgot. A counterspell is not a dispel — it is cross-examination. A duel is two pleas running at once, each mage seated as the other's ∀. Initiative is the outermost quantifier.

§ 3 · Worked Pleas

Three spells, audited to the stroke

The MDYN discipline, kept: every headline claim gets its arithmetic shown. Read each plea as an auditor reads a filing — the sentence, the warrant and its price, the settlement and its price, and the ways it goes wrong.

PLEA 01

Emberfield

Π₁ · circle onegrace-heavythe fireball
the sentence φember ≡ ∀x ( In(x, Ball(c, 6 m)) → Aflame(x) )

The ask

Everything within six meters of the anchor point c, burning, now. A classic. Note the form: a universal over a spatial domain. Nothing in the sentence says how much burns — LoMa never charges for joules. It charges for the ∀.

The domain choice

Before casting you must scope: carve the ball into named cells. Fine grain (1 dm cells) → 904,779 things in scope. Coarse grain (1 m cells) → 905 things — but ~450 of those straddle the rim, and a straddling cell flips whole. Coarse fire has jagged edges. Ask the singed.

The warrant, three ways

Brute: instantiate the ∀, one stroke per cell — honest and hopeless at fine grain. Prepared: brute, but inscribed last night onto a scroll; combat-viable, single-use. Induction: cite the ball's constructor — it is generated from c by six unit-breaths — and prove base + step. Thirteen strokes, any grain.

Failure modes

Unstructured scope — a crowd has no constructor; no induction, brute only (or spend strokes imposing an order first, then induct over it). Opposed falsifier — the foe plays your ∀ and names the rain-soaked cell your step-proof skipped; the plea collapses mid-cast, strokes forfeit. Grace drought — see the table's right column.

The strategic reading

Induction fixes your ledger, never the world's: the warrant shrinks 70,000-fold, the flips don't move. So the fine Emberfield is a strategic weapon — one clean sphere of fire that leaves the field stiff for a season, a scorched-earth doctrine with a proof obligation. Treaties name it. Colleges refuse to teach the step-lemma at fine grain. This is the tradeoff MDYN prices in kelvin and LoMa prices in cardinality.

You do not pay for the fire. You pay for the word all. — drillmasters' proverb, the Emberfield form

PLEA 02

The Long Wish

Σ₁ · circle onethe monkey's paw, audited
the naive sentence — 2 strokes, and worth every penny φwish ≡ ∃x ( Gold(x) ∧ Mine(x) )

The ask

Gold, and mine. The warrant is almost free — two strokes; consistency with the Bedrock is obvious. The danger is never the warrant. It is the settlement: the world must merely find some witness, and by the Miser's Law it finds the cheapest.

The Miser's answers

Enumerate the minimal settlements before you plead — this is what the Casebook drills. Own a wedding band already? The plea is already satisfied: zero flips, nothing happens, strokes wasted. Otherwise one flip suffices, and there are several one-flip models. None is the one you meant.

Lawyering the witness

Each rider strikes a family of cheap models, at a stroke price: r1 “never before held by me” (+3) · r2 “no part of the pleader” (+3) · r3 “within my purse” (+4) · r4 “a coin” (+2) · r5 “no prior claim on its matter” (+5).

Failure modes

The rider you didn't write. The Casebook's standing warning: a wish is a contract you draft against an adversary with perfect knowledge of your omissions and no malice at all — malice would at least be predictable. The Miser is merely cheap.

The audit

Naive wish: 2 strokes, 1 flip, and a gilded tooth. Airtight wish: 19 strokes (2 + 3 + 3 + 4 + 2 + 5), forcing the ≈40-flip creation — an object's whole atomic diagram, minted against the local tide. The aphorism the numbers prove: a wish is exactly as safe as it is long. And the Penny Gilding survives as honest commerce: six strokes, one flip, gold for the price of a penny — the loophole kept, priced, and taxed.

Entry the ninth: pleader wished for “a horse that cannot lose.” Settlement: a horse that cannot race. One flip. The Casebook does not record what was said. — the Brindle Casebook, annotated edition

PLEA 03

The Quarter-Day Breach

wards as theoriesex falso quodlibet8 strokes, 0 grace

What a ward is

A standing theory bound to an anchor-stone: a finite writ of axioms the region enforces by refusing settlements that violate them. The anchor is a proof-checker, not a judge — it admits any entry petition it can derive from its own writ plus the day's attested facts. Therein the trade.

The writ of Bellhall

W1 none cross uninvited · W2 no invited one bears iron · W3 the lord's amendment: the tax-collector crosses each quarter-day. And one fact of the realm, attested beyond the ward's power to refuse: the collector's badge of office — the king's seal is iron by statute.

The breach

On quarter-day the writ is inconsistent, and a proof-checker holding ⊥ will countersign anything — from falsehood, everything follows. The sapper waits for the bell, presents the eight-line derivation, and is admitted by the ward itself, politely. Eight strokes, zero grace: the breacher flips nothing. The ward does.

The defense

Premium anchors are cut paraconsistent — “a stone that can hold two truths without going mad.” Explosion fails inside them; the contradiction is quarantined to the taxman's file and the gate stays shut. Four times the price at inscription. Cheaper defense: hire a lawyer to read your amendments before the king does.

The breacher's menu — every dungeon door, honestly keyed

Three lawful families of breach, in ascending elegance: (1) predicate lawyering — satisfy the writ's definitions de jure (its “Invited” is whatever its inscription says; definitions are attack surface); (2) the unmeant model — enter through an interpretation the inscriber never fixed (the old well predates the survey the writ quantifies over — see §6, Löwenheim–Skolem); (3) explosion — find the quarter-day. Every locked door in a LoMa dungeon is one of these three, and the key is always a derivation the player can actually write.

The stone did not fail. The stone did exactly as instructed, which is the oldest failure there is. — survey of the Bellhall incident, marshals' appendix

§ 4 · Vessels — the advanced practice

Rituals are monads

A settlement is an instant. Real work — healing, transmutation, anything worth calling a ritual — is a sequence of steps, any of which can fail, under state that must be threaded and residue that must go somewhere. Naive casters chain pleas raw, and raw chains break in the middle: the half-splinted arm, the lead half-goldened. The advanced practice wraps every step in a vessel — a context that carries the effect — and composes steps by pouring one vessel into the next. To put a plain value into a vessel is to bottle it. A rite is a chain of pourings; the pour-sign is written ▷ (the archives also keep the old form, >>=).

rite Mend(limb) ≔ in the Cracked Vessel:
    wound ← survey limb        — may find nothing · crack
    set   ← splint wound       — may slip · crack
    knit  ← graft set          — may take ill · crack
    bottle (Whole limb)        — only now does a settlement file

— contrast, from the accident registry: the same three steps pled raw,
  no vessel. The splint settled; the graft cracked. The arm kept the splint
  and the open wound, three days, until a chirurgeon-logician re-pled it.

Why the world honors this: a vessel is only a vessel if its pourings obey the Three Duties. They are not etiquette. A rite whose steps break them is rejected at the warrant stage — the duties are what make “the same rite” mean the same thing twice.

First Duty · bottle, then pour

Bottling a thing and pouring it into a rite is just the rite. Consecration adds nothing to action.

bottle x ▷ f  ≡  f x
Second Duty · pour into a bottle

A rite that ends by bottling what it was given is the vessel it started with. No free blessings.

v ▷ bottle  ≡  v
Third Duty · pourings regroup

How you bracket a chain of pourings cannot matter — only their order may. Grouping is free; order is fate.

(v ▷ f) ▷ g  ≡  v ▷ (λx. f x ▷ g)

The named vessels of the standard practice:

The Cracked Vessel Maybe / Either

Carries “or nothing, and here is why.” Fizzle-safe casting; the crack rail above. The reason ritual magic is transactional and accidents are a choice.

The Vessel of Ash Writer

Every pour appends a line of residue to an ineffaceable log. Forensic thaumaturgy is reading ash. “Silent casting” — pouring over the trivial monoid — is detectable only by its absence, and outlawed in every jurisdiction that can spell it.

The Vessel of the Ledger State

Threads a hidden working-state through the steps — the enchanter's vessel, for rites that must remember what they did three pours ago.

The Vessel of the Locale Reader

Reads the ambient writ — ley, sanctity, jurisdiction — without changing it. The same rite poured in a different locale is lawfully a different rite. Why ground can be sacred.

The Vessel of Many List

Divination. A scry answers with every settlement the Miser could afford, as one unindexed manifold. Prophecy is a list you cannot subscript — the seers' entire guild exists to argue about ordering functions.

The Sealed Vessel World / IO

The outermost vessel, in which every rite ultimately runs. No one holds its run — you only ever get ▷. This is the in-world statement that nobody, at any circle, stands outside the world to invoke it.

Teaser · the Bottled Remainder

Masters of the continuation bottle the rest of the day itself as a value: break the bottle and the day resumes from the pouring — the contingency spell, the time-loop, the “if I fall” clause, all one construction (call/cc). The Guild licenses two such vessels a century, and the pitch keeps them off the table: a full Remainder treatment is its own page.

The Second Duty is the one students resent — no free blessings — and the one coroners cite. — chirurgeon-logicians' handbook, preface

§ 5 · The Mint — the second order

Enchantment quantifies over predicates

Everything so far binds things. The second order binds predicates — and that one step up is the whole difference between casting and enchanting. A first-order plea flips facts; when it settles, it is spent. An enchantment charters a new predicate into the world's signature, by licensed comprehension, and binds it to a bearer. The word persists. That is why the sword stays sharp after the wizard dies: the Record now contains a word for it.

a charter, as struck at the mint — “Keen,” bound to one blade ∃K ∀x ( K(x) ↔ ∀y ( Touches(x, y) → Cut(y) ) )  with  K ↤ the blade

The price of a word

The Mint's fee is the defining formula's length — eleven strokes for Keen above — plus a standing tithe: one grace a day, drawn from the bearer's locale. A charter is a greatest fixed point: it holds exactly as long as it is fed. Stop the tithe and the word fades from the Record — which is what “the enchantment wore off” has always meant.

True names are categoricity

To command a thing outright you must pin it uniquely — a description only it satisfies. For a finite soul that is merely expensive: a person is ~10⁶ atomic facts, so a true name is a lifetime's transcription (hence heirloom name-scrolls, and why stealing one is capital). For an infinite thing — a river, the sky — no first-order description pins it at all: every infinite writ has unmeant models (§6). Pinning the infinite takes a second-order axiom. That is the entire, rigorous reason hedge-wizards cannot command rivers and archmagi can.

The Antinomy — why the Mint has laws

Comprehension is a loaded instrument. The canonical catastrophe, taught on the first day: the Sundering of Ward-upon-Wards — a fortress charter struck to ward all and only wards that ward not themselves, then asked, by a visiting examiner, whether it warded itself. The answer has been burning since. Guild law now admits only predicative charters: a new word may speak only of words already struck, never of the totality that would include itself. The examiner's name is not taught.

First order moves the world. Second order teaches it vocabulary. Mind which you are doing. — the Mint-Warden's oath, second line

§ 6 · The Limits

Where MDYN has conservation laws, LoMa has theorems

These six are the system's hard physics. They are real mathematics wearing in-world names, which buys something no house rule can: they are not patchable. No errata, no divine intervention, no clever player breaks them — the same way no clever player breaks arithmetic. Design against them and the ceiling of the magic system is load-bearing.

Gödel · incompleteness
The Silent Truths

Any Tongue that cannot lie leaves truths it cannot speak. There are miracles with no warrant at any price — true sentences the world would happily settle, that no proof reaches. The mystics' whole vocation is circling them.

Tarski · undefinability
No Mirror

The Tongue cannot define truth-in-the-world within itself. Scries show facts, never Truth entire; no plea means “show me everything that is so.” Omniscience isn't forbidden. It is inexpressible.

Löb · provability
No Bootstraps

A plea that cites its own reliability buys nothing new: “trust this spell” only warrants what was already warranted. Faith-casting, confidence tricks, and the entire genre of believe-hard-enough magic — closed, by one modal theorem.

Rice · undecidability
No Assay

No rite can read what an arbitrary charter does except by running it. Every nontrivial question about a strange word's behavior is undecidable — which is why cursed rings pass inspection, and why the Mint keeps provenance papers instead.

compactness
The Patient Siege

If every finite day of the siege is consistent with the ward's writ, the siege entire is. A finite writ cannot forbid a limit it never names — wards fall to patience, one innocent day at a time. Siegecraft is applied compactness.

Löwenheim–Skolem
The Unmeant

Every writ with an infinite reading has readings you did not mean — and things live there. The fae are creatures of nonstandard models: they satisfy the letter of every treaty, in an interpretation no signatory fixed. You cannot out-draft them. The theorem says so.

We do not fear the mage who breaks the law. We fear the one who reads it to the end. — Treaty of the Unmeant, preamble

§ 7 · Why It Games

The design payoffs

Seven claims, stated as testable design bets. (Handles are stable — cite them as LOMA.1–LOMA.7 in later pages and reviews.)

LOMA.1
Costs are countable

Strokes and flips are literal integers; every spell in every future page can be audited by arithmetic, MDYN-style, without a physics engine. The pitch's three pleas are the proof of concept.

LOMA.2
Skill is proof brevity

Progression is not a bigger mana pool — it is shorter proofs: lemmas learned, grimoires-as-caches looted, warrants prepared. Vancian slots fall out for free (a prepared spell is a pre-paid warrant, consumed at settlement). Finding a lemma page literally makes your fireball cheaper.

LOMA.3
The monkey's paw is a theorem

Underspecified ∃ + the Miser's Law = adversarial witness, mechanically. Wish safety is bought clause by clause at listed stroke prices — the oldest trope in fantasy, converted into a pricing table the player can actually optimize.

LOMA.4
Duels are evaluation games

Game semantics gives combat for free: ∃-moves yours, ∀-moves the falsifier's, and an enemy mage may buy the falsifier's seat. Counterspelling is cross-examination, initiative is the outer quantifier — a duel system with a soundness theorem under it.

LOMA.5
Wards are theories; breaches are proofs

Every locked door keys to one of three lawful breach families (lawyering, unmeant models, explosion) — so dungeon design becomes writ design, and the key to a door is a derivation the player can literally write out. Puzzle content with an answer checker.

LOMA.6
Rituals are transactions

Vessels make multi-step magic atomic — crack and nothing files. The Three Duties are validity conditions, residue (the Vessel of Ash) powers forensic gameplay, and magical accidents become a choice the fiction can price rather than a GM's whim.

LOMA.7
The endgame is the metatheory

The power ladder tops out in real mathematics — second-order charters, true names as categoricity, the Bottled Remainder — under six theorem-hard ceilings (§6) that no content patch removes. Endgame power stays interesting because its limits are honest.
