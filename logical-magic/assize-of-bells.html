The Assize of Bells

Crown against Corb Fen, sapper, in the matter of the Bellhall ward — heard the quarter-day after the famous breach, before Justice Ambry; the Crown by Serjeant Marrow; the accused by Advocate Quill. A trial is conducted as all trials here are conducted: as a duel of derivations, each party seated as the falsifier in the other's pleas, the Clerks counting strokes from the gallery.

The question before the court is not whether the accused entered Bellhall. He entered by the front gate, bowing. The question is whether a man can trespass through a door that the house itself, by valid derivation, opened for him. — Justice Ambry, opening the record

Ten slides: the matter, the seating, five exchanges, the verdict, and the ledger. Arrow keys or the buttons below. The arithmetic in the margins is the Clerks'; the opinions are the gallery's.

The Matter · one year ago, at the quarter-day bell

A ward with three axioms and one bad day

The court takes notice of the facts, which no party disputes. Bellhall's anchor-stone enforced a writ of three lines: none cross uninvited (W1); no invited one bears iron (W2); and the lord's amendment, the tax-collector crosses each quarter-day (W3). The king's seal being iron by statute, the writ contradicted itself four mornings a year — and a proof-checker holding falsehood will countersign anything.

On the quarter-day in question, the accused presented the now-celebrated eight-line derivation at the gate. The stone verified it — it is valid; no one has ever said otherwise — concluded Invited(fen), and drew the bolt. Fen crossed, bowed to the household, inspected the silver unhurried, and left with none of it. He was, he has said since, making a point. The Crown replies that points are made in pamphlets.

Eight strokes, no grace. The cheapest entry in the history of burglary, and possibly not burglary at all — which is why we are all here. — the Clerks' preface to the trial bundle

The Seating · how a trial is a duel

Every plea seats two players; the court seats them fairly

Outside these walls, the falsifier's seat is taken by force — a duelist buys into your ward's game and plays your for-alls like a cross-examiner. In here the seating is the whole architecture. The Crown pleads a sentence; the defense is entitled to its ∀-moves. Then they trade. The Record itself sits in the witness stand: neither party may assert an atomic fact — they may only cite one, and the Clerks fetch it.

Serjeant Marrow is a careful, unhurried pleader with a reputation for short warrants. Advocate Quill has never in nine years pled a sentence longer than necessary, and is said to regard the Miser's Law less as physics than as a colleague.

A court is a duel in which the world holds both seats and lends them out. — motto of the Clerks of the Ledger

Exchange the First · the countersigned guest

The Crown pleads trespass; the defense produces the house's signature

Marrow opens with the shortest warrant the charge allows: Fen crossed Bellhall's boundary; Fen was not invited; a crossing uninvited is trespass. Five strokes, spoken at an unhurried four a second, each one countersigned by the Clerks as it lands. It is a good plea. For most defendants it would be the whole trial.

Quill rises for the falsifier's move on ¬Invited(fen), and does not argue. She cites. Exhibit A goes to the Bench: the anchor-stone's own verification record, eight lines, concluding — in the stone's neat lithographic hand — Invited(fen). The house examined the accused's derivation, found it valid, and drew its own bolt.

My lord, I do not claim my client was welcome. I claim he was invited, and I have the house's signature to it. If the Crown quarrels with the invitation, its quarrel is with the stone. — Advocate Quill, exchange the first

Exchange the Second · the Rule of Sound Warrant

Marrow reads line seven aloud, twice

The Serjeant's rebuttal is three strokes and a raised eyebrow. Exhibit A is valid — he concedes it cheerfully, the way one concedes that a forged note is beautifully engraved. But the court's own standing rule bars any derivation that passes through falsehood: ex falso, everything; therefore, before this Bench, nothing. Line seven of Exhibit A is ⊥, naked as the bell. A conclusion bought at line eight with the coin of contradiction is not evidence of invitation. It is evidence that the stone can be made to say anything — which, Marrow observes, is rather the Crown's point.

Ambry sustains without retiring. The stone's countersign is struck from the record as proof of invitation, admitted only as proof of mechanism — the how of the entry, not its lawfulness.

The stone believed him invited the way a man at the bottom of a well believes himself at the center of the earth: validly, from where he sits. — Serjeant Marrow, exchange the second

Bellhall's was not. The law is, as usual, the better-engineered stone.

Exchange the Third · no writ, no wall

Quill accepts the Rule — and hands it back edge-first

The defense does not appeal the ruling. Quill adopts it, with a courtesy that makes the Serjeant sit very still. If derivations through ⊥ establish nothing, she argues, then consider what Bellhall's writ was on quarter-day morning: a theory that proves ⊥ from its own three axioms and one statute. By the Crown's freshly sharpened rule, such a writ establishes nothing — no invitations, and no prohibitions either. There was no lawful boundary in force at Bellhall that morning. And trespass, she reminds the Bench, is a crossing against a boundary in force. No writ, no wall.

Marrow objects that the manor's stones and mortar were plainly in force. Quill agrees the mortar was innocent; her client is not charged with trespassing against mortar. The Bench takes the point under advisement, which from Ambry is a form of applause.

The Crown cannot have it halved and whole: if the stone's ⊥ voids my client's invitation, it voids the wall he was invited through. — Advocate Quill, exchange the third

Exchange the Fourth · the old well

The Crown pleads on the Bedrock — and the falsifier names the well

Marrow abandons the stone entirely, as a good pleader abandons a lost square. He pleads trespass at common law, beneath any writ: there exists a standing intent of the household, declared in the survey of the manor, such that every entry against it is trespass. A second-circle sentence — his ∃ to name the intent, then Quill's ∀ to test it against every entry. He names the survey of Bellhall, sealed and archived, eleven strokes of careful pleading. It covers the gate, the walls, the windows, the cellar-door, the chimneys. It is a very good survey.

Quill, in the falsifier's seat, is entitled to name one entry. She names the old well in the orchard — dry since before the survey was drawn, deeded with the orchard, surveyed never. The court sends a Clerk. The Clerk returns with cobwebs on his cuffs and a nod. The survey is silent on the well; Marrow's step-proof cannot close over it; the plea collapses mid-cast, strokes forfeit. It is the first true silence the Serjeant has produced all day.

Every writ with an unread corner has a door in it. My client merely reads to the end. — Advocate Quill, exchange the fourth

The accused did not enter by the well. That was never the point. The survey had to answer for it anyway.

Exchange the Fifth · the shape of the hole

The Bench subpoenas the ash

With the trespass charge bleeding out on the floor, Justice Ambry turns, unprompted, to the second count — the one the gallery had forgotten and the Clerks had not. The court's prerogative: a subpoena of the Vessel of Ash for the accused's residue across the week before the breach. Every pour a mage makes appends a line to the ash, ineffaceable, dull as tide-tables. The Clerks unroll Fen's week: brewing, mending, a prepared warrant inscribed on the Tuesday — and then, the night before quarter-day, nothing. Not an empty stretch. A gap: the log's continuity broken and rejoined around a hole with edges, exactly eight strokes long.

Somebody had rehearsed the breach — poured a reconnaissance rite through the ward's writ to find its bad morning — and had done it in the trivial monoid, where the ash takes no imprint. Silent casting: lawful mathematics, and a crime in every jurisdiction that can spell it. Quill, for the first time, has no move; there is no falsifier's seat at a subpoena. Fen, from the dock, is heard to say the eight strokes were "tidy work," which his advocate does not adopt.

You cannot erase the shape of an absence. The ash remembers even what it was not permitted to see — it remembers the length of not seeing it. — the Clerks of the Ledger, reading the residue

The Verdict · read at the evening bell

Acquitted through the door; convicted through the hole

Charge I — trespass

acquitted

On quarter-day morning Bellhall's writ proved ⊥ and therefore, before this court, nothing — no invitation for the accused, and no boundary against him. A man cannot cross a line the law cannot draw. Let the record show the stone did not fail: the stone did exactly as instructed, which is the oldest failure there is — and it is the instruction, not the stone, this court declines to enforce.

Charge II — casting unashed

convicted

The gap in the accused's residue, eight strokes long on the eve of an eight-stroke breach, admits one honest reading. Silent casting is proven not by what the ash records but by what it was prevented from recording. The accused's observation that the work was "tidy" is noted, and taxed.

The sentence

restitution in kind

The accused shall re-derive Bellhall's writ paraconsistent — a stone that holds two truths without going mad, at the standard four-fold inscription price — every stroke his own, poured ashed, under the Clerks' open audit. The court finds it fitting that the realm's most celebrated breacher spend a season as its most closely watched ward-wright.

And let the lords of the realm hear the dictum beneath the verdict: a ward is a theory, and you are liable for its theorems. Amend your writs the way you amend your rooflines — with an engineer present. — Justice Ambry, in re Bellhall

The Ledger · the Clerks close the book

Thirty-eight strokes, zero flips

The full accounting, entered and sealed. Note what is absent: grace. No party flipped a single fact of the Record all day — the entire war was fought in warrants, which is what courts are for. The most consequential magical dispute of the year cost the world's ledger exactly nothing, and Bellhall's lord a paraconsistent stone he should have bought the first time.
