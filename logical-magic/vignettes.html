Everyday Records

Three ordinary days, filed low — the same law that audits the fireball and the wish, run through a dying woman's name-scroll, a boundary treaty kept too well, and a clean-broken arm. The strokes are few here and the flips fewer; the world reads every word all the same.

LVIG.1

Transcription Nights

A granddaughter takes her family shift on the heirloom name-scroll, copying her dying grandmother a few thousand facts at a time, in a house where a soul is a document you finish together.

The scroll lived in a fruitwood case with a parish seal on the clasp, and it did not leave the house. To hold another's name-scroll without right is to hold the person, and the assizes are plain about that. So on the nights it was Wenna's turn she carried it only as far as the table by the bed, unrolled the working leaf, trimmed the pen, and copied until the candle guttered. A few thousand facts, some nights. It sounds like a great many. It is nothing against a life.

Most of what she set down she was only copying. The old leaves had gone soft at the folds and the ink was foxing, and an heirloom that means to last another keeping has to be renewed — a fair hand onto fresh vellum, fact for fact, nothing added and nothing let fall. That part went quickly. Copying is not living; you can lay down a thousand true things in a night, if someone has already been to the trouble of making them true.

The making had taken Gran Merrild eighty years.

A true name runs near a million facts — the old count, give or take; the scribes round it and so does everyone. Not grand facts. That she was left-handed. That she took her tea over-steeped and complained of it and steeped it so again. The name of a dog forty years dead. Her grandmother's holding, and the bound of it, and the year the beck shifted. Every one had to be lived before it could be entered, and witnessed before it could be warranted, because you cannot wish a fact onto a name-scroll. The world will not warrant a kindness about a person that is not already so. A name is the one document that cannot be flattered.

Wenna had asked once, small, why a person could be finished and not the beck that ran below the house. Her aunt had said: no one has ever finished a river. A river does not end, and what does not end cannot be held entire — only people are small enough for that. It had sounded like a sad thing to be told. She knew now it was the opposite.

They were finishing the name because a finished name is the surest instrument there is. When the estate was read the clerk would want the dead woman named to the letter — not a Merrild, of whom the rolls held four, but this one, under no reading of the word that was not her. When the little ones were blessed as Merrild's own, someone would have to hold Merrild in the saying, precisely, or the blessing would go wandering off and settle on a stranger. An unfinished name has gaps. Gaps are where the wrong person gets in.

None of it was against her dying, and Wenna kept herself clear on the point, the way her mother had been clear with her. The scroll does not argue with the Bedrock. Gran would die on some plain afternoon like anyone, and stay dead, that being the oldest law and, they say, the kindest, though it does not wear the face of kindness. What the scroll kept was not her breath. It was her name — so that afterward, speaking of her, they would be speaking of her and of no one else.

At the end there were only the last facts left, and those Wenna could copy from nowhere. They had to come from Gran.

“Say it for the leaf,” she said, the pen ready. “The Harrow debt.”

“Forgiven.” The voice was down to a thread, but it was hers. “Say I forgave it. It's true now. Now you may write it.”

Wenna wrote it. It was true now, so she could.

That was the hard seam of the work, and no one in the house pretended otherwise: to finish a person is to have her complete and to be done, in the one stroke. The last leaf of a name-scroll and the last day of the named are the same edge; you do not reach the one without the other. She caught herself writing slower as the leaf filled, which helped nothing, and which her mother, come to take the next shift, did not remark upon.

They finished it together, which is the only way it is ever finished — a hand at a time, night over night, the pen going round the household as everything did that was worth keeping and could not be plowed. That was how the keeping passed, too: not written into any will, only learned at the table, one shift at a time, until the youngest hand was the steady one. When the clerk took the completed scroll into the rolls he did not call it a soul. He called it a sound name, sound to the Vell standard, entered and sealed. The house kept the other word. A soul, they held, is a document you finish together — and then you have her whole. Not returned; never that. But whole, which is the most and the least that can be done for anyone, and enough.

LVIG.2

The Letter Kept

A hedge-farmer's boundary treaty is being honored perfectly and horribly by a fae reading nobody meant, and the visiting treaty lawyer must find the interpretation before dark.

The geese were where the widow had not put them.

Every dawn for a fortnight, Mave Cardew had found her flock stood in a neat rank along the old ditch — the dead line, thirty paces inside her own field — watered and minded, beaks all one way. Nothing had harmed them. Something had kept them. And the milk-bowl she set at the yard gate each night was carried, by no hand she ever saw, out to the fallen gatepost by the old well, and left there full and sweet: exactly as the accord said milk should be left, at a gate, nightly. Only not her gate. Not this century's gate.

“They've not touched a feather,” she told the advocate. “That's the maddening of it. My grandmother's grandmother signed the accord, and the fae have kept it to the letter every night since. It's only the letter's gone… old.”

Advocate Serrel had ridden out from the assizes with a satchel of stamped writ-paper on her knees, and she did not look up from the treaty-stone at the gate. She was reading it — all of it, down past the fair clauses a farmer keeps by heart, through the copied-forward boilerplate, to the last worn line. The way you read a thing when you have stopped hoping the trouble lies in the part anyone bothers to read.

“Here.” She tapped a clause rubbed nearly smooth. “‘The fair folk shall keep to their side of the hedge, and mind the marches and the beasts upon them.’ Your meant hedge is the quick-set your people laid at the last survey. Sensible. Fixed on the ground.” She straightened. “But the writ never says which hedge. It says ‘the hedge,’ and trusts you to know. This stone has carried that trust two hundred years and never once been asked to defend it.”

“There's only the one hedge,” said Mave.

“There is now. There was another.” Serrel nodded at the ditch. “That dead line your geese are minded to — that was the boundary before the survey moved it. It predates the writing. And a name no one pinned down will take any shape that fits it.” She said it the way one says water runs downhill. “Out past your ditch lies a reading of this accord in which ‘the hedge’ is the old line, and every clause of it is true, and something patient keeps house there — leaving its milk, minding your beasts, never once breaking a term. You can't out-argue it. The fault isn't a lie; it's a room your treaty left unlocked.”

Mave looked at the ditch, and at the light already going amber over it. “Can you lock it?”

“I can narrow the meaning until only your hedge fits.” Serrel was already drawing paper. “A definition rider — one clause, laid on the stone, fixing ‘the hedge’ as the quick-set standing at the last survey, and no line before it. Once the writ says so, the old reading stops being a reading of it at all. Their side goes back to being their side.” She glanced west. “It must lie on the stone before dusk. The nightly clause settles at dark — that's when they keep. A rider filed before then binds tonight's keeping to the meaning you meant. Filed after, your geese walk to the old well one more night, and I've missed the fair with you.”

She wrote standing, and she wrote short — for every clause added is a clause to be proved consistent, and proof is paid in strokes, one to a step. Fewer things claimed, fewer steps to warrant before her breath ran out. She read it through twice, counting the steps; then she spoke it to the stone at a drilled four strokes a second, laying down the proof that the narrowed accord still agreed with the Bedrock, and with itself. The stone took it. There was no thunder — there is never thunder, only a boundary quietly ceasing to be true where it had been true, a handful of facts turning over, nothing a vale that makes ten thousand a day would feel.

The amber went to gray. Out at the old ditch the flock stirred, milled, and — finding no line there any longer to be kept to — came wandering home to the water Mave had always meant them.

“They'll bring your bowl to this gate tonight,” Serrel said, latching the satchel. “They're honest. They were always honest. That was never the trouble.”

At the cart she paused. “Keep the rider with your deed. And read the whole of a thing before you sign it renewed — down to the last line, the parts no one reads. That is the whole of my trade.” She almost smiled. “The great Treaty they all keep under says as much, at the top: We do not fear the mage who breaks the law. We fear the one who reads it to the end. Your neighbors out past the ditch read yours to the end. My work is only to get there first.”

LVIG.3

Crack and Splint

A chirurgeon-logician's mending rite cracks clean (strokes forfeit, arm untouched, exactly as promised), and patient and healer sit with the everyday arithmetic: pay 22 strokes again, or six weeks in a splint.

The forearm was a good clean break, which Mistress Halder said aloud the way a wheelwright says a good clean spoke — approvingly, and to no one. Tamsin, who coopered barrels for the brewery on Cask Row and had felt a slipped hoop kick a stave up into her arm that morning, did not find it good. But she had come to the right window. The plate by the door read CHIRURGEON-LOGICIAN, VESSEL-CERTIFIED, and beneath it, smaller: mends bottled — never poured raw.

“Hold it as it lies.” Halder scoped the arm — this bone, these two ends, the sound shape it wanted — and began to say the rite.

It is not a long saying. The Rite of Mending runs twenty-two strokes, and Halder said them at the drilled four a second, so the whole of it took not quite six seconds: a low even recitation, each step poured into the next — knit ▷ seat ▷ true ▷ close — nothing touching the world yet, all of it held in the vessel until the last word would bottle it. Tamsin braced for the warmth everyone promised came at the end, the small deep click of a bone remembering where it lived.

It did not come. Halder reached the bottle, and the rite cracked.

She felt it decline to close — the warrant went to seat against the last step, found no purchase, and rather than force it she let the whole pour fall. Twelve flips the world would have spent on a mend; it spent none. The Record did not move. Tamsin's arm was exactly as the morning had left it: broken, unmended, not one whit worse.

“Cracked,” Halder said, unbothered, already turning the arm to the light. “Good. That's the vessel earning its keep — it mends you all the way or not at all. The mends that frighten me are the ones that take when they shouldn't.” She pressed along the bone, two inches above the fresh break, at a faint old ridge. “You broke this arm before. Long ago. Set by hand?”

Tamsin had forgotten it entirely — a hayloft, a childhood, a bonesetter with a splint and no rite, because rites were for people with coin. It had healed crooked by a hair, and she had coopered on it twenty years without a thought.

“There it is.” Halder nodded, satisfied the way a healer is by a symptom that confesses. “My scope told the world your bone was whole before this morning. It wasn't — it had an old lie set into it. The rite went looking for the sound shape to mend you back toward, met that ridge, and couldn't warrant the last step honestly. So it cracked. Cleanly. As it's built to.” She said it like a proverb, and it was in fact a theorem: a rite that cracks leaves the world as it found it.

“So — is it mended?”

“It's untouched. The strokes are spent — I said them, the labor was real, those don't come back — but nothing settled, so the world charged you nothing it didn't burn. Nothing at all.” Halder wiped her hands. “And now I know the truth of the bone, I can scope it honestly: mend the new break, leave the old ridge where it's lived its life. Same rite. Twenty-two strokes again.”

Here was the arithmetic, and Tamsin did it the way everyone does — aloud and badly. “Six seconds of talking, and I pay the same twice.”

“Six seconds of saying,” Halder agreed. “You're not paying for the seconds; any fool can make noise for six seconds. You're paying that I can prove the saying sound before I pour it — the certification, the vessel, the years I spent learning to crack instead of to maim. The raw menders down Cask Row will pour you for a third of my fee, and every so often they mend an arm to the wrong shape and name it the arm's fault.” She let it sit. “The world's part in this is nothing. Twelve flips. The vale lays down ten thousand a day and would never feel yours. It's my share that costs — the saying, not the settling.”

“And the splint?”

“No strokes, no coin. Six weeks. A cooper works one-handed for six weeks, or she doesn't work.” Halder shrugged, kind and unsentimental. “That's the whole sum. My fee, and it's done before you've buttoned your coat — or free and slow, and you nurse it through the summer trade.”

Tamsin looked at the arm; at the old ridge she'd carried all this time unknowing; at the door, and the barrels waiting on Cask Row. It was, in the end, the plainest kind of decision — the sort the world leaves to ordinary people, having declined, cleanly, to make it for them.

“Say it again,” she said. “The right way, this time.”
