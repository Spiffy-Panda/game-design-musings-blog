Problem Set One — Pleas, Prices, and One Broken Ward

Text: the LoMa pitch, §§1–6; constants from the tuning table (§8)  ·  40 marks  ·  50 minutes

Name M. SedgeAnswer key — Corvane Bench iii— Date quarter-day eve—

Instructions

  1. Answer in the spaces provided. Show the audit — marks follow the ledger, not the arrival.
  2. Price every plea in both currencies. A stroke count without a flip count is half an answer.
  3. Underline final counts. State whose ledger each cost lands on.
  4. Constants are on the reference card and may be cited without proof.
  5. You may not, for any question on this paper, simply wish for the answer. The Bursar has asked me to add: especially not with riders. — C.

Section A — Seats & circles10 marks · 2 each

Circle one letter per question.

A1. In the plea ∃x ( Gold(x) ∧ Mine(x) ), the witness for x is chosen by [2]
  1. (a) the caster, at the moment of scoping.
  2. (b) the world, among the settlements the Miser's Law leaves standing.
  3. (c) the nearest Mint clerk of appropriate rank.
  4. (d) nobody — existential pleas have no witness.

Key · (b) The whole peril of ∃ in one sentence: the warrant is yours, the witness is not. Riders narrow the world's choices; they never take the choosing back.

A2. A duelist who counterspells your ward has bought which seat in its evaluation game? [2]
  1. (a) the verifier's — they must now supply your witnesses.
  2. (b) the falsifier's — your ∀-moves are now played against you.
  3. (c) the Bench — they may rule on the ward's admissibility.
  4. (d) no seat; counterspelling is superposition of opposed wells.

Key · (b) The verifier's seat (∃) was always yours and cannot be sold. (d) is the answer to a different course — MDYN students sit two doors down, and evidently one of them wrote this option. Most-picked distractor is (a); see M. Sedge's paper, and half the bench's.

A3. Warranting a ∀ by the Schema of Ascent instead of brute instantiation reduces [2]
  1. (a) both strokes and flips — elegance is thrift entire.
  2. (b) strokes only; the flips fall due unchanged at settlement.
  3. (c) flips only; the proof is unchanged.
  4. (d) neither; induction is a notational courtesy.

Key · (b) Induction fixes your ledger, never the world's. This is the single most examinable sentence in the course.

A4. A rite whose outcome changes when its pourings are re-bracketed — (a ▷ b) ▷ c versus a ▷ (b ▷ c) — violates [2]
  1. (a) the First Duty: bottle-then-pour is just the rite.
  2. (b) the Second Duty: no free blessings.
  3. (c) the Third Duty: grouping is free; only order is fate.
  4. (d) no Duty — order and grouping are the same thing.

Key · (c) The perennial confusion. The Second Duty (v ▷ bottle ≡ v) forbids a rite from blessing what it merely hands back; the Third is about brackets. Re-read the coroners' preface until the difference hurts.

A5. No first-order description, however long, can be the true name of the river Und, because [2]
  1. (a) rivers change; the Record cannot keep pace.
  2. (b) the Mint refuses charters on moving water.
  3. (c) every first-order writ with an infinite reading has unmeant models — something else answers to it.
  4. (d) it can, but the stroke price exceeds a lifetime at 4 strokes/s.

Key · (c) (d) is true of finite souls and is why name-scrolls are heirlooms — but the river is not finite, and for the infinite the failure is absolute, not economic. Pinning the Und takes a second-order clause; hence the licensing.

Section B — Audits22 marks

Show the ledger. State whose it is. Underline final counts.

B1. A drillmaster orders the battle-standard Emberfield: ∀x ( In(x, Ball(c, 6 m)) → Aflame(x) ) at 1 m grain. (i) Price the brute warrant in strokes and give its cast time aloud. (ii) Price the warrant by the Schema of Ascent and give its cast time. (iii) One cost is identical on both routes — name it, count it, and say whose ledger it lands on. [8]
(i) 905 cells, one stroke each = 905 strokes. 905 ÷ 4 = 226… 226 minutes, call it 3¾ hours ≈ 4 minutes?? units. leaving it. (ii) base 1 + step 8 + invoke 1 = 10 strokes → 2½ s. (iii) the flips stay = 905, they land on the world (grace).

margin: a small sketch of a sphere with a jagged rim, labelled "sorry, front row."

(i) brute: 905 cells × 1 stroke = 905 strokes; 905 ÷ 4 strokes/s = 226 s ≈ 3¾ minutes of unbroken speech — a ritual, not a battle-cast. (ii) Ascent: survey 3 + base 1 + step 8 + invoke 1 = 13 strokes; 13 ÷ 4 ≈ 3¼ s. (iii) flips: 905 on either route — the settlement is the same sentence made true. They land on the world's ledger, drawn from local grace (905 ≈ two hours of a quiet vale's tide). Marks: 2 + 2 + 4. The four are for (iii); the first two questions were arithmetic, the third was the course. Re Sedge: units in (i) — strokes per second, so 226 seconds; the crossing-out shows the right instinct losing a fair fight to panic. In (ii) the survey is forgotten, as it is every year: citing the constructor is part of the proof. Three strokes is cheap; assuming the domain is inductive without saying so is how crowds get burned. (iii) full marks, tersely earned.
B2. A client, wintering poorly, brings you the plea ∃x ( Warm(x) ∧ In(x, cottage) ) and two pennies. (i) Exhibit two settlements of ≤ 1 flip that the Miser's Law permits and the client would regret. (ii) Draft one rider against each, with stroke prices. (iii) Total the lawyered plea and name the settlement it forces. [8]
(i) the cat — already warm, already inside. plea is TRUE already, 0 flips, nothing files, 2 strokes wasted. also: her bedding catches fire. warm ✓ inside ✓ one flip. bad. (ii) — (iii) base plea is 2 strokes so total 2 strokes?? it's so cheap. why is this section 8 marks

margin: "ask Brindle" circled twice.

(i) The null settlement: her cat (already Warm ∧ In) — the plea is already satisfied; 0 flips, nothing files, the fee is wasted. The hostile settlement: the bedding, alight — Warm ✓, In ✓, 1 flip, and the Miser owes her no kindness. (ii) r1 "…and x is no creature, nor any thing already so" — strikes the cat and every null model · +4 strokes. r2 "…and x warms without flame upon any chattel of the house" — strikes the bedding and its cousins · +5 strokes. (iii) 2 + 4 + 5 = 11 strokes; cheapest surviving settlement: the hearthstone itself warms — 1 flip, lawful, and exactly what she meant. Marks: 3 + 3 + 2. The trade in one line: nine extra strokes to move one flip from her bedding to her hearth. Re Sedge: (i) is full marks and correctly macabre. Then the paper stops, and the note in the margin — "ask Brindle" — is, I concede, the professionally correct instinct. But the Casebook is a bestiary, not a substitute for drafting. "It's so cheap" is how the Casebook gets new entries. Riders are the answer to the question the client didn't know they were asking.
B3. After the Assize, the lord of Bellhall repealed W3 (the tax-collector's quarter-day writ). This quarter-day, a second sapper presents last year's eight-line breach at the gate, verbatim. (i) Trace where the derivation now fails. (ii) State the sapper's losses. (iii) Where does the tax-collector stand, and why is the crown displeased? [6]
(i) the seal is still iron, so line 6 still holds, so ⊥ still comes?? the contradiction is in the FACTS. (ii) 8 strokes gone. (iii) outside. the crown wants its taxes.
(i) It fails at line 1. Crosses(taxman) was never a fact — it was W3's writ, and the writ is repealed. Lines 2–8 are a staircase with no ground floor: Iron(taxman) alone is a consistent, harmless truth. Facts don't explode; writs do. (ii) A collapsed plea forfeits its warrant: 8 strokes, gone; 0 flips either way (the breach never cost grace — that was its charm). (iii) Outside the gate, lawfully refused — no writ compels his crossing, W2 still bars his iron seal. The ward is now consistent and perfectly rude. The crown is displeased because the repeal that sealed the breach also sealed out the revenue: the lord fixed the logic by breaking the law's errand. Marks: 3 + 1 + 2. Re Sedge: (i) is the classic error, stated with admirable confidence: a contradiction needs premises, and iron was only ever half of one. Circle the derivation's line 1 and ask what rule licenses it now. Nothing does. (ii), (iii) correct — the last sentence of (iii) more so than its author suspects.

Section C — Theory8 marks · 4 each

Three sentences or fewer. A theorem cited is a sentence saved.

C1. Silent casting is lawful mathematics — the Three Duties hold over the trivial monoid. Why then is it a crime, and how was it ever proven against anyone? Answer in terms of the Vessel of Ash. [4]
Because you can't detect it, so it's banned. It was proven at the Assize because… the log was empty?
The Duties don't fail — the audit does. Pouring over the trivial monoid appends nothing to the ash, so the rite runs lawfully and leaves no account; jurisdictions outlaw what they cannot audit, not what the mathematics forbids. And it is proven by its shape: ash is continuous, so a rite poured silent leaves a gap of exactly its own length. At the Assize the Clerks read a hole eight strokes long. You cannot erase the shape of an absence. Re Sedge: "you can't detect it" is precisely half a mark — the whole point is that you can, negatively. An empty log and a gapped log are different objects. 2/4.
C2. State the Sundering of Ward-upon-Wards in one sentence, and name the discipline by which the Mint prevents its recurrence — being precise about what that discipline does not forbid. [4]
A fortress ward was chartered to ward all wards that don't ward themselves, then someone asked if it warded itself, and it's still on fire. Prevention: ban second-order magic.
The Sundering: a charter struck to ward all and only wards that ward not themselves, asked of itself — a question with no consistent answer, still burning. The discipline is predicativity, not prohibition: a new word may speak only of words already struck, never of a totality that would include itself. The Mint charters second-order works daily — the crime is self-inclusion, not altitude. Re Sedge: the first sentence is exam-perfect, including "still on fire." The second would abolish the Mint, the river-wardens, and this college's endowment. Half marks, and a term of caution about cures that outbid their diseases. 2/4.
