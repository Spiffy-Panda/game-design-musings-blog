Adventuring Guild Teller

You are the person behind the guild counter. Stamp what's true, post what's worthy, and — after hours — quietly decide who this town's heroes become.

Papers, Please hands·Stardew heart·Tomodachi Life soul

The working title is the brief's own phrasing. This page is the pitch as I understand it: first the shape, then twelve numbered claims — each one tagged by how much of it is your words versus my inference, so any misreading can be corrected by handle. Correction round 1 (2026-07-12) is folded in below: nine claims settled, three still open — AGT.1, AGT.5, AGT.7. The desk research page holds the precedent studies these claims lean on.

The shape of a day

The town runs continuously underneath; this is the order the player meets it:

  1. I · day shift

    The Desk

    Requests filed, proofs across the counter. Reference books open. Right or wrong.

  2. II · after hours

    The Floor

    The board, the favors, the loadout advice, the introductions. Your call.

  3. III · offscreen

    The Night

    Parties go down into the dark. You don't. The sim resolves without you.

  4. IV · at dawn

    The Summary

    How your characters fared, told as gossip — the hard numbers wait in every bio.

Skeleton confirmed as the player-facing cadence; underneath it, expeditions run async and can span multiple days (AGT.3).

Three pillars

Pillar I · the hook

The Desk

after Papers, Please × Strange Horticulture

The main loop and the thing most players come for: the procedural day-to-day of validating questing. Check collected herbs against their descriptions, check an adventurer's rank against a quest's demands, judge whether a request is even valid given what the guild can currently field.

Mostly objective, mostly checkable — so a practiced teller can drop into flow the way a Papers, Please veteran reads passports.

At the counter there is a right answer.

Pillar II · the decisions

The Floor

after Stardew Valley's non-farming half

Hanging out in the guild afterward — and this is where all the real decisions live. Which of today's validated quests goes up on the open board? Which gets hand-delivered to a befriended adventurer? Plus the rest of running the place.

The social verbs live here too: suggest loadouts, introduce one regular to another, and through that shape which parties form and go down. And the floor never ticks — no time limits, no decaying affections; linger, or don't (AGT.11).

Influence, never orders. And no clock, ever.

Pillar III · the fishbowl

The Roster

after Popup Dungeon × Tomodachi Life

For creators who want more: author the cast itself — starting attributes, models and paperdolls, a backstory apiece. Done mostly outside play sessions, Popup Dungeon-style.

Then the town plays with your toys: a nightly summary reports how your characters fared in the dungeon. It may flag real events — a sword that wouldn't bite — but the details always live on the character sheets in-game. Flavor forward, zero homework (AGT.10).

The summary is gossip, not telemetry.

The one distinctive move

Papers, Please generates its drama by smuggling dilemmas into the checking loop — the rules say deny, the sob story says approve, your family starves either way. This design does the opposite, and I think that's its spine: discretion is evicted from the counter. The desk stays objectively checkable so it can be flow; every judgment call is deferred to the guild floor, where there's time, tea, and consequence.

Same person, two chairs, two kinds of thinking: at the desk you ask "am I right?", on the floor you ask "what do I want for these people?" If a moral dilemma shows up at the counter, it's either a bug in the day — or a very deliberate, very rare scripted exception.

My read, in twelve claims

Each claim is tagged: given restates your brief, read is my interpretation of it, and gap marks something the brief doesn't settle. A settled chip means a correction-round ruling is folded in — struck text is superseded, the ruling sits on the green line. Handles are stable and append-only — correct by number.

  1. AGT.1givenThe seat is the counter.

    You play the guild's teller — not the guildmaster, not a party member. Registering requests and confirming completions is the job; the fantasy is quiet competence plus total context, because everything and everyone in town crosses your desk eventually.

    if the player is meant to hold formal authority beyond the counter (hiring, budgets, guild policy) rather than authority-by-influence.

  2. AGT.2givensettledThree parts, three genres.

    One part procedural job sim (Papers, Please), one part social/management (Stardew's non-farming parts), one part townee-fishbowl (Tomodachi Life, The Sims). My one framing addition: the fishbowl is the substrate the other two act on — autonomous townsfolk are what make desk stamps and floor favors matter.

    confirmed as framed — fishbowl-as-substrate included. (The full-town ruling that arrived with this confirmation lives under AGT.9.)

  3. AGT.3readsettledThe day skeleton.

    Desk shift while parties are out → guild floor in the evening → runs resolve overnight → summary at dawn. The brief never states the order; I stitched it from "day-to-day," "hanging out afterward," and "nightly summary."

    mostly yes — but expeditions can span multiple days, and departures aren't pinned to a morning tick; the fishbowl runs async beneath the daily loop, and returns queue at the desk whenever they land.

  4. AGT.4readsettledThe desk has two sides.

    Intake: register incoming requests, judging validity against rank rules and current availability. Verification: confirm completions against the request's own description — the herb check. I read "validating adventure rank" as checking a taker's rank against a quest's requirement; the alternate reading is auditing rank-up applications, which would make a great rare desk event.

    both — rank gate-checks are routine desk work, and rank-up audits are real (rarer) desk events.

  5. AGT.5readThe desk is objective on purpose.

    "Mostly right vs wrong answers so the player can go into a flow" — I take this as a hard commitment, the spine of the pitch (the callout above): the desk is not the dilemma engine, and "mostly" buys occasional variety, not routine moral squeeze.

    if you do want Papers, Please-style conscience pressure at the counter itself.

  6. AGT.6readsettled"Strange apothecary" = Strange Horticulture.

    I'm reading the desk's stated inspiration as Bad Viking's Strange Horticulture — identify the specimen on the counter by cross-referencing a lovely reference book, cozy-occult register, mistakes mislead rather than fail-state. The research page leans on it accordingly.

    confirmed — and doubled: Strange Antiquities (Bad Viking's 2025 occult-antiques follow-up) is co-canon with Strange Horticulture. The research page now covers the pair.

  7. AGT.7givenEvenings route the day's quests.

    Every quest received that day gets a routing decision: the open board, or straight into the hands of a befriended adventurer — favoritism as a first-class verb, with the board as the neutral default. "Other management of the guild" is in the brief but unspecified; I've assumed fees/upgrades/scheduling-scale housekeeping.

    on what's actually in the "other management" bucket.

  8. AGT.8readsettledInfluence, never orders.

    Loadout suggestions and introductions are suggestions: adventurers are autonomous residents who can decline, and the ownership feeling ("that team exists because of me") comes precisely from the fact that they didn't have to listen. This is the fishbowl contract applied to the social verbs.

    confirmed, with the mechanism: acceptance rides on two affinity scores — the suggestee's affinity for the player (teller trust), and their affinity for what's being suggested (the person, the loadout). Refusals get a citable cause by construction (AGR.2).

  9. AGT.9givensettledThe creator authors the cast, out-of-loop.

    Starting attributes, models/paperdolls, backstories — built mostly outside play time, with Popup Dungeon named as the bar to clear. The authored characters then live in the town like everyone else.

    ruled (it arrived under the AGT.2 reply): the game ships with a full town, and the creator covers adventurers, quest-posters, and anyone else — customization always available well beyond anything required.

  10. AGT.10givensettledThe nightly summary is flavor — and staying that way.

    "Does not provide too much that is useful and is more for flavor" — I read that as a discipline, not a shortcoming. The report is an attachment device in the Tomodachi register; the moment it carries real signal, optimizers turn it into homework. The version I'd commit to: quotable, barely actionable.

    refined: the summary may carry genuinely actionable events ("her sword failed to penetrate the hide") — that's fine. The anti-homework guarantee lives elsewhere: every detailed stat sits in each character's in-game bio, always available, so the player never has to remember or write down anything. All of it stays in-game.

  11. AGT.11readsettledThree audiences, concentric.

    Desk players (everyone — the flow hook) ⊃ floor players (the town-shapers) ⊃ creators (the customization tail). Implied by that ordering: the floor needs a light default path — "post everything to the board" — so desk-first players aren't punished for skipping the socializing.

    ruled, and stronger than my read: the floor never has a ticking clock — no time limits, no decaying affection, ever. Another round of papers plus a low-effort floor is always a legitimate day, even mid-team-setup, at zero social cost.

  12. AGT.12gapsettledStakes are unset.

    The brief doesn't say whether adventurers can fail, get hurt, or die — including the ones a creator lovingly authored. This is the biggest open lever I see: permadeath collides with cozy, zero stakes makes desk rigor and floor favors weightless. Injury-and-recovery is the obvious middle, but it's your call, not mine.

    ruled: no death — a downed adventurer respawns gearless at the dungeon entrance. The gear stays where it fell, and lost gear can seed future retrieval quests: failure generates desk content instead of grief (AGR.1, AGR.6).
