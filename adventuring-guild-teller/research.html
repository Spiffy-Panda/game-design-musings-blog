Desk research · precedents by pillar

What the neighbors already built

A precedent scan for the pitch, organized by its three pillars, plus the trope the fantasy comes from and a risk register. Each entry ends with what AGT should take and what it should skip. Facts spot-checked July 2026; anything still fuzzy is phrased to match its fuzziness.

I · The Desk — checking games

the papers-please-likes: one verb, growing reference material

Papers, Please — Lucas Pope, 2013

The founding text. An immigration booth where rules accrete day over day, the core verb is find the discrepancy, and feedback is instant (the citation slip). Its famous move is injecting moral dilemmas straight into the checking loop — rules say deny, the sob story says approve — against a salary-and-family clock.

Takeescalating rulebook; inspect-mode UI (tap two facts, get the contradiction); end-of-day ledger; shift pacing. Skipthe dilemma injection (AGT evicts discretion to the Floor — the pitch's spine) and the misery economy.

Strange Horticulture / Strange Antiquities — Bad Viking, 2022 / 2025

The confirmed "strange apothecary" pair (AGT.6, settled). A plant shop where identification is the game: match the specimen on the counter to the field guide by elimination — leaf shape, smell, habitat — for customers who describe what they need sideways. Cozy-occult; mistakes raise a dread meter and mislead you rather than fail-stating. Strange Antiquities (September 2025, same Undermere world) reruns the verb on occult antiques — proof the identification-shop loop survives a domain swap, which is exactly AGT's bet: herbs today, any quest proof tomorrow.

Takecross-referencing paper tools as the whole verb; the tactile ledger; vague customer requests as difficulty; gentle failure. Skipnothing major — these are the closest models for AGT's whole desk.

Potion Craft — niceplay games, 2021 EA / 1.0 2022

Alchemist sim where customers state symptoms, not products — you diagnose, then brew by physically navigating an alchemy map. Request-parsing as a soft check, plus a haggling minigame.

TakeNPC vagueness as a tunable dial on desk difficulty. Skipcrafting depth — AGT's teller verifies, she doesn't manufacture.

The rest of the booth — four quick files

Death and Taxes (Placeholder Gameworks, 2020): grim-reaper desk job; profile-judging under quotas — quota pressure is a clean pacing knob. Not Tonight (PanicBarn, 2018): bouncer papers-like; venue modifiers keep one verb fresh across a long game. Mind Scanners (The Outer Zone, 2021): diagnosis via gadget micro-skills under time — tool dexterity as variety. No Umbrellas Allowed (Hoochoo Game Studio, 2021): pawnshop appraisal against a manual with drifting market values — appraisal-vs-reference is literally AGT's herb check, and daily price drift shows how to keep a fixed check fresh.

Takeone lesson each, as noted. Skiptheir shared dystopian register — see the synthesis below.

Yes, Your Grace — Brave At Night, 2020

Petitioners file past the throne; resources are scarce; every audience is triage. Relevant as the counter-example: it's all discretion and no verification — exactly what AGT's desk is not — but its intake-under-scarcity is the closest model for "judging what quests are valid based on current availability."

Takeavailability as a visible, finite resource the teller checks against. Skipmaking every audience a judgment call.

Booth lessons
  • Depth comes from the reference material growing, not from new verbs — the rulebook is the difficulty curve.
  • Feedback must be immediate and physical: citation slip, stamp, dread meter.
  • Authored papers-likes run short (a few evenings); a long-tail cozy game needs procedurally generated requests and proofs — see AGR.1.
  • The genre defaults to bleak. The cozy seat is nearly empty — that's the opening.

II · The Floor — social, management, indirect control

after-hours verbs: routing, favors, introductions

Stardew Valley — ConcernedApe, 2016 (non-farming half)

The named reference for pillar II. What its social half actually runs on: NPCs with legible daily schedules (you can find anyone, so relationships feel pursued), friendship hearts gating authored vignettes, gifts as the currency, and a festival calendar as the town's clock.

Takelegible routines; content gated by friendship depth; the calendar heartbeat. Skipgifting-as-vending — two loved gifts a week is a toothless economy; AGT's favors should cost something real (AGR.4).

Recettear: An Item Shop's Tale — EasyGameStation, 2007 / EN 2010

Item-shop sim where adventurers are your regulars: what you sell and lend them shapes the dungeon runs they take (and that you can tag along on). The proto-model for "my counter choices equip the town."

Takeadventurers-as-regulars; gear provenance you can trace ("that's the sword I sold her"). Skipaccompanying them into the dungeon — AGT stays behind the counter on purpose.

Potionomics — Voracious Games, 2022

Potion shop where socializing has mechanical teeth: relationships with a fixed cast unlock haggling cards, and befriended heroes go adventuring to fetch you ingredients. (Corrected note from checking: Quinn is the ingredients vendor; the adventuring is its own befriend-and-send system.)

Takerelationships that pay off in play; send-a-friend-out as a loop. Skip negotiation-as-card-combat at the desk — that's a flow-breaker in AGT's booth.

Majesty — Cyberlore Studios, 2000

The grandfather of indirect control: you can't command heroes, only post bounty flags and let class personalities decide (rangers wander, rogues chase gold). This is AGT's quest board with a kingdom budget — and its frustrations are the genre's textbook: when you can't tell why nobody took the bounty, charm curdles.

Takeincentives as the only steering wheel; personality-driven refusal. Skipopaque intent — refusals must come with readable reasons (AGR.2).

VA-11 Hall-A / Coffee Talk — Sukeban Games, 2016 / Toge Productions, 2020

Bartending as a social frame: a light mixing task keeps your hands busy while regulars talk. The counter as confessional — the exact register AGT's guild floor wants at night, where listening is the content and agency is deliberately low.

Takethe serve-and-listen rhythm for after-hours scenes; regulars whose stories advance on their own clock. Skipfull VN linearity — AGT's cast is procedural-ish and player-authored.

Darkest Dungeon — Red Hook Studios, 2016 (the embark half)

Not the combat — the roster: quirks, stress, provisioning, and the party you assemble before anyone leaves town. Proof that team assembly alone carries expressive weight, and that visible quirks make refusals and failures legible.

Takequirks as the vocabulary that explains behavior; loadout-before-embark as the player's authored fingerprint on a run. Skipthe stress-grind economy.

Dungeon Village — Kairosoft, 2012

The RPG town seen from the town's side: adventurers visit, you make the place attractive, outfit them, and watch them level and settle. Charm-loop proof that "town as attractor" sustains a whole game.

Taketown appeal as a slow stat; adventurers who immigrate because of your work. Skip the spreadsheet flatness — Kairosoft casts are interchangeable; AGT's must not be.

Adjacent one-liners: Moonlighter (Digital Sun, 2018) is the inversion — shopkeep who is the dungeon-diver; useful only as a boundary marker. Guild of Dungeoneering (Gambrinous, 2015) and Loop Hero (Four Quarters, 2021): more indirect-control kin — you shape the world, the hero walks it.

Floor lessons
  • Influence-not-command is a proven pleasure if intent and refusal are readable.
  • Favors need costs. A hand-delivered quest should move jealousy, board economy, or reputation — otherwise it's a menu, not a decision.
  • After-hours socializing works best as its own register (bar-time), not as a management screen with dialogue on top.

III · The Roster — authored casts and fishbowls

create, release, read about it at dawn

Popup Dungeon — Triple.B.Titles, 2020

The named bar for the creator (AGT.9): make heroes, abilities, enemies from your own images, numbers, and writing — papercraft minis on a tabletop — and the game folds them into play and quotes your text back at you. Also the cautionary tale: the creator's breadth took a Kickstarter half a decade to ship.

Takecreator = portrait/paperdoll + small stat block + prose the game can quote — the nightly summary should splice the player's own backstory lines into reports. Skipability scripting and effect editors (AGR.5).

Tomodachi Life — Nintendo, 2013/2014

Create Miis, drop them on an island, watch. You service requests and can nudge — including literally introducing residents to each other, AGT's floor verb — but residents refuse, drift, and surprise. The news-and-dreams reports are the tonal model for AGT's summary: absurdly specific, socially juicy, useless as data.

Takethe gossip-report register; nudge-able but refusable relationships. Skipthe pure toybox — AGT's fishbowl still has jobs to do (quests, ranks, a town economy).

The Sims / Wildermyth — Maxis, 2000 / Worldwalker Games, 2021

The Sims wrote the fishbowl contract: autonomous wants + free will = lives worth watching that don't need you. Its wants system is the hook AGT can service via quest routing ("Brindle wants a forest job"). Wildermyth is the state of the art in making players care about generated people fast: heroes accrue scars, relationships, and comic-panel history that persists on the character sheet.

Takewants as routable hooks; visible consequence-marks on the paperdoll (the scar from the job you posted). Skipdirect control of anyone, ever.

One-liners: RimWorld (Ludeon, 2018) is the anecdote-engine benchmark — systems tuned to produce tellable stories. Football Manager's text match reports prove reading-about-it can bond you to generated people harder than watching it would — encouraging for a summary that's prose on purpose.

Roster lessons
  • Author-then-watch is the strongest ownership loop there is — but only if the world visibly uses what was authored.
  • Reports should quote the player's own writing back (Popup Dungeon's trick, pointed at Tomodachi's register).
  • Consequences belong on the character sheet, where attachment lives — not in a stats screen.

IV · The seat itself — and its audience

the guild receptionist is already a beloved fantasy

The counter seat AGT proposes is a fixture of JRPG-adjacent fiction: the guild receptionist who registers the party, posts the notice, and knows everyone — Guild Girl in Goblin Slayer, Luna at Konosuba's counter. It even carries a whole series: I May Be a Guild Receptionist, but I'll Solo Any Boss to Clock Out on Time (Mato Kousaka, Dengeki Bunko, 2021; CloverWorks anime, early 2025) — whose receptionist heroine secretly solos bosses because stalled dungeons mean overtime. The joke works because the audience already believes the premise underneath: the desk is where the adventuring world actually runs. AGT plays that premise straight and never picks up the war hammer.

Meanwhile the games nearest the fantasy all sit in a different chair: shopkeeper games (Recettear, Moonlighter, Potionomics) put a counter between you and adventurers but sell to them; guild-management games make you the guildmaster moving units. The teller seat — verify, route, influence, never command — appears to be unshipped. That's rare.

V · Synthesis — the gap, and the risk register

where AGT stands, and what precedent warns about

The positioning gap: checking games skew dystopian; cozy games don't check anything. A warm bureaucracy — Papers, Please's hands on Stardew's town square, with Strange Horticulture (cozy-dark) as the nearest proof the blend holds — is close to unclaimed shelf space. The risk register below is append-only, handles AGR.n, each tied to the pitch claim it pressures.

  1. AGR.1Content fatigue at the desk.→ AGT.4, AGT.5

    Authored papers-likes ship 2–6 hour arcs and stop; AGT wants a long cozy tail. Requests and proofs must generate procedurally against a growing reference library without turning into rule soup — the desk's hardest engineering problem.

    helped by round 1: lost gear persists in the dungeon and seeds retrieval quests (AGT.12) — failure itself now feeds the request generator. The rule-soup half stays open.

  2. AGR.2Indirect control curdles when intent is opaque.→ AGT.8

    Majesty's lesson: autonomy delights until the player can't tell why the bounty sat untaken. Every refusal needs a readable reason on the surface ("she won't take swamp jobs; ask her brother").

    the round-1 mechanism makes causes citable by construction: acceptance is two visible affinities — teller-trust and target-liking (AGT.8). Surfacing them is now UI work, not AI archaeology.

  3. AGR.3Summary utility creep — in either direction.→ AGT.10

    If nightly reports carry signal, optimizers grind them into homework; if they're pure noise, attachment fades. Tomodachi holds the line with surreal specificity; Football Manager proves prose bonds. Tune to quotable, barely actionable — and keep it there on purpose.

    reframed by round 1: summaries may be actionable ("her sword failed to penetrate the hide"); the anti-homework line is held by always-available in-game bios instead — never memory, never out-of-game notes (AGT.10). The risk moves to bio UX: the sheet must answer every question the summary raises.

  4. AGR.4Favoritism needs teeth.→ AGT.7

    Hand-delivering a quest must trade something off — board liquidity, other adventurers' jealousy, the teller's reputation for fairness — or routing is a menu cosplaying as a decision. Stardew's toothless gifting is the warning.

  5. AGR.5Creator scope creep.→ AGT.9

    Popup Dungeon spent years shipping its editors. AGT's creator should stay a paperdoll, a small stat block, and prose cards the game can quote — never an ability scripter. The nightly summary is what makes shallow authoring feel deep.

  6. AGR.6Tone is decided by the stakes policy.→ AGT.12

    The genre's bleakness is a choice, not a law — but so is coziness. Permadeath would break the warm register; zero stakes would make the desk's rigor decorative. Injury-and-recovery (visible on the paperdoll, per Wildermyth) is the obvious middle; unresolved until AGT.12 gets a call.

    ruled — no death: a downed adventurer respawns gearless at the dungeon entrance, and the gear stays where it fell, becoming future quest hooks (AGT.12). Cozy holds; the desk keeps real stakes through material loss and story.
