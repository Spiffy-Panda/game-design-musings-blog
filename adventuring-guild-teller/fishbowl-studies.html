Design studies · pillar III, the machinery

How should the fish-bowl think?

The pitch fixed what the bowl must produce; the desk research fixed the register it must speak in. Neither picked the machinery. This page surveys six ways games have simulated a town — at surface level, on purpose — scores them against the bowl's actual job, and composites a pick. The pick is then specced as a playable prototype in the fish-bowl proposal.

The job description

criteria first, taken from the settled claims — the machinery serves these

The bowl is the substrate the desk and floor act on (AGT.2). It is read, not watched: the player meets it as a dawn summary in the gossip register (AGT.10), while expeditions and lives run async underneath the daily loop (AGT.3). So the question is never "does the town behave believably at 60 fps" — it is "does the town generate tellable material, and can a reader always find out why." Six criteria, each traceable to a settled claim or risk:

What the machinery is scored on
  • Gossip yield — distinct, tellable events per night at village scale; the summary must never starve or repeat itself into wallpaper (AGT.10, AGR.3).
  • Explainability — every event traceable to citable causes. Majesty's curdle is the standing warning: autonomy delights until nobody can say why (AGR.2).
  • Authorability — a player-authored townee (paperdoll + statlet + prose, never a scripter) must slot into the machine whole (AGT.9, AGR.5) — and directed feelings must be first-class, because acceptance is two affinity scores (AGT.8).
  • Dawn-cadence fit — the machinery must tolerate being read once a day. Systems built to be watched live waste their best material offscreen (AGT.3, AGT.11).
  • Build weight — implementation and tuning cost in a Godot / C# prototype. Scored as lightness: ● means cheap.
  • Determinism — same seed, same week. Not a claim; a research requirement — the prototype exists to answer questions, and unrepeatable runs answer nothing.

Six machineries

surveyed at surface level — each ends in adopt / mine / decline

FBS.1

Clockwork timetables

Stardew Valley (town half) · The Legend of Zelda: Majora's Mask · Animal Crossing

Every resident owns an authored timetable — place by hour, weekday and festival variants, a few exception rules (rain, holidays). The town is a train station: the 7:10 always arrives, and the player learns the board. Stardew's social half runs on exactly this legibility (the research page already took it); Majora's Mask is the form at its most polished — a 72-hour town you can set a watch by.

And that is the whole trick: clockwork presents lives, it does not generate events. Nothing responds to anything; a pure timetable bowl starves the summary by construction. Majora's is gorgeous because every minute was authored — which is precisely the budget a procedural town doesn't have.

Adopt — as Layer 1. The stage, the schedule, the co-presence: who is where, together, when. Cheap, deterministic, findable (AGR.2 loves it), and the substrate every other candidate turns out to need. Never the drama.

FBS.2

Needs & utility agents

The Sims lineage (Maxis, 2000 →) — decaying meters, smart objects that advertise

Agents carry decaying need meters; the world advertises satisfactions (the smart-object trick — the fridge broadcasts what it cures); each tick, the agent takes the highest-scoring bid. The result is the famous fishbowl feel: lives that visibly drive themselves, and wants the rest of the game can service — the research page already earmarked wants as hooks the desk can route.

Two problems for this bowl. Behavior isn't story: a utility town converges on eat-work-sleep loops that are true but untellable. And the arbitration is soup — "why did she skip lunch?" has a fourteen-term answer, which is exactly the opacity AGR.2 forbids. The Sims also earns its keep by being watched live; a bowl read at dawn throws that away.

Mine — for the meters. Keep slow-moving drives as pressure that arms events (Layer 2 fuel); drop per-tick action arbitration entirely. The meters stay; the soup goes.

FBS.3

Goal planners

GOAP after F.E.A.R. (Monolith, 2005) · Oblivion's Radiant AI as the cautionary tale

Agents hold goals; a planner chains world-model actions to reach them. Jeff Orkin's F.E.A.R. write-up made the pattern famous, and it genuinely produces novel chains — the machine improvises. The Elder Scrolls IV shipped the town-scale version as Radiant AI, and the lore around its pre-release demos (townsfolk creatively wrecking quest state until the leash went back on) is half legend — but the tuning tax the legend names is real.

For this bowl it's the wrong expense: plans are opaque artifacts (explaining one means dumping a search trace), authoring the action/world model dwarfs authoring timetables, and nobody here even watches the town move — the prototype is readouts, not pathing. Improvisation is the one thing we can't explain at dawn.

Decline. Every point it scores is a point somewhere this design doesn't keep score.

FBS.4

Social practices & storylets

Prom Week / Comme il Faut (UCSC EIS, 2012) · Versu (Evans & Short, 2013) · King of Dragon Pass · the Fallen London lineage

Social state (directed feelings, statuses, shared history) plus a library of authored moves and scenes, each with preconditions and effects; whatever is eligible fires, weighted. Prom Week's social physics and Versu's practices are the deep dialect; storylets — King of Dragon Pass's scenes, Fallen London's deck — are the tractable one. Both aim drama exactly where the state is interesting.

Two properties make it the spine candidate. Explainability comes free: a fired event can print its own preconditions — the because-list AGR.2 demands is just the rule's left-hand side. And directed dyads are native: AGT.8's two-score acceptance (teller-trust × target-liking) drops into the state model without adaptation. The cost is honest: the library is the content, someone must keep writing it, and alone it's a deck with no table — it needs a substrate generating co-presence and pressure to check preconditions against.

Adopt — as Layer 3. The event grammar: JSON-authored rules over the clockwork's co-presence and the meters' pressure, emitting chronicle entries that carry their own causes.

FBS.5

Colony work & economy

RimWorld (Ludeon, 2018) · Dwarf Fortress (Bay 12, 2006 →)

Needs plus job assignment plus real resource flows; stories emerge when the flows fail. RimWorld is the research page's anecdote-engine benchmark for a reason — but look at what its anecdotes are made of: harvests, sieges, and hauling disasters. Colony-survival stories, generated at colony stakes.

A village of twelve isn't a production chain, and the desk's town doesn't need one: "Petch's shelves ran bare" is one scalar going low, not a commodity graph. Economy simulation is a correctness tarpit (every flow is a new way to be wrong), and its drama arrives at scales the bowl will never run.

Decline the ledger — and keep the moral: livelihood gives days texture and failures stakes. It survives as two Layer-2 drives (purse, trade), not as flows.

FBS.6

Knowledge & rumor propagation

Talk of the Town / Bad News (Ryan et al., UCSC) · Shadows of Doubt (ColePowered, 2023) · Crusader Kings III

Agents hold beliefs about facts — who knows what, learned from whom, possibly wrong — and information travels through co-presence, decaying and mutating as it goes. Talk of the Town simulated more than a century of a small town's hearsay to power the live performance piece Bad News; Shadows of Doubt runs a whole information economy; Crusader Kings trades secrets as currency.

For a game whose pillar rule is "the summary is gossip, not telemetry," this is the only candidate that makes the rule literal: the dawn report becomes what the town heard, not what the engine logged. The full-fat version is a research project (Talk of the Town is famously deeper than it is playable) — but one hop of it is nearly free once clockwork co-presence exists: an event reaches the summary only if someone was there to carry it.

Mine — the thin slice. A heard-it-from layer (witness → carrier → summary) riding the co-presence graph. Belief distortion, misremembering, and long-term memory stay declined until the bowl earns them.

No single machinery clears the bar — the two adopts fail in opposite directions (clockwork can't generate, storylets can't stand alone), which is the tell that the answer is a composition, not a winner.

The composite — a clockwork town, under pressure, that files events

CPS: clockwork · pressures · storylets, plus a summarizer wearing the gossip register

The engine writes a log; the town tells a story; the player reads the town. Effects also flow back up — storylet aftermath moves pressures and regard.

Layer 1 — Clockwork (FBS.1): authored day-plans resolve into a slot-by-slot itinerary. Its one output is the co-presence timeline — the stage.

Layer 2 — Pressures (FBS.2 distilled, FBS.5's moral): four slow drives per townee plus directed regard per dyad, drifting by data-driven rules. Pressures never pick actions; they arm the layer above.

Layer 3 — Storylets (FBS.4): JSON-authored rules whose predicates read co-presence, pressures, regard, and calendar. Firing mutates state and files a chronicle entry that records the predicate snapshot that let it fire.

The Summarizer (register per the research page; selection thinned from FBS.6): at dawn, pick 5±2 entries by tellability, but only events a gossip-carrier witnessed or later heard about — the summary quotes the town's telephone game, not the engine log. Each line renders through the actionability dial (AGR.3, now a knob instead of a guess), and marked events append dated one-liners to bios (the Wildermyth move, proposed as FB.8).

Why this order and not another: storylets without clockwork have no stage to check predicates against; pressures without storylets are meters nobody narrates; clockwork alone starves the summary. Each layer is exactly the part of its donor machinery that survived the criteria — and what got declined pays rent too: no planner means every event can explain itself; no ledger means tuning six dials instead of sixty.
