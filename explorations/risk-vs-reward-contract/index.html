Pricing the Danger

The faction prices the deficit and prices the distance. It will never price the one thing that decides whether you get paid at all — whether the drop-off is still standing when you arrive. That number is on the map, not the contract.

A 3× payout into a system that falls in two ticks isn't a windfall. It's a voided trip with a fat number on it — and the number is the bait, because the danger was always in the geometry, never in the price.

This is the rose lineage — the enemy/front reading of Minimalist Space Logistics. You own one hauler keeping an unwinnable war supplied. The friendly faction posts contracts (R.4): each pays base(deficit) × distance — fatter where the front is starving, fatter the farther you haul. That payout is honest about two things and silent about a third.

The thing it stays silent about (M2.4): the enemy front advances on a known, seed-fixed schedule (R.5), and a drop-off three jumps into a segment about to fall is worth zero if you arrive after it's overrun. The faction won't pay you a risk premium for that — the only tell is where the drop-off sits relative to the line. The skill the game trains is reading that geometry against a fall clock. The instrument below lets you practice it, then cheat to check your work.

How to read it

M2.4.1 · The payout prices two things and hides the third

Open the deck. Every card shouts one number — the payout — and that number is doing honest work: it scales with the front's deficit (how badly the war needs this good) and with the haul distance (how far you carry it). That's R.4, the faction's whole pricing model. What the card will not tell you is the only thing that decides whether you collect: the drop-off's ticks-to-overrun. The faction doesn't know your ship's position, doesn't model your risk, and has no incentive to discount a doomed run. So the fattest payout on the board is frequently the deepest drop-off into the most threatened segment — distance pays, and distance is exactly what walks you into the fall.

M2.4.2 · True EV = payout × P(arrive in time) − haul cost

The real value of a contract isn't its payout. Flip REVEAL TRUE EV and each card grows a second block: its drop-off's ticks-to-overrun and its honest EV. The arithmetic the faction won't do for you:

if haul time ≤ ticks-to-overrun → you arrive in time → EV = payout − haul cost
if haul time > ticks-to-overrun → overrun first → contract VOIDED → EV = −haul cost (a wasted trip)

That's why a 3× payout into a system falling in two ticks, when your haul takes four, is exposed as deeply EV-negative: you were never going to arrive. The reveal toggle annotates exactly this, turning the implicit geometry into the explicit number the faction declined to print — and a fat payout next to a red falls in 2t · arrive in 4t is the whole lesson in one card.

M2.4.3 · Fog → mastery: risk is just knowledge you don't have yet

Leave FOG on and the board is what a first run feels like: a payout and a vague sense of how close the drop-off sits to the line. You're guessing. Now play several boards, watch which picks void, and a pattern resolves — the front advances on the same schedule every time for this seed (R.5). Turn FOG off (or REVEAL on) and the guesswork becomes a readout: the cased map of a veteran who has run this war before. This is the M2/R.5 mastery arc in miniature — early on, "will it fall before I arrive?" is a gamble; with the schedule learned, it's a certainty you read off the geometry. The danger never changed. Your knowledge did.

M2.4.4 · Greedy loses to EV-aware over a run

Run the auto-play race. GREEDY-BY-PAYOUT takes the biggest number on every board — the strategy the contract UI quietly nudges you toward. EV-AWARE computes the same true EV the reveal shows and takes the best positive one, skipping the board entirely when every contract is a baited void. Over a couple hundred boards greedy racks up voided trips — full hauls into systems that fell, each one a total loss plus the wasted distance — and bleeds out behind the picker that learned to price the danger. The gap between the two lines is the skill this page teaches, made financial.

The aha, in one line: the payout prices the deficit and the distance; it never prices the danger — and learning to price the danger yourself, by reading the drop-off against the fall clock, is the game.

Risks, named

Legibility — the danger must be readable without a label that spoils the skill

If the contract simply printed "ticks-to-overrun: 2" next to the payout, there'd be no skill left — the player would read a number instead of a map, and the entire M2.4 reading-the-geometry loop collapses into arithmetic the game did for them. But if the geometry is illegible, the player can't tell a doomed drop-off from a safe one and the voids feel like noise, not consequence.

Mitigation the model shows: the danger lives in the lane map — drop-off position vs the advancing front, the only honest channel — and the contracts carry a wordless proximity bar and a ▼ near-front flag, never a ticks number. The REVEAL TRUE EV toggle exists as a teaching aid, off by default: it's training wheels for learning the geometry, not the default HUD that does the reading for you.

Determinism — if the fall clock drifts, "risk" never becomes "knowledge"

The whole mastery arc rests on the fall schedule being learnable. If overrun timing ever consulted wall-clock time, an unseeded Math.random, or a non-fixed draw order, then board #7 of a seed wouldn't match the board #7 the player rehearsed — and "I learned this war" silently becomes "I got lucky," breaking R.5 and the seed-as-a-run promise (APR.5).

Mitigation the model shows: one reseedable mulberry32, advanced in a fixed per-board order (front advance → draw goods → place drop-offs → price payouts), with the fall schedule a pure function of the seed and the advance slider. Same seed and settings reproduce the same boards and the same overrun times, every run — which is exactly what lets the auto-play race two strategies against an identical sequence of wars and attribute the gap to the picks, not the dice.

This page sits in the rose lineage beside enemy-attack-schedule (the offensive's learnable opening book) and front-as-fluid (the front as advancing pressure). It borrows their fall clock and asks one focused question: given a board of honestly-priced contracts, which is actually worth your one hauler's next run — and why the biggest number is so often the wrong answer.
