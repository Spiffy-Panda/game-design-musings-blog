The Contract Board

You run one hauler. The faction posts the work. Every contract on the board is a coin the treasury could spare and a hole at the front it needs plugged — so the board, read as a whole, is a live X-ray of a war you never see directly.

A quiet board is not the designer's quest list drying up. It's a war going broke. When the postings thin out and skew military-first, that isn't less content — it's the treasury telling you, in the only language it has, that it can no longer afford to want everything it needs.

This is the amber lineage — the economic reading of Minimalist Space Logistics. Supply and demand is the friendly faction AI's job (R.4): it reaches you the way a trucker meets the economy in Euro Truck Simulator — as job postings on a board. The faction fights an unwinnable war on a finite treasury that drains as it pays bounties and refills slowly on war bonds; the front runs chronic deficits in military goods.

A sibling page — utility-ai-fit — already covers how you, the player, should score and rank these contracts (response curves vs greedy pick). This page is about something else: the board itself as an economic signal across a whole run — how it is generated, how it expires, and how a player learns to read it. Not which job to take. What the shape of the offers is trying to tell you.

How to read it

R.4.1 · The board is the treasury, drawn sideways

Nobody hand-authors this list. Each posting cycle the faction does one cheap, legible thing — a budget-constrained greedy fill, knapsack-flavored:

1. scan unmet front-segment deficits → rank the gaps by urgency
2. walk the ranked gaps; for each, compute bounty = base(good) × urgency × treasury-confidence
3. post it only while the spendable slice (treasury − floor) can still fund that bounty
4. stop when the slice is exhausted, or the slot cap is hit

That's the whole generator. The generosity of the entire board — how many jobs, how fat the payouts — falls straight out of one treasury number. A fat treasury floods the board with many high-bounty contracts; a thin one posts a short list of lean, military-first jobs and cuts the generals entirely. You are not reading a content schedule. You are reading a live histogram of what the war can afford to want.

R.4.2 · Generosity tracks the treasury — watch them move together

Open the sim and watch three numbers move as one: the treasury gauge, the generosity meter (sum of every live bounty), and the posting count. Drag Starting treasury up and the board floods; drag it down and it empties. They are not three independent dials — generosity is the treasury, expressed as offers. The "treasury-confidence" factor means a war that's running low doesn't just post fewer jobs, it posts stingier ones: a faction tightening its belt pays less per job even for the jobs it still posts. That double squeeze is why a board can feel rich one stretch and threadbare the next on the very same map.

R.4.3 · A thinning, military-skewing board is an early-warning system

Here is the read the whole page is built to teach. As a run wears on, the treasury drains faster than war bonds refill, and the front advances. The board responds in a specific, learnable order: first the generals get cut (food, parts, luxuries — nice-to-haves the war can no longer justify), then the payouts shrink on what's left, then the count collapses toward the floor. A board that is all military, all small, all urgent is a faction in its last stand. Learn to read that shape and you get a warning the game never spells out: the war is losing, and your income is about to dry up with it. The "war going broke" state isn't a fail screen — it's a thin amber line of three desperate jobs.

R.4.4 · Expiry reads the front, and the danger isn't in the payout

Contracts expire on the same clock that overruns systems. A posting's value decays as its drop-off nears the front; if the destination is overrun before you arrive, the contract is voided — struck through on the board, paying nothing. Crucially (M2.4), the danger is not priced into the payout. The faction won't pay you a risk premium for a near-front run; the only tell is where the drop-off sits relative to the advancing line. A fat bounty on a system the front is about to swallow is a trap the board states plainly without ever warning you — you read it in the geometry, the little ▼danger marker, not the number. That's deliberate: pricing the danger in would do the reading for you.

The aha, in one line: the board isn't authored content — it's a live histogram of what the war can afford to want. A quiet, military-skewed board is the cheapest early-warning instrument in the game, and it costs the designer nothing because it's just the treasury, drawn as a list.

Risks, named

A board that goes too quiet starves the player

If generosity is purely the treasury and the war is losing, the board can empty so far that the player has no viable income exactly when they most need to fund a comeback — a death spiral the player can't act their way out of, which reads as the game being broken rather than the war being lost.

Mitigation the model shows: the spendable fraction is a treasury floor — a slice the faction will dip into to keep a minimum board alive even when broke. Pull the floor slider down to 0 and watch the board flatline to nothing; raise it and a thin-but-real board of military jobs survives the worst stretch. The floor is the difference between "hard" and "unwinnable-and-boring."

Legibility — "why are there so few jobs?"

A board that silently thins is indistinguishable, to a confused player, from a bug or content that ran out. If they can't tell why the offers dried up, the signal is noise and the early-warning value is lost.

Mitigation the model shows: a one-line bounty rationale is pinned under the board every cycle ("treasury at 12% → only 2 military jobs funded, generals cut"), and the treasury gauge sits directly above the postings so cause and effect are stacked. The "why so few jobs?" question always has a visible, plain-language answer — the board explains itself.

Determinism rot

If posting generation ever consulted wall-clock time, an unseeded Math.random, or a non-fixed iteration order, two runs of the same seed would post different boards — and a seed-as-a-run economy quietly stops being reproducible (APR.5), breaking replays and the scrub bar above.

Mitigation the model shows: one reseedable mulberry32 advanced in a fixed order, integer-credit bounty math, and a fixed cycle order (refill → advance front → expire/void → rank gaps → greedy post). The scrub bar replays a stored history; same seed and settings give the same run, every time.

The board is the faction AI's whole external surface (R.4): you never see the treasury ledger or the front's order-of-battle, only the offers they cast off. That's the economy of it — one cheap greedy generator turns a hidden war's solvency into a list a player can learn to read at a glance.
