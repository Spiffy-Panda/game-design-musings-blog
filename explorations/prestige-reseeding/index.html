Cartographic Prestige

You never buy a stronger hand or a weaker enemy. You spend the prestige a loss banked on where supply is born — and a better map is the only thing that ever makes the front hold.

The campaign win is reached sideways: a macroeconomic equilibrium you bend one producer at a time, until production finally outpaces attrition — and you never once reach past the ledger to touch the war.

Minimalist Space Logistics hands you one hauler and a war that cannot be won in a run. Every run ends in an overrun, and that's the point: loss is the currency. Being overrun banks prestige APR.4 — the deposit you carry between runs. The trick the whole meta turns on is what prestige is allowed to buy.

It can never buy the war down. There is no difficulty dial, no "the front holds" button, no stat on your hauler that goes up. Prestige buys position and possibility APR.3 — a producer, a shipyard berth, a little more starting reach. It edits the agent population and the economy HAND.5, never your numbers and never the front HAND.6. Seeding a refinery doesn't make you stronger; it instantiates a new place that supply comes from, permanently reshaping every downstream price gradient for the next run.

So the campaign is won the long way around. You lose, you bank, you re-seed the map a little closer to demand, and you lose again — but the world you were born into this time produces a touch more where it matters. Loop after loop, sustainable production climbs toward a fixed attrition line. The run you finally cross it, the front holds on its own — not because you bought a win, but because the macroeconomy you've been editing finally tips into an equilibrium that favours you. This page makes that tangible: a map you re-seed, and a ledger you watch cross.

the modelA banded supply map, a price gradient, two lines that cross

Lay the theatre out as a small banded graph: rear → midfield → forward, the forward edge being the front, where the war consumes. Every node carries a supply level and, from it, a price. Producers you've seeded emit a good; that good diffuses outward across lanes, attenuating with every hop — so supply is high (and price cheap) near a producer, and low (and price dear) far from one, especially out at the hungry forward edge.

  • The gradient. Price at a node is its unmet demand: how much the front's pull exceeds the supply that actually reached it. A producer flattens the gradient near itself — cheap there, supply meeting demand — and barely touches nodes many hops away. A producer in the deep rear is real supply that mostly evaporates before it reaches the line.
  • Sustainable throughput. What the front can be held at this loop is the supply delivered to the forward edge — production weighted by how close it sits to demand. Geometry is everything: the same output, seeded one band forward, lands far more on the line.
  • Attrition (the war). A fixed line prestige can never touch HAND.6. It even rises as you hold more territory forward — a real headwind, not a number you lower. You only ever raise the production line to meet it; you never lower the war to meet you.
// per node n: supply is the sum of seeded producers, attenuated by graph distance
supply(n) = Σp ∈ producers output(p) · attdist(p,n)   // att ≈ 0.46 per hop; near-producer ⇒ high, far ⇒ ~gone
price(n) = max( 0, demandPull(n) − supply(n) )   // the gradient = unmet demand
// what the front can be held at this loop — supply that reaches the forward edge
production = Σn ∈ forward edge min( supply(n), cap(n) )   // cap ⇒ diminishing returns on over-seeding one spot
attrition = base + creep · (held forward territory)   // fixed by the war; prestige never edits it
if ( production ≥ attrition ) the front holds   // the sideways win — an equilibrium, not a purchase

Every term is fixed-point integer — supply, price, attenuation, the lot are scaled by a constant and only ever added, multiplied-and-shifted, or floor-divided. A loop is a seed APR.5: same producers, same map, bit-for-bit the same gradient and the same two lines. The only randomness is a reseedable mulberry32 used for cosmetic node jitter — never for the economy.

how to drive the instrument

You have a prestige balance (top-left), banked from losses. Pick a good and an output, then click a node on the map to seed a producer there — it costs prestige, and the price-gradient heat-field instantly re-flows: cheap (cyan) blooms around your producer, and the dear (pink) forward edge flattens if you seeded close enough. Then press Run next loop: the run ends in a loss, banks more prestige, and the loop ledger on the right plots this loop's sustainable production against the fixed attrition. Seed forward, run again, and watch the production line climb. When it crosses attrition, the front holds — that's the campaign win, reached entirely from the ledger.

Campaign log seeds · loops · the crossover

what you just didYou bought a better world to be born into

Notice what you never did. You never lowered the attrition line — you couldn't; there's no control that touches it, by design HAND.6. You never bought a stat. Every click spent prestige on a place supply comes from, and the only thing that climbed was the production the map can sustain. The win, when it comes, is the green line crossing the rose one — an equilibrium tipping over, not a purchase clearing.

The geometry is the whole game. Seed three producers in the deep rear and the heat-field tells the truth immediately: a bloom of cheap cyan back home, and the forward edge still pink and starving, because that supply attenuates to almost nothing over the hops to the line. The ledger barely moves. Seed one producer one band forward — closer to demand — and the same prestige flattens the dear edge instead; production jumps, and the gap to attrition visibly closes. You're not buying supply, you're buying the distance between supply and demand.

That's the reading of HAND.5 made literal. "Production-station locations" isn't flavour for a stat bump — it instantiates a new producer on the map, and because price is a gradient over graph distance, every downstream node's price moves the instant it lands. You are editing the agent population. The war just lives with the economy you leave it.

And the win is sideways APR.4. No single loop wins; loss is the deposit every time. What wins is the slow accretion of a supply map that finally produces, at the forward edge, more than the front burns. You nudged a macroeconomy into an equilibrium that holds — one manifest at a time — and the campaign tipped over without you ever firing a shot or buying the front down.

why it's a climb, not a snowballThe two brakes that keep it honest

An economy you only ever add to wants to run away — seed enough and production explodes past attrition trivially, and the sideways win becomes a formality. Two model terms keep it a genuine climb, and you can feel both in the instrument:

  • Diminishing returns on over-seeding. Each forward node caps how much delivered supply it can route to the line. Stack a fourth producer behind a node already at cap and the gradient there is already cyan — the new output mostly spills into price you don't need. The ledger shows it: the second producer in a fresh spot buys far more than the fourth behind a saturated one. Spread where demand actually is, or pay for surplus.
  • Attrition that scales with what you hold. The line isn't flat. As your production lets the front sit further forward, attrition creeps up — more territory held is more territory the war pushes on. The target you're climbing toward backs away as you climb, so the crossover takes real, geometry-aware seeding, not brute repetition. (It's still reachable — just not free.)

risks, namedWhere this design can rot

Snowball / runaway. A prestige economy that only ratchets up is the classic meta failure: once you're ahead you stay ahead, every loop is more producers and more production, and the "climb" flattens into an inevitability you grind out. If over-seeding always pays, geometry stops mattering and the sideways win is just a number of loops.
Mitigations, both in the model: diminishing returns on piling producers behind one node (a per-node delivery cap, so the fourth refinery in a saturated spot is mostly wasted), and attrition that scales with held territory — the harder you push the front forward, the harder the war pushes back. Together they make the crossover a real, geometry-aware climb: you must seed closer to demand, not just more, and the target recedes as you near it. The win stays reachable; it just refuses to be trivial.
Illegibility of a long-run macro outcome. The campaign win is a macroeconomic equilibrium that shifts over many loops — exactly the kind of slow, diffuse outcome a player can't see happening. If all they get is "you won on loop 14," the most important system in the game is invisible, and the satisfying part — watching their map tip the balance — never lands.
Mitigation: surface the two things that make it legible — the gradient field (so a seed's effect on price is immediate and spatial, cheap blooming where you placed it) and the crossover chart (so the long-run climb of production toward a fixed attrition line is a single, readable picture, loop by loop). The "why" line names each shift in plain numbers — "refinery at C → forward gradient flattened, production +14" — so the macro outcome is something you watch converge, not a result you're told.
