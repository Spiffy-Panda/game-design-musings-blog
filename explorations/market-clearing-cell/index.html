The Clearing Cell

One market, one good, no money — just a price that nobody sets and everybody pushes. This is the cell the whole economy is grown from. If it doesn't feel alive when you poke it, nothing built on top of it will.

Nobody hand-places this price. It emerges — a producer adding stock, a starving front pulling it, and you shoving cargo across the counter, all trying to clear at once. You don't trade against a number; you trade against a tendency.

This is the root of the amber lineage — the economic reading of Minimalist Space Logistics that calls itself The Invisible Hand. The pitch is blunt about where to start: build the single-market clearing cell first, because "if this one cell doesn't FEEL like a market reacting to you, nothing scales up." Everything else — multiple systems, your hauler arbitraging between them, the war overhead — is just a mesh of these cells trading with each other.

Deliberately, there is no money here. No treasury, no wallet, no payout. This page is the pure price-discovery base: a market that quotes a price and lets that price move. The sibling cell solvency-cell clamps what a station can actually pay you by its cash on hand; here every counterparty is assumed liquid, so we can watch price alone, with nothing to muddy it. How to read it

HAND.1 · The rule: damped tâtonnement that never quite clears

Each tick, the market totals up what everyone wants. Net excess demand is

net_demand = (consumer_draw + your_buys) − (producer_add + your_sells)

and the quoted price nudges by a constant-elasticity rule on that flow, normalized by depth:

Δprice = k · elasticity · (net_demand / depth)

then it is damped toward that adjustment so it settles instead of ringing. The catch — and the whole point — is that it never reaches the fixed point: the producer keeps adding, the front keeps pulling, so the moment it would land it is shoved off again. Economists call the groping-toward-a-price process tâtonnement; in a live game it never converges, and that permanent not-quite-cleared wobble is the gameplay. The dashed amber line is where the price would settle given the current producer/consumer balance — the equilibrium it orbits but won't touch.

HAND.2 · Thin vs deep — the same trade, two different worlds

Depth is the denominator. A single hauler's load of cargo barely tilts a deep market (big depth → small Δ) and lurches a thin one (small depth → violent Δ). Drag Market depth to ~12 and SELL 40 — the price craters. Drag it to ~120 and SELL 40 — it barely flinches. Same order, same goods; the only difference is how much water there was to absorb the splash. The thin frontier systems are where one ship moves the price; the deep core is where it doesn't. (Demo ① scripts exactly this contrast.)

HAND.3 · Self-correction — your success eats your edge

Find a fat shortage and keep selling into it and something quietly cruel happens: you flatten the very gradient you were living on. Every unit you dump is supply the market didn't have a moment ago; the price you were beating sinks toward equilibrium with each repeat trade, and your margin thins to nothing. The your edge gauge is literally the distance from equilibrium — watch it collapse as you hammer one side. The lesson the game is built to teach: you get paid for being EARLY, for filling a gap before the invisible hand does. Arrive late and there is no gap left, because the earlier you already closed it.

HAND.4 · The heal — let go and it relaxes back

Stop trading. With your pressure gone, the producer and consumer reassert their balance and the price relaxes back toward its moving equilibrium, orbiting it again. Nothing resets it by fiat; it heals because the underlying flow was never gone, only briefly out-shouted. That reversibility is what makes the market feel like a place rather than a vending machine — your dent fills back in, and the gap can re-open for the next early mover. (Demo ③ milks, then stands back so you can watch the relaxation.)

The aha, in three moves:

1. Thin vs deep — same SELL of 40, one market shrugs, the other convulses. Depth is the whole difference between a frontier price and a core price.

2. Milk a margin — keep selling into the shortage and the gradient you were harvesting flattens under you. Your edge gauge drains.

3. Stop — and the price heals back toward the orbiting equilibrium. The gap re-opens for whoever's early next.

Risks, named

Legibility collapse

Elasticity, depth, damping, net flow, a moving equilibrium — it is easy for the player to stop understanding why a price moved, and then the depth is just noise they can't act on.

Mitigation the model shows: keep depth and elasticity stable within a market so its feel is learnable, and pin one plain-language why-line to every move ("you sold 40 into a thin market → price dropped 14%, gradient flattening"), backed by the signed net-flow bar and the supply/demand tilt. The model stays small enough to hold in your head (APR.1).

Oscillation / runaway

A constant-elasticity rule with no brake will ring like a bell or blow up — price ping-ponging tick to tick, or marching off to a rail and pinning there. Either way the market stops being readable.

Mitigation the model shows: the damping slider is the brake — pull it toward "settle" and the wobble tightens around equilibrium; push it toward "twitchy" and you can watch the ringing start. Per-tick Δ is also bounded so a single huge order can't teleport the price across the board.

Determinism rot

If price discovery ever consulted wall-clock time, an unseeded Math.random, floats that drift per-CPU, or a non-fixed iteration order, two runs of the same seed would diverge — and a seed-as-a-run economy quietly stops being reproducible (APR.5).

Mitigation the model shows: one reseedable mulberry32 advanced exactly once per fixed tick, integer / fixed-point price math, and a fixed evaluation order (produce → consume → your orders → reprice). Same seed and the same trades on the same ticks give the same market, every time.

This is the pure market base — price discovery and nothing else. The sibling cell solvency-cell stacks money on top: it clamps a station's payout by the cash actually in its till, so a real shortage on a broke counterparty pays you almost nothing. Here every counterparty is liquid by assumption, which is exactly what lets us isolate the one thing this page is about: a price that emerges, drifts, and heals. Build the clearing cell first; the network is just clearing cells that trade with each other (APR.2).
