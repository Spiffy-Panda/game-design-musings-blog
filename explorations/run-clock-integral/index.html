The Endogenous Doomsday

The run doesn't end on a turn counter or a minute timer. It ends when the front overruns you — and the front advances by exactly the supply you failed to deliver. The doomsday clock is an integral, and you are the one writing it.

You are not racing a clock. You are racing the running sum of your own failures to cover — supply well and you author a long run; let a shortage fester and you bring the end on yourself.

Minimalist Space Logistics gives you one hauler and an unwinnable war to keep fed. There's no scoreboard you can run out, no clock ticking down to zero in the corner. The run ends when the front overruns you — and the front advances because it is eating its supply faster than anyone can deliver it. So the doomsday is endogenous HAND.2: the timer isn't handed to you from outside, it is computed from your coverage. Cover the demand and the clock barely moves; let it starve and the clock leaps forward to meet you.

This page takes the most quietly brutal reading of that front — the hand reading of the clock — and makes it literal. The front's displacement, how far the line has eaten toward you, is the running integral of the shortage you didn't cover. Not a metaphor: a fixed-point accumulator that adds max(0, demand − coverage) every tick. When that accumulator crosses an overrun threshold, the run is over. Unmet burn becomes lost ground M2.3 — and ground, once lost, is on the board.

the modelDisplacement is ∫ of the gap

A run is a sequence of fixed ticks, evaluated in a fixed order, in integer / fixed-point arithmetic, from a single seed APR.5 — so two runs of the same seed and the same coverage are bit-for-bit identical. Each tick has three numbers that matter:

  • Demand d(t) — what the front eats this tick. A baseline intensity you set, plus some seeded variation (the war is noisy), plus — optionally — a term that grows with the backlog you've let pile up. Hunger compounds.
  • Coverage c(t) — what your hauler delivers this tick. This is the curve you author: a steady rate, or a live slider you ride as the demand plays out.
  • The gap g(t) = max(0, d − c) — the uncovered shortage. Cover fully and the gap is zero; fall behind and it's whatever you left on the table. This is the shaded band, and it is the only thing that moves the clock.
// every fixed tick, in fixed order, integer arithmetic
gap = max( 0 , demand − coverage )   // the uncovered shortage this tick
displacement += gap · dt   // FRONT ADVANCE = the running integral of the gap
backlog += gap · dt   // unmet burn accumulates (drives compounding hunger)
demand = base + noise(seed) + hunger · backlog   // optional positive feedback
if ( displacement ≥ overrun ) end_run()   // the endogenous doomsday

The whole clock lives in line two. Displacement is the area under the gap. Hold the gap at ~0 and the integral is flat — the line crawls, the run stretches out indefinitely. Let a shortage open and leave it, and every tick you don't cover stacks more area under the curve; the displacement lurches, and the line swallows the next system early. The "estimated ticks to overrun" readout is just (overrun − displacement) / current_gap — a live projection of when the area you're accumulating will reach the wall.

how to drive the instrument

Press Play and a demand curve (rose, with seeded variation) plays out tick by tick. Ride the coverage slider — or drag the coverage line directly on the top chart — to keep your sky-blue line on top of the rose one. Wherever coverage falls short, the gap shades amber; the middle chart is the running integral of exactly that shaded area, climbing toward the red overrun line. Watch the big ticks-to-overrun number: cover well and it runs to ∞ (the clock stalls); let the gap fester and it collapses. Hit “let it fester” to take your hands off and watch the integral lurch — then compare the two ghost traces.

Run log covers · lurches · the overrun

what you just didYou authored a clock by covering — or failing to

The first thing the instrument teaches is that the corner timer is a lie of convenience in most games and here it's been replaced by something honest. There is no countdown. There's a demand pouring in, your coverage answering it, and the area between them — the amber gap — being quietly summed into the front's advance. When you held coverage on top of demand, the gap pinched to zero, the integral went flat, and the big number ran to ∞: the clock stalled because you gave it nothing to count. The doomsday is something you push out indefinitely by supplying, not a fuse lit at the start.

The second thing it teaches is why falling behind hurts out of proportion. The displacement is an integral, so a shortage you leave open doesn't cost you a fixed penalty — it costs you area, and area grows with time. Ten ticks uncovered isn't ten units of trouble; it's a wedge of trouble that gets wider the longer you ignore it, because the gap keeps stacking. That's the lurch: the displacement line kinks upward, and the ticks-to-overrun number — which is just the remaining distance divided by the current gap — collapses from comfortable to single digits in a few ticks. Unmet burn became lost ground M2.3, and the ground compounds.

Turn compounding hunger up and the lesson sharpens into a warning. Now demand itself grows with the backlog you've let pile up: fall behind, and the front eats faster precisely because you're behind. The gap feeds the backlog, the backlog feeds the demand, the demand widens the gap — a positive-feedback spiral where the end accelerates toward you. Crucially it's a slider, not a switch: at low values it's a gentle penalty for sloppiness; cranked, it's the death-spiral that ends careless runs fast. Either way the loss isn't a wall you hit — it's a consequence you authored, tick by uncovered tick.

So the skill the AMBER clock asks for isn't reflex against a timer — it's reading an integral before it reads you. The ghost traces make the whole thesis visible on one pair of axes: cover it stays a flat green floor along the bottom; let it fester peels up and off the top early. Same seed, same demand, same wall — two completely different doomsdays, and the only variable was how much of the gap you chose to cover.

one fiction, two readingsThe integral vs. the price you pay

This is the same amber lineage as the sibling economic pages — the hand reading of the clock rather than the ledger reading. Where solvency-cell and market-clearing-cell ask can you afford the next delivery, this page asks what does not delivering cost you, accumulated over the run. Same invisible hand, pointed at the clock instead of the wallet: the market reading prices each move; the integral reading sums every move you didn't make into the distance the front still has to travel. Pick the reading that makes the doom feel most like your own doing at the table — this one makes it impossible to blame the timer.

risks, namedWhere this design can rot

An integral is hard to feel. "Your displacement is the time-integral of the uncovered gap" is true and completely opaque to a player mid-run. If all they see is a number marching up, the cause→effect that makes the system fair — this shortage, right here, bought that much advance — is invisible, and an invisible clock feels arbitrary, which is the one thing an endogenous clock must never feel.
Mitigations, all on screen: shade the gap as a literal area on the demand/coverage chart, and draw the running sum directly as the second chart — so the integral isn't described, it's shown accumulating the exact band above it. The ticks-to-overrun readout and its meter translate the abstract area into the only currency a player cares about — time left — and the one-line "why" narrates each lurch in plain language ("12 ticks uncovered → displacement +N → overrun in ~M"). Legibility of an integral is a rendering problem, and it's solved by drawing the area and its sum side by side, not by hoping the player does calculus.
Runaway compounding. The demand-grows-with-backlog feedback is the most dangerous knob here: set it too hot and a single missed tick spirals to an unrecoverable loss with no counterplay, which feels like the game cheated. A positive feedback loop with no ceiling is a difficulty cliff, not a mechanic.
Mitigation: the feedback is a tunable coefficient, defaulting to off — the base clock (pure ∫ of the gap) is fully playable and fair on its own, and hunger is layered on top as an opt-in intensity. Even on, it's bounded by the same fixed-point arithmetic as everything else and is meant as a tension dial, not a kill switch: at sane values, an alert player who covers the gap promptly always pulls out of the spiral. The instrument shows the extreme precisely so the failure mode is visible and tuned against, not discovered in production.
