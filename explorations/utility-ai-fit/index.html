Does a Utility AI Fit?

Score each candidate action by passing weighted considerations through response curves, then take the argmax. A clean engine — but clean for what? This is a fit audit of utility AI across the three decision-makers in Minimalist Space Logistics, and it says plainly where the answer is no.

A posted bounty is not a command. It is a dent in the player's utility landscape — and a thing that only ever dents landscapes is exactly what utility AI was built to be.

MSL is one hauler keeping an unwinnable war supplied across ~14–20 systems, rear to forward. You buy cheap, haul, sell into the shortage at the front, bank the margin, get overrun, bank prestige, repeat. Three pieces of the sim make decisions, and the temptation — once you have a nice utility-AI hammer — is to drive all three with it. Resist that. Below, each decision-maker gets weighed on its own merits.

The reference architecture here is the designer's own public, AI-authored utility-AI project, PandasAutonome. Its load-bearing ideas, summarized transformatively:

  • Considerations & response curves. A score is Σ curve(property) × magnitude over a handful of considerations. Each consideration reads one game property, bends it through a curve (rising, falling, a cliff, a gate, a step), and contributes a weighted vote.
  • Modifiers. Memories, directives, and passive effects are one mechanism: anything that additively reshapes an agent's scoring — an actionBonus on a specific action, possibly with a completionReward.
  • The embodied / disembodied split. Embodied agents physically act. Disembodied agents realize goals only by issuing directives that reshape the utility landscape of the agents beneath them. They never haul anything. They change what hauling is worth.

Hold that last bullet. It is the whole verdict.

The faction poster, as a tunable scorer

The friendly faction posts contracts (R.4): Euro-Truck-Sim-style job postings that say "haul this good to that system for this bounty." Today it is specced as a budget-constrained greedy knapsack — rank unmet front deficits by raw urgency, post bounties down the list until the treasury's spendable slice runs dry. Why the faction is the strong fit

Map the faction onto the reference architecture and it lands in exactly one slot: it is a disembodied agent. It owns no ship. It cannot move ore. Its only verb is to issue directives — and a posted bounty is a directive in the PandasAutonome sense: an actionBonus attached to "haul good X to system Y," carrying a completionReward (the payout) and a lifetime (the contract expiry).

The board is a utility landscape, and the faction is the thing that dents it. The player is the embodied agent below. The player already scores hauls on their own private considerations — buy price, sell price, distance, overrun risk, hold space. The faction cannot reach into that scoring and command a route. What it can do is post a bounty, which raises the player-visible utility of one specific haul. The player still takes their own argmax. The faction just changed the terrain it runs over.

That is the textbook disembodied-utility-agent loop, and MSL gets it for free from the fiction. No contortion. The contract board was always going to exist (R.4); modeling its author as a utility scorer is not adding a system, it is naming the one already implied.

And the fit pays rent in concrete places the greedy knapsack struggles with — each visible in the lab:

  • The overrun cliff. Greedy ranks by deficit, so it cheerfully posts the juiciest bounty at a drop-off that will be enemy-held in two ticks — luring your one hauler into a crater. A cliff curve on overrun-risk lets the faction stop wanting deliveries it can't expect to see fulfilled, without hand-coded "if risk > X skip" branches. Tune the cliff in the lab and watch a high-deficit forward contract drop off the board.
  • Treasury confidence as a gate. A gate curve on treasury state lets the faction get pickier when broke and spammier when flush — a smooth policy, not a step function bolted on after the fact.
  • Military priority as a step. A step curve on good-category encodes "munitions outrank rations at the front" as one tunable lever instead of a special case threaded through the ranking code.
  • Legibility through curve shapes. "Why was this posted?" answers as a bar chart of considerations (the breakdown panel) — which is arguably more legible to a designer than a knapsack's opaque sort key, because each lever is named and visible.
The faction never hauls. It only changes what hauling is worth. That is the one job description utility AI was born for — so here, the answer is an unforced yes.

Why the enemy is the wrong tool

The enemy offensive planner is currently a keyframed schedule + threshold automaton — a deliberately dumb thing that advances on a timetable and trips a few thresholds. Reaching for utility AI here is the textbook "solution looking for a problem." It is worse than unnecessary; it actively dismantles three things MSL is built on.

1It erases the asymmetry that is the whole game. The core fantasy is that you cannot out-trade a thing that does not trade. The enemy's refusal to optimize is what makes your optimization meaningful and your loss inevitable. Give the enemy a utility scorer and it starts responding to your routes — now it's a trading partner in a negotiation, and the "small competent person inside a catastrophe" tone collapses into a symmetric duel. 2It destroys legibility. A keyframed schedule is readable: the player can learn the war's rhythm, anticipate the next push, and feel clever for routing around it. A utility argmax over the enemy's situation is opaque — the front lurches for reasons the player can't reconstruct, and "the war has a pattern you can master" becomes "the war is noise." 3It spends the determinism and tuning budget for nothing. Every consideration and curve is state you must keep deterministic (APR.5) and a knob you must tune and balance. The schedule needs none of that. You'd be buying opacity, fragility, and weeks of curve-balancing in exchange for behavior the keyframes already deliver — and deliver better, because "dumb and predictable" is the spec, not a limitation.

This is the honest core of the audit: a technique fitting one decision-maker beautifully is not evidence it fits the next one. The enemy's dumbness is a designed feature. Utility AI's job is to make an agent responsive — which is precisely the property the enemy must not have.

Rival haulers — moderate, but shelved

Rival haulers (R.2) are the one place utility AI would fit naturally and embodied: a rival is an agent that scores routes on buy/sell/distance/risk and takes its own argmax — the same scorer the player reasons about, pointed at an NPC. It would even reuse the player's mental model of "what's a good haul." That is a clean moderate fit.

But rivals are shelved, and the audit respects scope: the moment they're real, this is the second place to deploy the engine — after the considerations and curve library already exist from the faction poster, so the marginal cost is low. Until then it's a hypothetical, and we rate it as one. Embodied-but-deferred, not strong-and-now.

The one-line takeaway

Utility AI earns its keep in exactly one place: the faction as a disembodied directive-issuer whose contract board is a utility landscape it reshapes. It is the wrong tool for the enemy, whose dumbness is the design. A fair yes here, no there.

Determinism footnote (APR.5)

Utility AI is compatible with MSL's hard determinism constraint only under discipline: response curves evaluated in fixed-point (no float drift across platforms), considerations summed in a fixed iteration order, ties in the argmax broken by a stable key (contract id), and any reseed driven by the one seeded PRNG. The lab here uses JavaScript floats for display convenience; a shipping implementation would quantize every curve output to a fixed integer scale before summing. Get that wrong and "deterministic" quietly becomes "deterministic on my machine."
