The Jumpgate Web

One hauler. An unwinnable war. The lane graph is the board — and a front that deletes nodes from one edge is the only opponent that matters.

A graph that looks richly connected can hinge, catastrophically, on a single vertex. The right topology turns "a node fell" into a strategic event. The wrong one erases geography entirely.

Minimalist Space Logistics is a routing game first. You buy cheap in the rear, haul forward, and sell into shortage at the forward line, banking the margin. You never fight. The war is sovereign: the enemy front advances, overruns systems from the forward edge inward, and that severs or reverses the lanes touching them. The whole game lives or dies on one design choice — what shape is the jumpgate web?

The seed for this page: "there are jumpgates, like X4 — a standard jump-gate web like Freelancer / X-series / Stellaris / Mass Effect relays." So the rest of this is a tour of those webs, made pokeable. Five real space games already ran this experiment for us; below you can load each one's topology over the same sixteen systems and watch how it behaves when the front eats it.

What you're meant to see

Load Mesh and start clicking forward systems — the route barely moves, because there's always another way around. That's the failure mode: geography that shrugs off losses isn't a board, it's wallpaper. Now load Pipe-corridor or Backbone and overrun the single keystone the heatmap is screaming about. The route doesn't degrade gently — it jumps, or a clutch of depots goes black all at once. That discontinuity is the whole game.

This is the Niarja lesson, in your hands: in 2020 a live event in EVE Online severed one high-sec system on the Jita–Amarr trade run, and the safe route went from about 9 jumps to about 44. One vertex. Find your keystone before the war does.
the five webs · what each game taught

Five shapes, five lessons

Each button above is a real game's answer to "how do ships get between stars," distilled to a topology you can stress-test. One sentence each on where it comes from:

Archetype · sourceShapeFront behaviour & tradeoff
Ring
EVE high-sec		 A single loop; every system touches exactly two neighbours. Elegant but thin — only two route lengths exist. The front cuts the near arc; reroute the long way until both arcs fall. Extreme chokepoint sensitivity, almost no depth.
Hub-and-spoke
Freelancer houses		 One or two high-traffic capitals; everything radiates from them. Spoke losses are trivial; a hub loss collapses the map. Drama, but the whole game revolves around one point of failure.
Mesh
X4 two-tier · dense		 Every system wired to 2–4 others; no dominant node. The front must erase a wide column before supply chokes. Rerouting is free. Forgiving to the point of having no tension — geography barely matters.
Backbone + clusters
Mass Effect relays		 A spine of junctions (one per band); small clusters hang off each. Lose a backbone junction and its entire cluster strands at once. Threshold moments at band scale — the recommended core.
Pipe-corridor
EVE null-sec pipes		 A backbone, plus a single-strand forward corridor with one way in. No reroute inside the pipe; lose its mouth and the whole corridor is gone. Lethal, high-stakes — a gamble route, not a default.

Two games sit at the edges of this space and define it by contrast. Stellaris proved topology only matters if you enforce it: while warp drives and wormhole generators let fleets opt out of the hyperlane graph, chokepoints and static defence were meaningless — patch 1.8 ripped both out and only then did geography become strategic. And Elite Dangerous is the control case with no gate graph at all: any ship jumps to any star in range, so space collapses to a pure distance metric and the entire board-game layer — defensible systems, severable corridors — simply vanishes. MSL commits to the graph on purpose. That commitment is the genre.

the recommendation

A board worth shipping

Load Pipe-corridor one more time — that's the proposal. Backbone + local clusters as the spine (one junction per band, a small resource cluster hanging off each), plus one forward pipe as an optional high-stakes fast lane with no in-pipe reroute. Sixteen systems: small enough to hold the whole map in your head, structured enough that a single fall is an event.

Why this shape

The backbone gives you threshold moments. Individual depot losses sting but never lock you out — the dead-end cluster spokes are designed to cost cargo, not routes. Then the front reaches a junction and a whole band's cluster goes dark in one beat. Pressure builds, then breaks. That cadence is the drama the mesh can't produce and the hub produces too bluntly.

The pipe is the gamble. It's the shortest path to the frontmost depot when it's open — real margin, real speed. But it has one mouth and no way to reroute inside it. Take it when you're ahead; get caught in it when the front jumps and you eat the full retreat. It's the optional spice on top of a sound spine, exactly the Freelancer insight (a corridor you can sever without taking the system) married to the EVE null-sec pipe (no reroute once you're committed).

Keep it ~16. Bigger and the keystone stops being legible; the player can no longer feel which vertex the war is walking toward. The threat has to be readable to be a threat.

The aha. Connectivity is not resilience. A web can look generous and still pivot on one node — and that fragility, made visible and made the player's problem, is what turns a supply map into a game.
