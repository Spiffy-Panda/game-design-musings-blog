The Solvency Cell

A shortage is only worth what someone can actually pay for it. This is the single station where that truth gets enforced — three accounts, double-entry, one tick.

The market can scream for cargo all it likes. If the till is empty, demand without solvency is worthless to you — the bid sags to what the books can cover, and you get paid in the gap.

This is the mutation called The Two Ledgers — the money branch of The Invisible Hand. The Hand made prices emerge from order flow. M1 asks a harder question: where does the money come from? In most trading games it's conjured at the point of sale — you sell, coins appear. Here, every credit you earn was already sitting in someone else's account a tick ago. Nothing is minted when you sell. You're moving money that already exists from one ledger to another, and the whole drama is whether the ledger you're selling into has any left.

Below is the thing the M1 pitch says to build first: the solvency cell. One station, one good, three accounts, on a fixed deterministic tick. Poke it.

How to read it

M1.1 · Two ledgers, one wallet between them

There are exactly three accounts: the faction treasury, the station till, and your wallet. Every money move is double-entry — it debits one and credits another in the same tick, the amounts equal, nothing minted at the point of sale. Watch the ledger strip flash DR / CR pairs. The green conservation light sums all three every frame and checks it against a reference that moves only when the war-bond legitimately mints (the one sanctioned inflow — see M1.2). Trades never change the sum; the light stays green. If it ever drifted on a plain trade, that's conservation rot, and the model is lying to you.

M1.2 · The faction treasury and the war-bond tick

The front is a consumer with a bottomless appetite but a real budget: each tick it buys the station's output and the money comes out of the treasury into the till. The treasury only refills from one place — a periodic war-bond tick that credits it. That's the only door new credits legitimately enter through (logged as a MINT, never disguised as a trade), plus a floor the treasury refuses to dip below. Without that external income the treasury can't grow, so it can only fund as much demand as the station's upkeep happens to cycle back — and the whole board stagnates. Turn both guards off with the demo and watch the treasury flatten at its reserve while demand starves and price sinks to the floor.

M1.3 · Stations go broke, or stations go fat

A till is not a number that only goes up. It carries a cost: every tick it pays a small upkeep back to the treasury, proportional to how fat it is — so a rich till bleeds faster and only steady income holds it up. Route demand through the station — keep it stocked so it keeps on-selling to the front, which keeps paying its till faster than upkeep drains it — and the till climbs. Cross the hub threshold (the amber notch on the gauge) and its bid ceiling lifts (22→30 cr): big sells now clear without clipping, and it becomes a reliable premium buyer. Stop feeding it and upkeep bleeds it straight back down past the notch — fully reversible. Run Fatten the hub, then Drain the till, back to back.

M1.4 · You are paid in the payer-gap

The core inversion. The station's effective bid for what you sell it is bid = min(price · qty, till). When the till can't cover the market price, the offered price sags toward what it can afford — the amber "market asks" bar visibly clips to the blue "till can afford" line, and the green "you're paid" bar is the shorter of the two. A fat shortage sitting on an empty till pays almost nothing. Your profit isn't the shortage; it's the solvent shortage — the slice where a hungry market and a funded till overlap. That gap is the whole game.

The aha, in three moves:

1. Hit Drain the till — sell hard into the station. Price collapses even though inventory is scarce and the front is starving. Shortage ≠ payday.

2. Hit Fatten the hub — feed it, let the front refill the till past the notch. The bid ceiling lifts; the same cargo now sells for more.

3. Through all of it, the conservation light stays green. The books always balance to the credit.

Risks, named

Deflationary spiral

With no external income, the treasury can't grow — it can only re-spend what the station's upkeep cycles back. Demand stays capped below what production needs, inventory piles up, price floors out, the till stagnates, and your sell-bid with it. The board doesn't crash; it quietly stops paying you, which is worse because it looks fine.

Mitigation the model shows: the war-bond tick (a bounded external credit) plus a treasury floor keep money entering the loop. Toggle them off via Guards off → deflate to watch the bid stagnate at the floor, then tick the war-bond back on and watch price, till, and your bid recover — same seed, no reset.

Conservation rot

If any posting credits without an equal debit — money minted at the point of sale — the invariant drifts and every downstream price is built on a leak. It's the kind of bug that hides for months.

Mitigation the model shows: a single double-entry post() primitive that always moves between two accounts, and a Σ light recomputed every frame from the raw account values. Green is a continuous proof. The only deliberate inflow (war-bond) is logged explicitly as a mint, never disguised as a trade.

Legibility collapse

Three accounts, a clamp, elasticity, fattening — easy for the player to stop understanding why a price moved, at which point the depth is just noise.

Mitigation the model shows: every price change writes a plain-language why line ("till can't cover bid → price sagged 38%"), the clamp is drawn as clipping bars rather than left as arithmetic, and the gauge marks the one threshold that matters. The model stays small enough to hold in your head (APR.1).

This is a single-cell prototype. The real game wires a handful of these together across systems and lets your one ship arbitrage the solvent gaps between them — selling into the funded shortage, hauling away from the broke one — while the war stays sovereign overhead (APR.2). Build the cell first; the network is just cells that trade.
