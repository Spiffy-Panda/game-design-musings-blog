The Deflation Spiral

A war economy can starve with full warehouses. The goods are there; the money to pay for them has pooled and stopped moving. Velocity is the vital sign — and three guards keep the blood pumping.

Money is never minted at the point of sale. Every credit you earn was sitting in someone else's account a tick ago. So the only question that matters is: is it still circulating, or has it pooled and died?

The sibling page, solvency-cell, models one station: a single till, and the clamp bid = min(price·qty, till) that pays you only what the till can cover. That's the micro view — one cell, one shortage, one payer-gap. This page is the macro: the whole money supply at once. One faction treasury, a handful of station tills, the front, and your wallet — and the thing that flows between them all, tick by tick, conserved to the credit.

In the "Two Ledgers" reading (M1), money is a finite, simulated resource. It isn't conjured when you sell. It enters the economy through exactly one door — a periodic war-bond credited to the treasury — and from there it must keep moving: treasury pays bounties to you and pays the front; the front buys station production, which fills the tills; you trade against the tills; and a small carrying cost bleeds each till back up toward the treasury every tick. Stir all that and a naïve economy just sloshes — money goes round, nothing breaks. The carrying cost is what makes pooling possible: money drains up into the treasury, and if it isn't pushed back down fast enough, it piles up where it can't pay anyone. The board goes quiet. You can't earn. That's the deflationary spiral, and it is a macro phenomenon — no single till is the culprit.

The five money moves, every tick, in fixed order — each one is double-entry: it debits one account and credits another by the same integer amount. Nothing is created except at the war-bond door.

  • 1 · War-bond tick — a guaranteed credit into the treasury. The only money source. Guard ①.
  • 2 · Bounties — treasury → your wallet, but only while the treasury sits above its floor.
  • 3 · Trade — your wallet ↔ the tills as you buy and sell goods.
  • 4 · Front pays tills — the front buys station output, paid out of the treasury into the tills. This is what re-circulates the hoarded treasury back down. Guard ②.
  • 5 · Carrying cost — each till bleeds a small fraction back up to the treasury. The leak that makes pooling — and therefore the spiral — real.

A sixth rule isn't a flow at all: a treasury floor (Guard ③) tops the treasury up when it's broke, forcing a minimum board so bounties never fully stop. Toggle any guard off and watch velocity decide the economy's fate.

how to read itVelocity is the vital sign

MSL.1 · Money is conserved — the books always balance

There are exactly four kinds of account: the faction treasury, the station tills (five of them), the front, and your wallet. Every money move is double-entry — one post() primitive that debits one account and credits another by the same integer amount, in the same tick. Nothing is minted at the point of sale. The green conservation light sums every account each frame and checks it against a reference that moves only when the war-bond legitimately mints (logged as a MINT, never disguised as a trade). Trades, bounties, the front's purchases, the carrying-cost leak — none of them change the sum. If the light ever drifted on a plain trade, that's conservation rot, and the model would be lying to you.

MSL.2 · A naïve model sloshes — the leak is what makes the spiral real

Here's the subtle part. If money only ever moved down (treasury → front → tills → you), the economy couldn't pool: a slow source plus circulation just redistributes the same pile, and the board never goes quiet. Run Naïve model (leak off) and you'll see it — velocity wobbles but the tills never starve. The carrying cost is the missing ingredient: it bleeds money up, against the flow, into the treasury. Now the direction of money depends on a race — leak draining up vs. the front pushing down. Throttle the front (Guard ②) and the leak wins: money climbs into the treasury and pools, where it can't pay anyone. That's the deflationary spiral, and it only exists because something drains upward. The guards are the counter-forces.

MSL.3 · The three guards, and what each one does

  • Guard ① — the war-bond tick. A guaranteed external source. Without it the treasury can only re-spend what the leak recycles, so the whole supply slowly grinds down to a trickle. It's the heartbeat: a bounded credit that keeps money entering the loop.
  • Guard ② — the front pays the tills. Circulation. This is the pump that pushes the hoarding treasury's money back down into the tills, against the leak. Throttle it and pooling wins; open it and the tills stay funded and keep paying you.
  • Guard ③ — the treasury floor. A backstop. When the treasury is broke, the floor tops it up so bounties never fully stop — it forces a minimum board even in the worst case, so the spiral bottoms out instead of flatlining to zero.

MSL.4 · The phase diagram — drag it across the line

The 2-D phase plot maps war-bond rate (the source, x-axis) against front-pays-tills rate (circulation, y-axis), shaded healthy where the economy can sustain a circulating supply and spiral where the leak wins and money pools out of reach. The amber dot is your current operating point — drag it and the two sliders move with it, live. Cross the boundary toward the lower-left and watch velocity collapse, the tills trend to zero, and the board go quiet; drag back up-right and circulation recovers. The boundary isn't drawn by hand — it's the same fixed-point model probed across the plane.

The aha, in three moves:

1. Hit Trigger spiral. The warehouses stay full but the tills drain to zero, velocity craters toward 0.1, and the board goes quiet — the economy strangles itself with goods on the shelf.

2. Hit Deploy a guard — the war-bond climbs and circulation reopens. Money starts moving down again, the tills refill, velocity recovers, contracts reappear. Same seed, no reset.

3. Through all of it the conservation light stays green. The total never changed — the money was always there. It had just stopped moving.

risks, namedWhere this design can rot

Macro legibility — "why is the board dead?"

A pooling failure is invisible from inside a single station. Goods are stocked, prices look normal, yet no contracts pay — and a player with no view of the whole money supply just sees a dead board and no reason for it. A macro failure mode that can't be read is indistinguishable from a bug, and players will (rightly) call it broken.

Mitigation the model shows: the failure is given instruments. A single money-velocity number is the vital sign; the active-vs-pooled time series shows the supply climbing into the treasury and going idle; the phase classifier labels the regime HEALTHY / SPIRAL outright; and the one-line why spells the chain ("front-pay throttled → tills drained to 0 in 18 ticks → velocity 0.1 → SPIRAL"). "Why is the board dead?" becomes a sentence the player can read.

Determinism — a money sim that can't be replayed

If the same seed and the same dials produced different runs, none of this would be trustable — a "spiral" could be a rounding artifact, and a replay-based bug report would be impossible. Floating-point credits drift across machines; an unordered account iteration reorders the postings; a stray Math.random in a fee desyncs two runs by tick 200.

Mitigation the model shows: every credit is an integer (fixed-point), every move goes through one double-entry post() in a fixed account order, and the only randomness is a single reseedable mulberry32 advanced exactly once per tick — used for small demand jitter, never for the money itself. Money is conserved by construction, so the conservation light is a continuous proof the replay is honest (APR.5).

This is the macro complement to solvency-cell's single till. The micro page shows one payer-gap; this one shows the money supply that funds every gap at once — and its one catastrophic failure, where a war economy starves with full warehouses because the blood stopped moving.
