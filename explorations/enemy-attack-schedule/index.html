Reading the Offensive

The enemy could strike a dozen places a dozen ways. It always strikes them in the same learnable order — so a veteran doesn't fight the war, she recites it. Until her own coverage choices start rewriting the script.

A first run is blind. A tenth run is a heist you've already cased — yet the take is never the same twice.

Minimalist Space Logistics gives you one ship and an unwinnable war to supply. The front is sovereign APR.2: you never fire a shot, you only haul goods into the shortage it creates. So the entire skill ceiling is knowing what the war will do next — and this page is about the part of the war you can actually learn.

The enemy here is not a rival trader. It is an attrition automaton M2.2 — it never bargains, never buys, never reacts to your prices. You cannot out-trade it. What it does have is an opening book: a scripted-yet-reactive sequence of attacks across a broad front, fired under conditions a careful player can memorise. Learn the book and the early war becomes navigable. That memorisation is the meta-game M2.5; the discoveries are logged in-world R.5, never handed to you on a menu.

the modelAn opening book, not a timeline

A flat timeline would be trivial — memorise four timestamps, done. The offensive is a small decision tree instead. Every operation fires from a gate built from three conditions, exactly the ones a veteran rehearses:

  • Time since run start — the op can't fire before tick t. The war has a tempo.
  • Time since the previous op — a minimum gap. The enemy can't sustain two blows back-to-back; cadence is its bottleneck.
  • A branch on the previous op's outcome — whether you held (covered it) or let it fall selects which op comes next. Same opening, two futures.

Formally each op fires when tick ≥ t AND tick − lastOp ≥ gap AND we are sitting on its node along the active branch. Four op types span the breadth of "many places, many ways":

  • MASS ASSAULT — overruns the next system in a segment. The only op that actually deletes map.
  • JUMPGATE DENIAL — cuts or reverses a lane, lengthening your haul.
  • DEPOT RAID — drains a forward depot's stock, spiking the very shortage you sell into.
  • INTERDICTION — raises a corridor's risk without taking ground.

And it is scripted-yet-reactive: holding a segment pushes its next op later (you bought tempo), abandoning one lurches the next strike early (the enemy exploits the opening). The opening is keyframed — learnable. Once the book is exhausted the front stops following a script and advances on pure accumulated pressure. Memorisation carries you through the opening; only skill carries you through the endgame.

what you just didTwo players, one memorised book, two wars

Run the same seed twice and the plan is identical: same first strike, same gates, same fork points. That is the heist you cased on run ten. But the moment you choose HOLD instead of LET FALL, the active branch flips and a different op queues next — a different target, a different gate. Two veterans who memorised the exact same opening still diverge the instant their coverage decisions differ. The script is shared; the playthrough is yours.

Watch the phase badge. While it reads SCRIPTED OPENING you are inside the book — every strike is a keyframe you could have known. When it flips to PURE-PRESSURE ENDGAME the keyframes are spent: the front now advances on accumulated pressure alone, and no amount of memorising helps. That is the deliberate seam between the two halves of mastery — recall gets you through the opening, play gets you through the close.

And notice the fog map: keyframes you haven't reached yet read as unobserved, not as noise. The schedule is fully determined from the seed APR.5 — you simply haven't seen it yet. A fresh player's ignorance is missing knowledge, never randomness; that is what makes the war learnable at all and what makes run-over-run mastery the actual game M2.5.

risks, namedWhere this design can rot

Memorisation > mastery. If the opening never changes, the skill collapses into rote recall: run ten plays itself, and the game stops asking anything of you.
Mitigations, both already in the model: a seed-family that shuffles which keyframes are pre-set versus emergent — the ROSE-#### field reseeds the book so the segment order, op types, and fork structure all move, defeating a single memorised line — and a script that runs out, handing the back half to the pure-pressure endgame where recall is worthless. Memorisation should be the price of admission to the real fight, not the fight.
Determinism rot. The whole promise is "same seed ⇒ same war." One stray Math.random, one float that rounds differently on another machine, one set iterated in hash order, and the cased heist desyncs — the player's memorised book stops matching the run and trust evaporates.
Mitigation: the sim is a fixed-timestep tick loop over a single reseedable mulberry32 PRNG (no bare Math.random anywhere), all gates are integer-tick comparisons, and iteration order over segments and ops is fixed and explicit APR.5. Determinism is a property you test, not a thing you hope for — replay the seed in CI and diff the op log.
