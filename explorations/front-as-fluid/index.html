The Tide Line

The front isn't a schedule and it isn't a market. It's a pressure field that flows across the graph — and your cargo is the only thing holding it back. Stop bailing, and it floods where you stopped.

A starved sector doesn't just fall. It ruptures, and cuts the lanes behind you — the ground gone from under your own supply line.

Minimalist Space Logistics gives you one hauler and an unwinnable war to keep fed across a graph of a dozen-odd systems. The front is sovereign APR.2: you never fire a shot, you only move goods into the shortage it makes. This page takes one reading of that front — call it the Tide — and makes it physical. Not an opening book, not a price curve. A fluid.

Concretely: enemy pressure is a scalar that diffuses across the lanes, seeping inward from the forward edge like water finding the low ground. Your supply is a second scalar that diffuses too — and where the two meet, supply consumes pressure. That's the whole fiction in two fields and one reaction: you bend the tide with cargo. The engine underneath is reaction-diffusion on a graph — a graph-Laplacian — the cheap, legible core the Tide reading is named for.

the modelTwo scalars, one Laplacian, a threshold

Lay the war out as ~14 systems in bands, rear to forward, wired by lanes. Each system holds two numbers: supply s and enemy pressure p. Every fixed tick, in a fixed order, in integer arithmetic, three things happen:

  • Diffusion. Both fields spread to their lane-neighbours by a discrete graph-Laplacian step — each node nudges toward the average of its neighbours. This is the heat equation on a graph, not particles bouncing around.
  • Reaction. The front injects fresh pressure at the forward edge each tick. Where supply and pressure coexist on a node, supply burns down pressure (and is spent doing it). Your cargo is the reaction term.
  • Rupture. Net local state is p − s. When a node's net crosses the threshold, it ruptures: it flips to overrun, its lanes are cut from the graph, and its pressure dumps into its surviving neighbours — which can tip them over. A cascade.
// diffusion, per node i, each tick — a graph-Laplacian step
si += D · Σj ∈ neighbours(i) ( sj − si )   // supply spreads
pi += D · Σj ∈ neighbours(i) ( pj − pi )   // pressure spreads
// reaction + rupture
pfrontier += intensity // front injects at the rim + every shore a rupture exposes
burn = min(si, pi · k) ;   pi −= burn ;   si −= burn
pi −= pi ≫ 6 // pressure dissipates inland — keeps the front a sharp line, not a fill
if ( pi − si > threshold ) rupture(i) // cut lanes, dump ¾ of its pressure to neighbours (≤ 1 hop / tick)

The coefficient D is a slider — turn it up and the field smears fast and even; turn it down and gradients stay sharp and local. Because every term is fixed-point i64 (we scale each scalar by a constant and only ever add, subtract, and floor-divide integers), the field is bit-for-bit deterministic: a run is a seed APR.5, and no float ever smears the same two runs apart.

how to drive the instrument

Press Play and watch pressure (rose, hot) seep inward from the forward edge while nodes cool toward cyan only where supply reaches them. Click any node to drop cargo there — you're the hauler — and watch the supply diffuse outward and push the tide back locally. The lanes show flux: which way, and how hard, each field is flowing. Mis-time it and a node ruptures, its lanes flash and cut, and the breach can cascade behind you. Flip stop supplying to take your hands off and watch the tide take the whole graph.

what you just didYou weren't fighting a clock — you were bailing a boat

The first thing the field teaches is that nothing here is scheduled. There's no list of attacks, no timestamps to memorise. There's a flow, and a leak. Pressure pours in at the forward rim every tick; left alone it dissipates inland, so the tide only advances where it can keep crossing the threshold — at the rim, and at every fresh shore a rupture exposes. Supply is the only thing holding that line, and the instant you flip stop supplying you can watch the front eat inward band by band — rim to home — until the whole graph has gone rose and ruptured.

The second thing it teaches is why a loss hurts. When a node ruptures, its lanes are cut from the Laplacian and its pressure dumps into the neighbours behind it. A sector you'd written off as "the front's problem" suddenly severs the path your own cargo was taking — and the pressure it was holding back floods inward, toward the rear you thought was safe. That's the line that matters: a starved sector ruptures and cuts the lanes behind you. You don't just lose ground; you lose the ground under your supply line, and the breach can cascade.

So the skill the Tide asks for isn't recall, it's triage under diffusion. Cargo dropped early, in the right node, spreads to cover several neighbours before they tighten — bend the field while it's still soft. Cargo dropped late, after a node's net has already crossed, is a bucket against a broken dam. Watch the nodes-at-risk readout: it's telling you where the gradient is about to win, one tick before the why line tells you it did.

Turn D up and the war becomes one smooth sheet — easy to read, hard to hold locally, because your cargo smears thin the instant it lands. Turn D down and the field clots into sharp pockets — your drops stay potent and local, but a single starved node can spike past the threshold before its neighbours feel a thing. The whole game lives in that tuning, and it's all the same three rules running on a fixed tick.

one fiction, two enginesThe Tide vs. the opening book

This is the same war, the same front, the same rose — read a different way. The sibling page, enemy-attack-schedule, makes the enemy a scripted-yet-reactive opening book: discrete keyframes you memorise, firing from gates, with the front advancing on accumulated pressure only once the script runs out. There the antagonist is a timeline you learn. Here it's a fluid you bend — no keyframes, no book, just a continuous field that floods wherever you stop bailing. Same fiction, a reaction-diffusion engine in place of an opening book; pick the reading that makes the front feel most alive at the table.

risks, namedWhere this design can rot

The field smears to mush. A continuous diffusion field's failure mode is illegibility: crank D and every node converges to the same lukewarm grey, the gradient flattens, and the player can't see where to drop cargo or why anything ruptured. A war you can't read is a war you can't play.
Mitigations, all in the model: keep the substrate discrete — a dozen-odd named nodes, never a pixel fluid, so there's always a countable "this system" to point at — and tune D into the band where gradients stay readable (the slider's default sits there; the extremes are shown precisely so the failure is visible, not hidden). Back it with a coarse, always-legible readout: nodes-at-risk and a single net-pressure tilt, so even mid-smear the player has a discrete signal of where the tide is winning. Legibility is a tuned property, not a hope.
Determinism rot. The promise is "a run is a seed." A continuous field is the easiest place to break it — one float that rounds differently on another machine, one Math.random in the jitter, one neighbour set iterated in hash order, and two runs of the same seed drift apart. For a field, drift compounds: a sub-pixel difference on tick 1 is a different rupture on tick 200.
Mitigation: the field is fixed-point i64 — every scalar is an integer scaled by a constant, and the Laplacian uses only add / subtract / floored integer divide, so there is no float in the field at all. Iteration over nodes and lanes is in fixed index order; the only randomness is a single reseedable mulberry32 used for cosmetic jitter, never for state. Determinism is a property you test — replay the seed in CI and diff the field hash, tick by tick.
