The Glass Cockpit

You never fire a shot. You read. And a war too big to walk across only becomes one person's to hold if the picture changes kind — not just scale — as the fleet under you grows.

Slide the fleet up far enough and the ships you were following stop being ships and become a current — and the strange part is that you still believe it.

Minimalist Space Logistics gives you one hauler and an unwinnable war to keep fed. You grow from that single hull into a combine of dozens R.1, across a front that is massive and abstract R.3 — a wall of attrition past the edge of the viewport. The twist of this reading — call it the Glass Cockpit — is that you have no guns. Reading is the verb: you observe, and you supply. So if you can't read the board, you have nothing to do. That makes the visual abstraction not a skin over the game. It is the game. Design the screen first; the rest is downstream of it.

The page you're about to drive is built around one rule, and it's the load-bearing one: a representation must change KIND, not just scale, as quantity grows. Zoom out on a normal map and a ship becomes a smaller ship; a thousand ships become a thousand tiny unreadable ships — glyph soup. That fails both halves of the brief at once. The fix is a LOD ladder of three tiers, and altitude is the only camera control M3.3: there is no free pan-and-zoom, only a discrete climb where each rung draws a deliberately different kind of thing.

  • CONTRACT — a single hauler is a discrete sprite: an arrowhead with a heading, towing a cargo pip colored and shaped by its good. You can follow one ship. M3.2
  • CORRIDOR — once the fleet is large, individual hulls dissolve into a flow ribbon on the lane. Width is throughput, color is the dominant good, opacity is reliability. You read logistics as a fluid, not a crowd. M3.2
  • THEATRE — systems collapse to a node graph, lanes to flux edges, the whole fleet to network brightness. The enemy is an advancing weather front — rose isobars, hatched, creeping inward. No single ship is visible, by design. M3.4

A hard line runs through all three: the enemy is weather, never a unit M3.4. You will never see a countable rose token to shoot at — only pressure, isobars, a displacement number. Drawing the front as ships would invite you to fight it, and you can't.

how to drive itThe one move that matters

The instrument has two coupled cameras-that-aren't-cameras. The fleet-size slider grows your hulls from 1 toward ~80. The altitude ladder climbs Contract → Corridor → Theatre. The thing to watch for: as fleet size crosses a threshold, the same lane visibly transitions sprites → ribbon → flux, with a brief morph so it reads as "the ships became a current," never "the ships vanished." When you don't believe it, hit Drill in — the ribbon re-explodes back into its individual hulls on demand. The abstraction has to be falsifiable, or it's a lie.

Then poke the rest: throughput (feed/starve) drives ribbon width & opacity and node brightness; front intensity drives the isobars creeping inward and lanes dimming and finally cutting. Read the instrument cluster on the right — three band gauges (rear / midfield / forward stock), two deficit sparklines, and one front-displacement number — and the one-line why under it.

the snapshot bus — why this is a VIEW, not the game

Everything you see is a one-way LOD render over deterministic tick snapshots. The simulation ticks in fixed order and emits an immutable snapshot; the renderer only reads it and chooses how to draw it at the current altitude. Changing tiers, drilling in, dragging fleet size — none of it ever writes back into the sim. Determinism holds APR.5: a run is a seed, and the picture is a lens you slide along it, never the source of truth.

the engine underneathWhy "KIND, not scale" is cheap and honest

The sim ticks a tiny deterministic world: a chain of systems in bands rear→forward, wired by lanes, with the front injecting pressure at the forward rim. Each tick emits a snapshot — per-lane throughput and reliability, per-band stock, the front's displacement. The renderer reads that one struct and picks a drawing strategy from the altitude and the fleet count. The transition between strategies is the whole trick:

// the LOD decision — a pure function of (altitude, fleetN), reading a snapshot
tier = ladder( altitude ) // 0 Contract · 1 Corridor · 2 Theatre
if tier == CONTRACT : draw each hull as an arrowhead + cargo pip
if tier == CORRIDOR : draw the lane as a ribbon  // w = throughput, α = reliability, hue = dominant good
if tier == THEATRE  : draw nodes (brightness = supply) + flux edges + isobars (the weather)
// morph m ∈ [0,1] cross-fades adjacent strategies so N sprites visibly CONDENSE into one ribbon

Because it's a one-way view, the morph is cosmetic — it never perturbs the snapshot. A reseedable mulberry32 drives only hauler jitter and the isobar grain; the stock/throughput/front numbers are plain arithmetic over the tick, so the same seed always draws the same war. Slide altitude with the fleet held still and only the kind of mark changes; the underlying quantities are identical. That's the claim made literal: same data, different kind of picture.

the ahaWhen the sprites become a current

Here is the moment the whole pitch lives or dies. Set altitude to Corridor and walk the fleet slider up. Around a dozen hulls, the arrowheads stop being things you track one-by-one and condense into a single flowing ribbon — and you don't experience it as loss. You experience it as resolution: the ribbon is those ships, its width is their throughput, its shimmer is them clearing the lane. One person can now read a flow that was a crowd a second ago. Don't trust it? Drill in, and the ribbon coughs the hulls back up. It told the truth.

risks, namedWhere this representation could lie to you

Dissolve reads as "they died."
Mitigation: never hard-swap. Cross-fade with a morph where sprites visibly travel into the ribbon's spine, and keep Drill in always live so the abstraction is falsifiable on demand. The ribbon must feel like a zoom, not a deletion.
Glyph soup at the Theatre tier.
Mitigation: hard cap on countable marks. Past Corridor, individual hulls are not allowed on screen — they roll up into node brightness and edge flux. Theatre answers "where is the whole machine winning or losing," not "how many ships are here."
Color-channel overload — twelve goods, not twelve hues.
Mitigation: never lean on hue alone. Each good carries a pip glyph (shape) as well as a color, and meaning rides shape + motion + color together — so the picture survives a colorblind reader and a dim panel. The ribbon's dominant good is its hue; the mix is in the pips when you drill in.
The enemy invites a fight.
Mitigation: the front is weather, never a unit M3.4. It is only ever isobars, a creep, a displacement number — nothing countable, nothing targetable. You can read where it's about to flood; you cannot shoot a cloud.
the bet, in one line

Change the kind of mark as quantity grows — sprite, ribbon, flux — and one calm screen can run a war too big to walk across. That single rule is the interface.

Designer's note — drag fleet size slowly with altitude on Corridor, then hit Drill in at ~50 hulls. If the ribbon doesn't feel like it contains those ships, the abstraction isn't honest yet and the whole reading is in question.
