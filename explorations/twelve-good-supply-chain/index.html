The Chain Has Depth

Twelve goods, six general and six military, each one rising from raw to intermediate to finished. The front only ever asks for the finished military goods — but those rest on three links of chain you never see on the contract board.

A shortage at the front is almost never where the problem is. Depth means you trace it upstream to the link that's actually choking.

Minimalist Space Logistics gives you one hauler and an unwinnable war to keep fed. The surface is deliberately thin: a handful of prices, a few contracts, a demand light at the front that blinks from green to red. That thinness is the pitch — you read a market, not a spreadsheet. But under the surface sits a production graph, and R.6 is its spine: twelve goods, a clean six general / six military split, and every good lives at one of three depths — raw → intermediate → finished.

That depth is the whole point of this page. The front is a sink: each tick it draws finished military goods and nothing else. A finished good is built from intermediates; intermediates are refined from raws; raws are mined at producers scattered across the map. So when the front light goes red on munitions, the cause might be the munitions fab — or the fuel feeding it, or the volatiles feeding the fuel, three links back. Logistics stops being "haul A to B" and becomes "find the one link that's starving the whole branch."

the modelA production DAG: twelve goods, three tiers, one flow

Lay the twelve goods out as a directed acyclic graph, left to right by depth. Raws (blue) emit a fixed amount each tick — they're the mines. Intermediates (violet) and finished goods (amber) are converters: each pulls its inputs from upstream at a fixed recipe ratio and pushes output downstream. The catch is the bound on how much it can push:

// one converter node i, each tick, in fixed order, integer quantities
want = capacityi   // what this stage could run if fully fed
canMake = min( capacityi,  ⌊ stock(in) / ratio(in) ⌋  for each input in )
// → a stage runs at the rate of its LIMITING input. one short input throttles all of it.
for each input: stock(input) −= canMake · ratio(input)
stock(i) += canMake   // output accrues; downstream pulls it next
// finally, the front draws finished military:   drawn = min( demand, stock(finmil) )

The limiting input rule is the engine. A converter is only as fast as its worst-fed input — so a single starved upstream good throttles everything below it, and the throttle propagates: the fab makes less, so the front gets less, so the light goes red. Cut a raw mine's capacity and you can watch a deficit travel three nodes to the right over a few ticks. Everything is integer and runs in a fixed node order APR.5 — given a seed, the run is bit-for-bit identical every time.

how to drive the instrument

Press Play. Edges thicken with live flow; a starved edge thins and reddens, and on every converter the limiting input is ringed in red. Click any node to bottleneck it (slam its capacity low) and watch the shortage ripple downstream to the front. Then click a finished-military good in the stock list, or its node, and hit Trace: the page walks the upstream path from the front shortage to the actual bottleneck and names it. Drop a relief batch of an intermediate — you're the hauler — to unstick a branch by hand. Tier sliders set capacity wholesale; the demand slider leans on the front.

what you just didThe shortage is a puzzle with an upstream cause

Bottleneck the volatiles mine and watch the front light go red on missiles. The naive read is "make more missiles" — but the missile fab is fine; it's starved. Trace it, and the page walks the path: missiles ← propellant-starved (the limiting input) ← volatiles mine at capacity. The fix isn't at the front. It's three links back, and the hauler's job is to carry the relief to the choke, not to the symptom. That's the difference depth makes: a flat economy would let you fix the shortage where you see it; a deep one turns every shortage into a small diagnosis.

This is also why the minimalist surface holds up. The player sees a price and a red light — a thin instrument. But because the model underneath has real depth, that thin reading is load-bearing: the red light is a true signal that some branch of a twelve-node graph is choking, and the interesting play is reasoning back to where. The surface stays small; the game stays deep. You don't need twelve gauges if one red light reliably points you at a chain worth untangling.

Notice the limiting-input rule do the work

Each converter runs at min(capacity, limiting input), so over-supplying one input does nothing if a different input is the bottleneck — pour all the alloy you like into the armor fab and plate output won't move if its composite feed is the short one. That's the rule that makes tracing meaningful: there is always exactly one binding constraint per starved branch, and finding it is the whole skill. Drop a relief batch on the right intermediate and the branch unsticks; drop it on the wrong one and you've wasted a haul.

risks, namedWhere this gets hard to keep honest

Risk — legibility. Twelve goods and a DAG is a lot to put on screen at once.
Mitigation: surface only the binding constraint, never the whole graph's numbers. The instrument keeps the layout fixed and readable, dims everything that isn't flowing, rings only the one limiting input per converter, and gives the player a single red light plus a Trace that names the cause. The discipline is the minimalist readout: the model can be deep as long as what you show is one signal and one path. If the player ever has to read all twelve stocks to act, the surface has failed.
Risk — determinism. Flow through a graph invites float drift and order-dependence.
Mitigation: fixed node order and integer quantities APR.5. Raws emit, then converters resolve in a fixed topological order, then the front draws — same sequence every tick. Every quantity is an integer and the limiting-input math is floor-division, so there's no float to smear two runs apart. A run is a seed; bottleneck the same mine on the same tick of the same seed and the cascade is identical. (The PRNG here only jitters mine yields and demand within fixed bounds — it never changes the order of resolution.)

The aha, stated plainly: keeping the war supplied is not running a single parcel from A to B — it's keeping a multi-stage chain fed. A front shortage is the last place to look for its own cause; depth means the answer is upstream, and the hauler who learns to trace beats the hauler who keeps re-running the same delivery into a fab that was never the problem.
