When the Script Runs Out

The opening is a heist you've already cased — every system falls on a beat you memorised. Then the keyframes run dry, and the front stops reciting and starts reacting. Recall carries you to the exhaustion point and not one tick further.

Memorisation gets you through the opening. Skill gets you through the endgame.

Minimalist Space Logistics gives you one hauler and an unwinnable war to keep supplied. In the "Known War" reading M2, the enemy isn't a rival trader — it's a schedule that eats the map. Its opening is scripted: a near-fixed order of which systems fall when, so a veteran reads the front like an opening book. A sibling page, enemy-attack-schedule, already builds that branching book — the Gantt and the decision tree of which operation fires under which condition. This page is about the seam the book hits: the moment the script runs out and live pressure takes over.

Here is the design tension in one line: a scripted opening risks collapsing the whole game into rote recall — memorisation > mastery. The fix is structural. The script has a length. Past it there are no more keyframes, and the front advances purely on the accumulated pressure you let build, segment by segment. The memorised map keeps drawing its old predictions; they stop coming true. The back half is always live.

the modelOne clock, two phases

The instrument runs a hauler's-eye view of a band of forward segments over a single deterministic clock APR.5. Fixed ticks, fixed order, one reseedable PRNG. Two phases share that clock:

  • OPENING — scripted. The first K system-falls are keyframes (target, scheduled_tick) expanded from the seed. Each keyframe is also gated by pressure, so your coverage perturbs its timing: feed a segment → push its fall later; abandon it → it lurches earlier. But the order is fixed and learnable. A memorised-map overlay predicts each fall — and in the opening, prediction matches. You've cased this.
  • ENDGAME — pure pressure. After the K keyframes exhaust, there are no more scripts. The front advances wherever accumulated pressure (the deficit you let build, per segment) is highest. The memorised map keeps drawing its old reference predictions, but they now diverge from what actually happens. Recall stops working.

The unknowns in the opening aren't random — they're unobserved keyframes. The schedule is fully determined from the seed; a fresh player simply hasn't seen it yet. That's what makes the opening learnable at all. After exhaustion it's not random either: it's emergent pressure, deterministic given your coverage — just not memorisable, because it depends on choices that differ every run.

how to read the instrument

Set coverage per segment (left of zero = starving it, right = feeding it), choose a script length K and a front intensity, then press Play (or scrub). The timeline runs left-to-right across an OPENING band and a ENDGAME band with the EXHAUSTION POINT marked. Watch the dashed memorised-map prediction ride right on top of the rose actual falls through the opening — then split after exhaustion. The recall-accuracy meter sits near 100% in the opening and decays once the script is spent. Flip on the second player to prove it: same memorised opening, different coverage, divergent endgame.

what you just sawThe opening matches; the endgame splits

Through the opening band, the dashed memorised map rides on top of the rose actual falls. Your coverage nudges each fall a few ticks early or late — the keyframe is pressure-gated — but the order is fixed, so the map still calls every target correctly. Recall accuracy sits at ~100%. This is the cased heist: on your tenth run you know what falls next before it does.

At the exhaustion point the keyframes run out. The map keeps drawing the predictions it learned from a reference run — keyframes that, past K, were never real to begin with — but the front has stopped following any script. It now falls wherever your accumulated pressure is highest, and that depends on coverage choices the reference run never made. The dashed line and the rose falls split. The recall meter decays. The driver readout flips from keyframe n/K to pure pressure: VELA highest deficit. No amount of memorising closes that gap — the only thing that helps now is having fed the right segments on the way in.

Turn on the second player. The ghost shares your exact memorised opening — identical seed, identical reference predictions, so through the opening the two of you are indistinguishable. But the ghost covered the band differently in the lead-up, so it arrives at exhaustion with a different pressure profile. From that tick on, the two runs diverge. That divergence is the proof: the back half was never on rails. The same seed's opening is a cased heist; its endgame is always live.

the aha

Recall is a finite resource with a known expiry. It pays out perfectly right up to the exhaustion point — and is worth exactly nothing one tick past it. The scripted opening isn't the game; it's the price of admission to the part that is.

risks, namedWhere this design can rot

Memorisation > mastery. A scripted opening invites pure rote: if the whole front is a fixed recital, run ten plays itself and the game stops asking anything of you.
Mitigation — this page is that mitigation, made visible. The script has a length. Past the K keyframes the front advances on accumulated pressure with no more keyframes to recite, so the back half is always skill. Drag K down and watch the recallable opening shrink and the live endgame swell; the seam between recall and play is a tuning knob, not an accident. Memorisation is the cost of entry to the real fight, not the fight.
Determinism. The promise is "a run is a seed" APR.5. One stray Math.random, one float that rounds differently elsewhere, one set walked in hash order, and the memorised opening stops matching the run — the cased heist desyncs and trust evaporates.
Mitigation. The opening is seed-stable: same seed ⇒ same keyframes, same order, same scheduled ticks. The endgame is deterministic given coverage — fixed-timestep ticks over one reseedable mulberry32, integer/fixed-point pressure thresholds, segments walked in fixed explicit order — just not memorisable, because it's a function of choices that change run to run. Determinism is a property you replay-test in CI, not one you hope holds; "not memorisable" and "not deterministic" are deliberately different things.
